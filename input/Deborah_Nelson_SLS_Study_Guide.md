## 🔑 The Big Picture — Standard Newborn Care

```
Healthy newborn delivered after uneventful labor
        ↓
IMMEDIATE (0–10 min):
  Dry, warm, stimulate → Assess APGAR → Skin-to-skin → Delayed cord clamping
        ↓
EARLY CARE (First Hour — "Golden Hour"):
  Skin-to-skin with mother → Initiate breastfeeding → ID bands → Vitamin K → Eye ointment
        ↓
ONGOING ASSESSMENT:
  Head-to-toe assessment → Vital signs → Weight/measurements → Glucose if at risk
  → Hepatitis B vaccine → Teaching → Documentation
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the components of the APGAR score?

| Parameter | 0 | 1 | 2 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A**ppearance (color) | Blue/pale all over | Blue extremities, pink body | Pink all over |
| **P**ulse (HR) | Absent | < 100 bpm | ≥ 100 bpm |
| **G**rimace (reflex irritability) | No response | Grimace/weak cry | Strong cry/sneeze |
| **A**ctivity (muscle tone) | Limp | Some flexion | Active motion |
| **R**espiration | Absent | Weak, irregular, gasping | Strong, regular cry |

- **Score 7–10:** Normal (most healthy newborns score 8–9)
- **Score 4–6:** Moderate difficulty; requires stimulation and possible O2
- **Score 0–3:** Severe; requires full resuscitation

Assessed at **1 minute and 5 minutes** of life. If score < 7 at 5 minutes, assess every 5 minutes until ≥ 7 or 20 minutes.

### Q2. What is the standard newborn head-to-toe assessment?

**Vital Signs (Normal Ranges):**
- Heart rate: 120–160 bpm (may drop to 100 sleeping; up to 180 crying — both normal)
- Respiratory rate: 30–60 breaths/min; irregular rhythm is normal
- Temperature: 36.5–37.5°C (axillary)
- Blood pressure: systolic 65–95 mmHg (not routinely assessed unless concern)

**Anthropometric Measurements:**
- Weight: 2,500–4,000 grams (5.5–8.8 lbs) — normal term
- Length: 48–53 cm (19–21 inches)
- Head circumference: 33–35 cm (larger than chest)
- Chest circumference: 30–33 cm

**Head-to-Toe Systematic Assessment:**

*Head:* Shape (molding from delivery — resolves in days); fontanelles (anterior — diamond-shaped, soft; posterior — triangular, may be closed); caput succedaneum (scalp edema — crosses suture lines) vs. cephalohematoma (blood between periosteum and skull — does NOT cross suture lines)

*Eyes:* Red reflex (white/absent = cataract, retinoblastoma); subconjunctival hemorrhages (common — from pressure during delivery; resolve in weeks)

*Ears:* Low-set ears (associated with chromosomal anomalies); hearing screening before discharge

*Nose:* Neonates are obligate nose breathers — any nasal obstruction is serious

*Mouth:* Palate intact (assess for cleft palate); sucking reflex; Epstein's pearls (white cysts on palate — normal)

*Neck:* Clavicles intact (assess for fracture from shoulder dystocia)

*Chest:* Symmetric chest rise; breath sounds clear; breast engorgement normal in both sexes (maternal hormone effect); nipple discharge possible (witch's milk)

*Abdomen:* Soft; bowel sounds; umbilical cord: 2 arteries + 1 vein (single umbilical artery associated with renal anomalies); assess for hernias

*Genitalia:* Females: labia majora cover minora at term; vaginal discharge (pseudomenstruation from maternal estrogen — normal). Males: testes descended; circumcision status

*Extremities:* Full range of motion; equal Moro reflex bilaterally; palmar creases (single palmar crease — simian crease — associated with Down syndrome)

*Spine:* Midline; no sacral dimple with tuft of hair (spina bifida)

*Hips:* Barlow (hip adduction to detect dislocation) and Ortolani (hip abduction and elevation to reduce dislocated hip) maneuvers — performed by provider

*Neurological:* Primitive reflexes: rooting (touch cheek → turns toward stimulus), sucking, Moro (startle), palmar grasp, plantar grasp, stepping, Babinski (normal in newborns — toes fan up)

### Q3. What are the routine medications and treatments given to all newborns?

**Vitamin K (Phytonadione) 0.5–1 mg IM:**
- Newborns have immature clotting factors and are deficient in vitamin K at birth
- Prevents **hemorrhagic disease of the newborn** (HDN) — serious bleeding including intracranial hemorrhage
- Given within 1 hour of birth
- Site: Vastus lateralis (anterolateral thigh)

**Erythromycin Ophthalmic Ointment 0.5%:**
- Prevents **ophthalmia neonatorum** — eye infection from maternal gonorrhea or chlamydia
- Applied to lower conjunctival sac of each eye (1 cm ribbon)
- Within 1–2 hours of birth; may temporarily blur newborn's vision

**Hepatitis B Vaccine 0.5 mL IM:**
- First dose of three-dose series
- Given within 12–24 hours of birth (sooner if mother HBsAg positive)
- Site: Vastus lateralis

### Q4. What parent teaching is required before discharge?

**Feeding:**
- Breastfeeding: colostrum first (immunoglobulins, low volume — normal); breast milk typically comes in 3–5 days; 8–12 feedings per 24 hours; wet diapers (6+ per day after milk comes in) as indicator of adequate intake
- Formula feeding: correct preparation; sterilization; feeding on demand

**Cord Care:**
- Keep dry; fold diaper below cord; do NOT submerge in tub until cord falls off (1–3 weeks)
- Signs of infection: redness, odor, drainage

**Safety:**
- **Back to Sleep** — supine position for every sleep; firm flat surface; no bed-sharing
- **Car seat** — rear-facing; installed and inspected before discharge
- No smoking in environment

**Signs of Illness — When to Call Provider:**
- Jaundice (yellow skin or eyes) spreading to abdomen or extremities
- Fever ≥ 100.4°F (38°C) rectal
- No wet diaper for > 8 hours after day 3
- Not waking to feed
- Breathing difficulty

**Circumcision care** (if applicable): Keep clean; petroleum gauze first 24 hours; signs of infection

**Newborn screening:** State-mandated metabolic screen (PKU, hypothyroidism, etc.) — blood draw at 24–48 hours; may need repeat at 2-week visit

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do

### Immediate After Delivery (Routine Care)
1. **Dry and stimulate** — vigorous drying; remove wet blanket
2. **Skin-to-skin** — place directly on mother's chest (if stable and no resuscitation needed)
3. **Delayed cord clamping** — wait 1–3 minutes if possible before clamping
4. **APGAR scores** — 1 minute and 5 minutes
5. **ID bands** — place on newborn (ankle) and mother and support person before any separation
6. **Initial assessment** — APGAR elements and vital signs
7. **Initiate breastfeeding** — within first hour (golden hour)
8. **Administer Vitamin K** and eye ointment within first hour

### Teaching Priorities for Deborah
- Breastfeeding initiation and support
- Normal newborn appearance (molding, vernix, milia, erythema toxicum)
- Safe sleep (Back to Sleep, no bed-sharing)
- Car seat safety
- Cord care
- When to seek emergency care
- Newborn screening tests

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. What is the purpose of the APGAR score?
Standardized assessment of newborn adaptation to extrauterine life at 1 and 5 minutes — guides need for intervention.

### Q2. Why is Vitamin K given to all newborns?
Newborns are born with low levels of Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X). Without supplementation, they are at risk for hemorrhagic disease of the newborn — serious, potentially fatal bleeding.

### Q3. What is "Back to Sleep"?
All infants should be placed on their **back** for every sleep until 1 year of age to reduce the risk of **sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)**.

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES (Newborn)

1. **Risk for Hypothermia** r/t transition to extrauterine environment — AEB: wet newborn in room temperature; immature thermoregulation
2. **Risk for Infection** r/t immature immune system and open cord site
3. **Readiness for Enhanced Breastfeeding** r/t positive maternal attitude and available support
4. **Deficient Knowledge (Parent)** r/t newborn care, feeding, and safety — AEB: first newborn at home; many questions
