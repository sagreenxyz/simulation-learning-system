## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four interlocking themes**:

1. **Medication error recognition** — the dialysis nurse gave ampicillin to a patient with a penicillin allergy; the student must recognize the anaphylactic reaction and understand it was caused by a medication error
2. **Anaphylaxis emergency management** — a rapid, systematic, life-threatening allergic response requiring immediate intervention; epinephrine is the FIRST medication
3. **Complex patient with multiple conditions** — T1DM + ESRD + hemodialysis creates a complex patient requiring specialized care considerations
4. **LGBTQ+ inclusive care** — Cynthia's preferred pronouns must be respected throughout; the scenario explicitly addresses equitable, person-centered care

The anaphylaxis cascade:

```
Documented penicillin allergy NOT checked before ampicillin administration
        ↓
IV ampicillin infused → Antibodies (IgE) bind to ampicillin
        ↓
Mast cells and basophils degranulate → Release histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes
        ↓
SYSTEMIC response within minutes:
  • Vasodilation → Hypotension
  • Increased vascular permeability → Third-spacing → Hypovolemic shock
  • Bronchoconstriction → Wheezing, dyspnea
  • Urticaria, pruritus, flushing
  • Angioedema → Airway compromise
        ↓
EMERGENCY: Stop ampicillin → Call for help → Epinephrine IM → O2 → IV fluid
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. List signs and symptoms from each body system during anaphylaxis.

**Respiratory:**
- Nasal congestion, rhinorrhea
- Cough
- Hoarseness, stridor (airway edema)
- **Wheezing** (bronchoconstriction)
- Dyspnea, shortness of breath
- Tachypnea
- **Laryngeal edema** → life-threatening airway obstruction
- Cyanosis (late)
- Apnea (severe/late)

**Cardiovascular:**
- **Hypotension** — hallmark; vasodilation + third-spacing
- Tachycardia (compensatory)
- Rapid, weak, thready pulse
- Chest pain/tightness
- Dysrhythmias
- Cardiac arrest (severe)

**Gastrointestinal:**
- Nausea, vomiting
- Crampy abdominal pain
- Diarrhea
- Bloating, abdominal distension

**Dermatological:**
- **Generalized pruritus (itching)** — early, common first symptom
- **Urticaria (hives)** — raised, red, itchy welts
- **Erythema** (diffuse redness)
- Flushing (especially face, neck, chest)
- **Angioedema** — swelling of lips, tongue, throat, eyes → airway emergency
- Pallor or cyanosis (late)

  - Document family communication in the medical record

### Q3. Explain the collaborative role of the nurse and priority nursing interventions for a patient undergoing hemodialysis.

**Nursing Role:**
- Collaborate with the nephrologist, dialysis technician/nurse, and dietitian
- Assess vascular access (fistula, graft, or central venous catheter) before and after each session

**Priority Nursing Interventions for Hemodialysis:**
1. **Assess vascular access site:**
   - Palpate for thrill (vibration) — presence = fistula/graft is patent
   - Auscultate for bruit (swooshing sound) — presence = patent
   - Assess for warmth, redness, swelling (infection), pain, numbness, or tingling
   - **Never take BP, draw blood, or start IV in the access arm**
2. **Monitor vital signs** — every 30–60 minutes during treatment; BP, HR, temp, SpO2
3. **Fluid balance** — weigh patient before and after; assess I&O; patient may be fluid-restricted
4. **Post-dialysis monitoring:**
   - **Reduced blood pressure** + **elevated heart rate** (tachycardia) — signs of hypovolemia from fluid removal
   - Nausea, vomiting, dizziness
   - Blood glucose changes (T1DM — insulin may need adjustment)
5. **Lab monitoring** — BUN, creatinine, potassium, phosphorus, calcium, albumin
6. **Medication timing** — many medications must NOT be given right before dialysis (they will be dialyzed out); consult pharmacist
7. **Patient education** — dietary restrictions (low potassium, low phosphorus, low sodium, fluid restriction); vascular access care; signs of infection

### Q5. What are the initial and ongoing nursing interventions for managing anaphylaxis?

**IMMEDIATE — Do ALL of these simultaneously (call for help):**

1. **STOP the causative agent** — STOP the ampicillin infusion immediately
   - Do NOT disconnect the IV — maintain IV access for emergency medications
   - Change IV tubing to new saline line (avoid any residual ampicillin entering patient)

2. **CALL FOR HELP** — activate rapid response/code team

3. **Assess airway, breathing, circulation (ABCs)** — priority assessment

4. **Administer EPINEPHRINE (1:1000) IM** — FIRST-LINE DRUG
   - 0.3–0.5 mg IM into the **lateral thigh** (vastus lateralis muscle)
   - Can repeat every 5–15 minutes if no improvement
   - Mechanism: vasoconstriction, bronchodilation, inhibits further mediator release

5. **Oxygen** — apply high-flow O2 via non-rebreather mask (10–15 LPM)

6. **Position** — lay patient flat, elevate legs (Trendelenburg) if hypotensive
   - If dyspneic, elevate HOB to aid breathing — balance with BP

7. **IV fluid bolus** — 0.9% NS large-bore IV; 1–2 liters to treat hypotension

**ONGOING — After Epinephrine:**
8. **Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)** IV — blocks H1 receptors → reduces urticaria, itching
9. **Corticosteroid** (methylprednisolone IV) — prevents late biphasic reaction; takes hours to work
10. **Albuterol nebulizer** — reverses bronchoconstriction/wheezing
11. **Ranitidine (H2 blocker)** — optional, blocks H2 receptors
12. **Continuous monitoring** — cardiac monitor, SpO2, BP q5 min, level of consciousness
13. **Incident report** — medication error that caused this event must be documented
14. **Prepare for intubation** if laryngeal edema worsens

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Immediate Recognition of Anaphylaxis
When Cynthia returns from hemodialysis with the ampicillin infusing and develops pruritus/urticaria/wheezing:

**Step 1:** STOP the ampicillin infusion immediately
**Step 2:** Do NOT disconnect the IV (need access for medications)
**Step 3:** Change to new saline line with new tubing
**Step 4:** Call for help ("I need help in Room X — anaphylaxis")
**Step 5:** Assess: airway (stridor?), breathing (wheezing, RR), circulation (BP, HR)
**Step 6:** Administer epinephrine 0.3 mg IM into lateral thigh
**Step 7:** Apply NRB O2 mask
**Step 8:** Initiate IV fluid bolus — 0.9% NS

### Checking Allergies Before Medications — PREVENT THIS EVENT
**Before ANY medication:**
1. Check the patient's allergy record in the EHR
2. Ampicillin → penicillin class → **ALLERGY** → do NOT administer
3. Contact the provider to report the allergy and request an alternative antibiotic (e.g., ciprofloxacin for UTI — but note: caution in ESRD, dose adjustment needed)
4. If allergy was missed by dialysis nurse — this is a medication error requiring disclosure and reporting

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up
- **W** — Wristband — verify identity; CHECK ALLERGY BAND
- **I** — IV: ampicillin running → STOP; maintain access with saline
- **P** — Pull cord/call light; call for help
- **E** — Emergency equipment: epinephrine available? Crash cart nearby?

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. After IV ampicillin starts and Cynthia complains of pruritus with erythema and wheezing, the nurse's first response is:
**C.) Perform a focused assessment, STOP the ampicillin infusion, and notify the physician.**

The IV should NOT be discontinued (access needed for emergency medications). Slowing or continuing the ampicillin is inappropriate — it is the allergen. The correct action: assess, stop the drug, maintain IV access, call for help, and notify the provider for further orders.

---

### Q2. Common symptoms during the EARLY stage of anaphylaxis include:
**D.) Generalized itching (pruritus)**

Dyspnea, difficulty swallowing, and hoarseness occur in LATER phases as airway edema progresses. Generalized itching is the most common EARLY symptom — the first sign the patient notices.

---

### Q3. Priority nursing intervention for anaphylaxis is:
**C.) Respiratory and airway patency assessment**

Anaphylaxis can rapidly progress to respiratory arrest from laryngeal edema. Airway is always the first priority (ABC). Cardiac assessment and vital signs are ongoing but airway takes precedence.

---

### Q4. Medication of choice for initial treatment of anaphylaxis:
**C.) Epinephrine**

Epinephrine 1:1000, 0.3–0.5 mg IM into the lateral thigh is the FIRST and most critical drug. It reverses vasodilation (vasoconstriction), opens airways (bronchodilation), and prevents further mediator release. Metaproterenol (bronchodilator only) and furosemide (diuretic) are not appropriate first-line treatments.

---

### Q5. During anaphylaxis, Cynthia's vital signs reflect:
**D.) A hypotensive state**

The initial anaphylactic response is vasodilation + increased capillary permeability → decreased systemic vascular resistance → hypotension. This is NOT hypertension. Tachycardia (not bradycardia) is also expected.

---

### Q6. Cynthia's SpO2 is dropping during anaphylaxis. Which action improves oxygenation?
**C.) Applying oxygen via non-rebreather mask**

A non-rebreather mask delivers 60–90% FiO2 — the highest flow available for non-intubated patients. Deep breathing and incentive spirometry are not effective during active bronchoconstriction. Leg elevation (Trendelenburg) helps blood pressure but does not directly improve oxygenation.

---

### Q7. Hypersensitivity type of anaphylaxis:
**Type I (Immediate Hypersensitivity)**

- IgE-mediated: antigen binds to IgE on mast cells/basophils → rapid degranulation
- Occurs within minutes of exposure
- Type II = cytotoxic (blood transfusion reactions)
- Type III = immune complex–mediated (serum sickness)
- Type IV = delayed (contact dermatitis) — takes 48–72 hours

---

### Q8. After hemodialysis, nurse must closely monitor for:
**C.) Reduced blood pressure, elevated heart rate**

Fluid removal during hemodialysis can cause hypovolemia — the most common complication. Signs: hypotension (reduced BP), tachycardia (elevated HR), nausea, vomiting, dizziness. Elevated temperature would suggest infection. A rising HR with falling BP is the key post-dialysis vital sign pattern to monitor.

---

### Q9. Chronic complications of diabetes mellitus:
**D.) Kidney disease** (ESRD — as in Cynthia)

Chronic hyperglycemia damages blood vessels → chronic kidney disease → ESRD. Other chronic complications include: retinopathy, neuropathy, peripheral vascular disease, coronary artery disease. Hyperglycemia, polyuria, and hypoglycemic reactions are ACUTE findings, not chronic complications.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Epinephrine 1:1000 (Adrenalin) — IM — FIRST-LINE DRUG
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Endogenous catecholamine / Sympathomimetic |
| **Mechanism** | Alpha-1: vasoconstriction (reverses vasodilation/hypotension); Beta-1: increases HR and contractility; Beta-2: bronchodilation (reverses bronchoconstriction) |
| **Indication** | FIRST-LINE treatment of anaphylaxis |
| **Dose** | 0.3–0.5 mg (0.3–0.5 mL of 1:1000 concentration) IM into lateral thigh |
| **Route** | IM — lateral thigh (vastus lateralis) — fastest absorption |
| **Can repeat** | Every 5–15 minutes if no improvement |
| **Adverse effects** | Tachycardia, palpitations, hypertension, anxiety, tremor, headache |
| **Nursing precautions** | Use auto-injector correctly; IM preferred over SQ for faster absorption; have second dose ready |

---

### Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) — IV
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | H1-antihistamine / Anticholinergic |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks H1 histamine receptors → reduces urticaria, pruritus, angioedema |
| **Indication** | Second-line in anaphylaxis — symptom management (NOT life-saving like epinephrine) |
| **Dose** | 25–50 mg IV |
| **Adverse effects** | Sedation, dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision |
| **Note** | Antihistamines do NOT reverse the life-threatening cardiovascular collapse of anaphylaxis — only epinephrine does |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Impaired Gas Exchange** r/t bronchoconstriction and laryngeal edema secondary to anaphylactic reaction
   - AEB: wheezing, SpO2 dropping, dyspnea

2. **Decreased Cardiac Output** r/t severe vasodilation and decreased preload secondary to anaphylaxis
   - AEB: hypotension, tachycardia, rapid weak pulse

3. **Risk for Anaphylactic Shock** r/t allergic reaction to ampicillin (penicillin class)
   - AEB: documented penicillin allergy, exposure to ampicillin, anaphylactic symptoms developing

4. **Risk for Unstable Blood Glucose** r/t T1DM and physiological stress of anaphylaxis
   - AEB: T1DM requiring insulin; epinephrine administration increases blood glucose

5. **Risk for Medication Error (System issue)** — ampicillin given despite documented penicillin allergy
   - AEB: allergy not checked before administration; medication error requiring incident report
