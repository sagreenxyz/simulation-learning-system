## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. How do you assess for fall risk and pressure ulcer risk?

**Fall Risk Assessment — Morse Fall Scale:**
Assess the following factors:
- **Age** — age ≥ 65 increases risk
- **Fall history** — previous fall is the SINGLE GREATEST risk factor
- **Secondary diagnosis** — multiple diagnoses increase risk
- **Ambulatory aid** — use of walker, cane, crutches, or furniture for support
- **IV/heparin lock** — presence of IV access
- **Gait** — weak, impaired, or unsteady gait
- **Mental status** — confusion, disorientation, impaired judgment
- **Medications** — antihypertensives, diuretics, sedatives, opioids, antidepressants
- **Elimination habits** — incontinence/urgency that causes rushing to the bathroom

**Pressure Ulcer Risk Assessment — Braden Scale:**
Assess the following six subscales (score 1–4 each; total 6–23; score ≤18 = at-risk):
1. **Sensory perception** — ability to respond to pressure-related discomfort
2. **Moisture** — degree to which skin is exposed to moisture (incontinence, perspiration)
3. **Activity** — degree of physical activity (bedbound vs. ambulatory)
4. **Mobility** — ability to change and control body position
5. **Nutrition** — usual food intake pattern
6. **Friction and shear** — movement against surfaces; requires assistance to move

> **For Lisa Rae:** She scores poorly on moisture (urinary incontinence), mobility (weak, needs assistance), activity (limited), and sensory perception (disoriented). She is at HIGH risk for pressure ulcers.

### Q2. What can be done to reduce the risk of a patient falling?

**Environmental Safety Measures:**
- Keep bed in **lowest position** at all times
- Ensure **call light is within reach** and patient knows how to use it
- Keep **one side rail up, one down** as a support point (raising all rails is a restraint)
- Remove **clutter** from bedside tables, hallways, and bathroom
- Ensure patient has **non-skid footwear** (slippers or socks with grips)
- Use **bath mats or non-skid strips** in shower/bathtub
- Place **disoriented patients in rooms near the nurses' station**
- Ensure **adequate lighting**, especially at night (nightlight)

**Patient-Centered Interventions:**
- Complete and document **Morse Fall Scale**
- Apply **fall risk armband/bracelet** and post fall risk sign at bedside
- Offer **scheduled toileting** every 2 hours to prevent rushing
- **Assist with ambulation** — do not leave patient alone while up
- Use a **gait belt** when ambulating
- Consult **physical therapy (PT) and occupational therapy (OT)** for evaluation
- Educate patient to **call before getting up** — do not get up alone
- Minimize **medications that increase fall risk** (sedatives, opioids, antihypertensives)
- **Reorient** confused patients frequently

**Monitoring:**
- Conduct **hourly rounding**
- Use a **bed/chair exit alarm** if ordered
- Reassess fall risk with every significant change in condition

### Q3. What are the stages of pressure ulcers? List and describe each.

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1** | Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin. Skin is intact but reddened; does not blanch (turn white) when pressed. May feel warmer or cooler than surrounding skin. Earliest stage. |
| **Stage 2** | Partial-thickness skin loss with exposed dermis. Wound bed is viable, pink or red. Ruptured serum-filled blisters may be present. Shallow, open ulcer. No slough or bruising. |
| **Stage 3** | Full-thickness skin loss. Adipose (fatty) tissue is visible. Slough and eschar may be visible but do not obscure the depth. Depth varies by location. |
| **Stage 4** | Full-thickness skin and tissue loss. Palpable fascia, muscle, tendon, ligament, cartilage, or bone may be exposed or directly palpable. Slough and eschar often present. |
| **Deep Tissue Pressure Injury (DTPI)** | Persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration, or epidermal separation revealing a dark wound bed or blood-filled blister. |
| **Unstageable** | Full-thickness skin and tissue loss in which the extent of tissue damage within the ulcer cannot be confirmed because it is obscured by slough or eschar. Must be debrided to determine stage. |

> **Key point:** Stage 3 and Stage 4 cannot heal on their own and may require surgical intervention. Always document location, size (length x width x depth), wound bed appearance, drainage, and periwound skin.

### Q4. What are risk factors for pressure ulcers?

- **Immobility** — inability to reposition; most critical risk factor
- **Moisture/incontinence** — exposure to urine or stool softens and weakens skin (Lisa Rae has urinary incontinence)
- **Malnutrition** — inadequate protein, vitamins C and A, and zinc impair wound healing; low hemoglobin reduces oxygen delivery
- **Advanced age** — skin becomes thinner, drier, less elastic, and more fragile
- **Impaired sensory perception** — inability to feel pressure and respond appropriately
- **Friction and shear** — movement across surfaces (repositioning without proper technique)
- **Reduced circulation** — hypotension, peripheral vascular disease, or prolonged pressure reduce blood flow
- **Cognitive impairment** — confused patients cannot communicate discomfort or reposition themselves
- **History of pressure ulcers** — previous breakdown weakens tissue
- **Diabetes** — impairs circulation and healing
- **Osteoporosis/prominent bony prominences** — sacrum, heels, hips, elbows are highest-risk areas

**Highest risk bony prominences:** Sacrum, heels, hips, ankles, elbows, and back of head

### Q5. What are nursing interventions for a patient with a pressure ulcer?

**Prevention and Skin Integrity:**
1. **Reposition every 2 hours** (or more frequently based on Braden score) — this is the single most important intervention
2. Elevate head of bed **no more than 30 degrees** to prevent shearing
3. Use a **pressure-redistributing mattress or overlay** (foam, air, or gel)
4. Keep skin **clean and dry** — cleanse promptly after incontinence
5. Apply **skin barrier cream/ointment** to protect from moisture
6. Perform **head-to-toe skin assessment** every shift
7. Consult a **wound care/ostomy nurse** for staging and treatment

**Wound Care (if ulcer present):**
- Cleanse wound with appropriate solution (normal saline or wound cleanser)
- Apply appropriate dressing based on stage (hydrocolloid, foam, alginate)
- Document: size (LxWxD), stage, wound bed color, drainage type/amount, periwound skin
- Monitor for signs of infection: increased redness, warmth, purulent drainage, odor

**Nutrition:**
- Ensure adequate **protein, vitamin C, vitamin A, and zinc** intake
- Monitor **albumin and hemoglobin** — low levels impair healing (hemoglobin must be adequate to carry oxygen to tissues for healing)
- Consult **dietitian** if nutritional needs are not being met

**Education:**
- Teach patient and family about **repositioning schedule**
- Instruct on **skin inspection** (especially heels and sacrum)
- Educate on **nutrition** for wound healing

### Q1. Which student interventions are NOT appropriate for a confused patient trying to get out of bed? (Select all that apply.)

**Every 2 hours** — to allow for hydration, toileting, repositioning, and range of motion exercises

### Q3. Which factor places a patient at the HIGHEST risk for a fall?
**A previous fall** — a history of falls is the single greatest predictor of future falls. Confusion, age >65, and impaired judgment are also risk factors but do not carry as much predictive weight.

---

### Q4. Which nursing diagnosis is of highest importance?
**Risk for Injury** — patient safety is always the highest priority among nursing diagnoses.

---

### Q5. What results in the greatest number of injuries to older adults?
**Falls** — falls, automobile accidents, and fires are the leading causes of injuries in older adults. Falls are by far the most common cause of injury in the hospital and in the community for the elderly population.

---

### Q6. Which medication type is most associated with increased fall risk?
**Antidepressants** — also included are antihypotensive agents, diuretics, antihypertensive agents, sedatives, and hypnotic agents. Lisa Rae is on hydromorphone (opioid/sedative) and hydrochlorothiazide (diuretic + antihypertensive), both of which increase her fall risk.

---

### Q7. A nurse notes a patient has a pressure ulcer. Impaired skin integrity is classified as which type of nursing diagnosis?
**Actual nursing diagnosis** — because the skin breakdown has already occurred. A *risk* nursing diagnosis applies when a condition could potentially develop; an *actual* diagnosis applies when the problem already exists.

---

### Q8. What hemoglobin level is needed to promote wound healing?
A hemoglobin level **above 12 g/dL** is generally needed to ensure adequate oxygen delivery to tissues for wound healing. Low hemoglobin (anemia) means less oxygen-carrying capacity → impaired healing.

### Q5. Lisa Rae was found hypotensive in the ED. Which time period is associated with the lowest blood pressure for most individuals?
**A.) 0000 to 0300 (midnight to 3:00 AM)**

Blood pressure follows a circadian rhythm (diurnal variation):
- **Lowest:** 0000–0300 (midnight to 3 AM) — body at rest, lowest metabolic demand
- **Rising:** 0300–0600 — gradual rise before waking
- **Highest:** 1000–1800 — peak activity hours
This is clinically relevant when scheduling antihypertensive medications and timing assessments.

---

### Q6. The initial sounds heard when taking a patient's blood pressure are known as:
**B.) Korotkoff sounds**

- **Korotkoff sounds** are the sounds heard through a stethoscope during blood pressure measurement as the cuff is deflated
- The **first Korotkoff sound** (tapping sound) = **systolic blood pressure**
- The **disappearance of sound** = **diastolic blood pressure**
- Other terms: Kussmaul sounds = breathing pattern in metabolic acidosis; Cheyne-Stokes = irregular breathing pattern

---

### Q7. When preparing to assess a patient's blood pressure, the nurse should first:
**C.) Attempt to control factors that may impact the blood pressure reading.**

Before taking any BP measurement, the nurse should:
- Ensure the patient has rested for at least 5 minutes
- Remove or avoid tight clothing on the arm
- Position arm at heart level
- Avoid pain, anxiety, or recent activity that could falsely elevate readings
- Use the correct cuff size for the patient's arm
- Avoid taking BP in an arm with IV access if possible

---

### Q8. What is the purpose of the Braden Scale?
**A.) The Braden Scale is a means to assess a patient's risk for skin breakdown (pressure ulcer risk).**

It is NOT used for falls (that is the Morse Fall Scale), psychosocial factors, or incontinence evaluation specifically. The Braden Scale has six subscales scored 1–4 (except friction/shear, scored 1–3). Total scores range 6–23. Score ≤18 = at risk; score ≤12 = high risk.

---

### Q9. Lisa Rae has osteoporosis. Which menu selection promotes bone health?
**A.) Spinach**

Osteoporosis is loss of bone density requiring increased **calcium and vitamin D**:
- **Spinach** and other leafy green vegetables are good sources of calcium
- **Dairy products** (milk, yogurt, cheese) are also high in calcium and vitamin D
- Lean red meat, eggs, and peanut butter are good **protein** sources but do not specifically address bone health needs

### Q10. List the potential complications of restraint use.

Restraints should be a last resort. Complications include:
- **Pressure ulcers** — immobility from restraints increases skin breakdown
- **Constipation** — reduced mobility slows GI motility
- **Pneumonia** — reduced movement limits respiratory expansion
- **Urinary and fecal incontinence** — inability to toilet independently
- **Urinary retention** — inability to reach toilet
- **Contractures** — muscle and joint tightening from immobility
- **Nerve damage** — improper application can compress nerves
- **Circulatory impairment** — restricted blood flow
- **Reduced self-esteem and humiliation** — psychological harm
- **Increased agitation and confusion** — restraints often worsen confusion

> **Restraint alternatives to try FIRST:** Bed/chair alarms, low bed position, frequent rounding, companionship/sitters, reorientation, scheduled toileting, removal of unnecessary equipment.

## 📏 ASSESSMENT TOOLS — KNOW BOTH

### Morse Fall Scale — 6 Components

| Factor | Scoring |
|---|---|
| History of falling | No = 0 / Yes = 25 |
| Secondary diagnosis | No = 0 / Yes = 15 |
| Ambulatory aid | None/bed rest/nurse assist = 0 / Crutches/cane/walker = 15 / Furniture = 30 |

| Gait | Normal/bedrest/immobile = 0 / Weak = 10 / Impaired = 20 |
| Mental status | Oriented to own ability = 0 / Forgets limitations = 15 |

**Score interpretation:**
- **0–24** = Low risk
- **25–50** = Moderate risk
- **≥51** = High risk

> **Lisa Rae will likely score HIGH RISK** — she has a history of falls, uses a walker, has an IV, is weak, and is disoriented.

- **19–23** = No risk

She is currently disoriented to time and became groggy after receiving hydromorphone."

**B — Background:**
"Mrs. Rae fell at her assisted living facility after accidentally taking a double dose of hydrochlorothiazide. She was admitted for rehydration and pain management. She has a history of hypertension, osteoarthritis, osteoporosis, and prior falls. She is on 0.9% NS at 75 mL/hr, hydromorphone PRN, and acetaminophen PRN. She has experienced urinary incontinence since admission. Her CBC and BMP are within normal limits."

**A — Assessment:**
"I am concerned about her fall risk due to the combination of hydromorphone sedation, persistent disorientation, hypotension, and use of a walker. She almost fell reaching the bedside commode. I am also concerned about skin integrity given her incontinence, immobility, and right hip hematoma."

**R — Recommendation:**
"I am requesting orders for a bed exit alarm, PT/OT evaluation, and review of the hydromorphone dose given her level of sedation and fall history. I have implemented fall precautions and a scheduled toileting plan. I would also like to discuss a bowel regimen given her opioid use."

## 🦴 OSTEOPOROSIS & BONE HEALTH — Know for This Case

**Osteoporosis:** Disorder resulting in decreased bone density and increased fracture risk

**Why Lisa is at risk from her fall:**
- Osteoporotic bones fracture more easily — her right hip hematoma could worsen or she could have an undetected fracture
- Falls in patients with osteoporosis are a leading cause of hip fractures

**Dietary interventions for bone health:**
- **Calcium:** Leafy green vegetables (spinach, kale), dairy products, fortified foods
- **Vitamin D:** Fortified milk, fatty fish, sunlight exposure, supplements
- **Protein:** Supports muscle strength to prevent falls

**Fall-osteoporosis connection:** Even a minor fall in a patient with osteoporosis can cause a hip fracture → immobility → pressure ulcers → cascade of complications.
