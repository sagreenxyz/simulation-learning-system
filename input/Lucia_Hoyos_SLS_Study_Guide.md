## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **Colostomy care** — assessment of stoma, pouch changes, skin care, and patient/family education (body image, dietary adjustments, independence)
2. **Hyperkalemia from medication error** — recognizing elevated potassium, understanding dangerous ECG changes, and implementing appropriate interventions
3. **Postoperative confusion** — related to hypoxia from the medication error; position the bed low for safety

The cause-and-effect chain:

```
Transverse colostomy creation (abdominal surgery)
        ↓
Postoperative medication administration
        ↓
MEDICATION ERROR: Excess potassium administered → Hyperkalemia
        ↓
Potassium > 5.5 mEq/L → Affects cardiac cell membrane
        ↓
ECG changes: Peaked T waves → Wide QRS → Sine wave → Ventricular fibrillation
        ↓
Confusion (hypoxia from cardiac compromise)
        ↓
Fall risk → Bed must be in lowest position
        ↓
Intervene: Stop K+, ECG, IV calcium, insulin + dextrose, kayexalate, furosemide
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is a colostomy and how is it formed?

**Definition:** A colostomy is a surgical procedure in which part of the colon (large intestine) is brought through the abdominal wall to create an artificial opening (stoma) for the elimination of fecal matter, bypassing the distal colon and rectum.

**Types:**
- **Transverse colostomy** (Lucia's type): created from the transverse (middle) portion of the colon; output is typically semi-formed to liquid; often temporary
- **Sigmoid colostomy:** created from the sigmoid colon; output is most formed; often permanent (after abdominoperineal resection for rectal cancer)
- **Ascending colostomy:** right side; output is very liquid; rare
- **Descending colostomy:** left side; output is semi-formed

**Healthy stoma characteristics:**
- **Pink to red color** — indicates good blood flow
- **Moist** — like the inside of the mouth
- **Protrudes slightly** above skin surface (0.5–1.5 cm)
- **Soft** to touch
- No pain (stoma has no nerve endings)

**Abnormal stoma findings — report to provider:**
- **Dark red, purple, or black** — compromised blood supply → necrosis
- **Pale or white** — anemia or ischemia
- **Sunken/retracted** below skin level → leakage risk
- **Prolapsed** — protruding excessively
- **Bleeding** (from edges — some is normal; excessive is not)
- **Peristomal skin breakdown** — from effluent contact, improper fit

---

### Q2. What are the nursing care interventions for a patient with a colostomy?

**Pouch/Bag Management:**
1. **Empty pouch** when 1/3 to 1/2 full — prevents weight from pulling on seal
2. **Change pouching system** every 3–7 days or when seal is leaking
3. **Measure stoma** each pouch change (stoma shrinks as swelling resolves post-surgery)
4. Cut the wafer to fit stoma size (leave 1/8 inch border around stoma)

**Stoma Site Care:**
1. Clean stoma and peristomal skin with warm water and mild soap; rinse well and dry completely
2. Inspect skin for breakdown, redness, irritation (from effluent)
3. Apply skin barrier paste/powder if indicated to protect skin
4. Assess and document stoma color, size, shape, moisture

**Patient and Family Education:**
1. **Self-care:** Teach how to empty and change pouching system; return demonstration required
2. **Diet:** Avoid gas-producing foods initially; progress diet gradually; chew thoroughly; stay hydrated
3. **Body image:** Acknowledge feelings; normalize concerns; connect with ostomy nurse (WOCN)
4. **Activity:** Can return to most activities including swimming (waterproof pouches)
5. **When to call the provider:** No output for 4–6 hours, bleeding, dark stoma, leakage, abdominal cramps

### Q3. What is hyperkalemia and what are its manifestations?

**Normal potassium:** 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
**Hyperkalemia:** K+ > 5.0 mEq/L (dangerous at > 6.0 mEq/L)

**Causes:**
- Excess potassium intake (oral or IV) — as in Lucia's **medication error**
- Renal failure (inability to excrete K+)
- ACE inhibitors, potassium-sparing diuretics (spironolactone)
- Tissue breakdown (burns, rhabdomyolysis, tumor lysis)
- Metabolic acidosis (K+ moves out of cells in exchange for H+)

**Clinical Manifestations:**

**Cardiovascular (MOST DANGEROUS — ECG changes):**
- **Peaked T waves** — earliest ECG change
- **Prolonged PR interval, wide QRS**
- **Sine wave pattern** — severe
- **Ventricular fibrillation** → cardiac arrest (life-threatening)
- Bradycardia, hypotension

**Neuromuscular:**
- Muscle weakness, fatigue
- Paresthesias (tingling, numbness)
- Paralysis (severe)

**GI:**
- Nausea, vomiting
- Abdominal cramps
- Diarrhea

**Neurological:**
- Confusion (as in Lucia — also from hypoxia secondary to cardiac effects)

---

### Q4. What pharmacological agents are used to manage hyperkalemia?

| Treatment | Mechanism | Onset | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **IV Calcium gluconate** | Stabilizes cardiac membrane (not lower K+) | Immediate (1–3 min) | Given FIRST in cardiac emergency; temporary |
| **Insulin + Dextrose** | Insulin drives K+ into cells; dextrose prevents hypoglycemia | 15–30 min | Dextrose given simultaneously to prevent hypoglycemia |
| **Sodium bicarbonate** | Alkalinizes blood → K+ shifts into cells | 15–30 min | Used if acidosis present |
| **Albuterol (nebulized)** | Beta-2 activation → drives K+ into cells | 15–30 min | Adjunct; temporary |
| **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate)** | Cation exchange resin → removes K+ via GI tract | Hours | Actual K+ removal; may cause constipation |
| **Furosemide (Lasix)** | Promotes renal potassium excretion | Hours | Actual K+ removal; only works if kidneys functioning |
| **Hemodialysis** | Removes K+ from blood directly | During treatment | Last resort; severe/refractory hyperkalemia |

---

### Q5. What is the focus of care for patients with a postoperative colostomy?

1. **Stoma assessment** — monitor viability and healing
2. **Electrolyte management** — colostomy output can cause fluid/electrolyte losses, especially dehydration and hypokalemia (in contrast to Lucia's hyperkalemia from error)
3. **Pain management** — adequate pain control to allow ambulation and recovery
4. **GI assessment** — return of bowel function (sounds, flatus, stool output from stoma)
5. **Wound care** — inspect abdominal incision; assess peristomal skin
6. **Fluid status** — monitor I&O, IV fluids, and colostomy output
7. **Body image and psychological support** — major adjustment for patients
8. **Patient education** — self-care for the colostomy; dietary modifications; when to call provider
9. **WOCN consult** — wound, ostomy, continence nurse specialist for expert guidance

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
- Recognize that Lucia is **confused** — immediately place bed in **lowest position**
- Apply fall precautions
- Assess level of consciousness and orientation
- Assess colostomy: stoma color, pouch status (empty? leaking?), peristomal skin

### Recognizing Hyperkalemia — Priority Assessment
- Check most recent **serum potassium** in EHR
- Obtain **12-lead ECG** — look for peaked T waves
- Assess cardiovascular status: HR, rhythm, BP
- Assess neuromuscular: muscle weakness, paresthesias
- Review medication administration record — identify the **medication error** (excess potassium)
- **Stop any further potassium administration immediately**

### Colostomy Care During Simulation
1. Assess the stoma — is it pink and moist? (good) Or dark/pale? (notify provider)
2. Assess the pouch — is it more than 1/3 full? Empty it
3. Assess peristomal skin — redness, breakdown, rash?
4. If dressing/pouch change needed: clean, dry, measure stoma, cut wafer to fit
5. Involve Lucia in her care — ask "Do you want to watch how I do this so you can learn?"

### Managing Postoperative Confusion
- Place bed in **lowest position** — first priority for safety
- Reorient gently: "Ms. Hoyos, you are in the hospital. You are safe."
- Assess SpO2 — confusion may be from hypoxia (cardiac effects of hyperkalemia)
- Eliminate environmental hazards
- **Do NOT restrain** without specific order and exhausting alternatives
- Notify provider of confusion

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Lucia has been diagnosed with hypokalemia (in post-sim quiz). Which statement about potential causes is correct?
**D.) Illnesses resulting in diarrhea and vomiting may cause hypokalemia** — GI losses are a major cause of low potassium. Renal disease, ACE inhibitors, and tissue breakdown are associated with HYPER-kalemia (excess potassium), not hypokalemia.

> Note: The scenario involves HYPER-kalemia from a medication error. Post-simulation quiz may flip this to hypokalemia to test knowledge of both states.

---

### Q2. Lucia Hoyos' provider ordered D5½NS infusion. Which statement is correct?
**B.) This solution is hypertonic and provides 170 calories per liter** — D5½NS contains dextrose 5% plus 0.45% sodium chloride. The dextrose provides approximately 170 kcal per liter. The solution is slightly hypertonic compared to normal plasma.

**IV Fluid Types:**
- **Hypotonic:** 0.45% NS (½ NS), 0.25% NS — moves fluid into cells
- **Isotonic:** 0.9% NS, LR, D5W (in the bag) — expands intravascular volume
- **Hypertonic:** D5NS, D5½NS, D10W — draws fluid out of cells

---

### Q3. Lucia experiences postoperative confusion. Which nursing intervention is indicated?
**D.) Placing the bed in a low position** — Postoperative confusion is most often related to hypoxia. Medication reduction is not warranted (reduces pain control). Restraints cause injury and should be avoided. Contacting the physician is important but the FIRST action is ensuring safety (bed low position).

---

### Q4. When assessing areas of ecchymosis in patients, palpation alone is sufficient. True or False?
**FALSE** — Assessment requires both visualization AND palpation. Visualization identifies color changes and bony prominences at risk. Palpation detects changes in temperature (warmth = possible pressure injury or infection).

---

### Q5. What blood transfusion consideration is correct for a postoperative patient?
Only **normal saline** may be administered with blood products. LR and dextrose-containing solutions are incompatible with blood (cause hemolysis or clotting).

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Insulin (Regular) — IV for Hyperkalemia
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Antidiabetic — rapid-acting for IV use |
| **Mechanism** | Stimulates Na-K-ATPase → drives K+ from extracellular fluid into cells |
| **Indication** | Acute hyperkalemia management (drives K+ intracellularly — temporary) |
| **Route** | IV (with concurrent dextrose to prevent hypoglycemia) |
| **Nursing precautions** | ALWAYS give with dextrose; monitor blood glucose every 30–60 min; HIGH ALERT medication — requires double check |
| **Note** | Does NOT remove potassium from body — only redistributes it; other therapies needed to remove excess K+ |

---

### Sodium Polystyrene Sulfonate (Kayexalate)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Cation exchange resin |
| **Mechanism** | Sodium ions exchange for potassium ions in GI tract → potassium excreted in stool |
| **Indication** | Hyperkalemia — removes actual K+ from body |
| **Route** | PO or rectal enema |
| **Adverse effects** | Constipation, GI obstruction risk, hypokalemia (if excessive), fluid retention (sodium exchange) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor stool output and consistency; monitor K+ levels; use with caution in patients with bowel motility issues (post-surgery) |

---

### Furosemide (Lasix) — IV for Hyperkalemia
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Loop diuretic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits K+ reabsorption in the loop of Henle → promotes renal potassium excretion |
| **Indication** | Hyperkalemia — removes K+ via kidneys |
| **Note** | Only effective if kidneys are functioning adequately |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Risk for Decreased Cardiac Output** r/t hyperkalemia secondary to medication error
   - AEB: elevated K+, potential ECG changes (peaked T waves), cardiac monitoring needed

2. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t colostomy effluent contact with peristomal skin
   - AEB: new colostomy, moist effluent at stoma site

3. **Acute Confusion** r/t hypoxia secondary to cardiac effects of hyperkalemia
   - AEB: disorientation, confusion, fall risk

4. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t colostomy care, dietary modifications, and self-management
   - AEB: new ostomy; first postoperative day

5. **Risk for Falls** r/t acute confusion and weakness
   - AEB: confused patient; bed must be in lowest position
