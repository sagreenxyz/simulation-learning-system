## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes**:

1. **Distinguishing dementia from delirium and depression (the 3 D's)** — accurate differentiation guides treatment and family education
2. **Therapeutic communication with a confused or agitated patient** — redirection, validation therapy, calm tone, simple language, and non-threatening body language are essential safety and de-escalation tools
3. **Wandering precautions and environmental safety** — patients with dementia face serious fall and elopement risks requiring specific nursing interventions
4. **Family education and caregiver support** — dementia affects the entire family; education about disease progression, caregiver burden, and community resources is a nursing priority

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the stages of Alzheimer disease and what should the nurse expect in Stage 2?

Alzheimer disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disease and the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60–80% of dementia cases.

**Stages of Alzheimer Disease:**

| Stage | Description | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Stage 1 (Mild / Early)** | Functional independence maintained | Mild memory lapses, difficulty with word-finding, may get lost in familiar places, early insight into deficits |
| **Stage 2 (Moderate / Middle)** | Increasing dependence required | Significant memory loss (recent > remote), disorientation (time, place), personality/behavior changes, wandering, agitation, sundowning, ADL assistance needed |
| **Stage 3 (Severe / Late)** | Full dependence | Loss of speech and recognition of family, immobility, dysphagia, incontinence, susceptibility to infection, end-of-life care |

**Ri Liang is in Stage 2:** Expect that he may not recognize family members consistently, may be confused about where he is, may become agitated or attempt to wander, and will need assistance with ADLs.

### Q2. How do you differentiate dementia, delirium, and depression (the 3 D's)?

Accurate differentiation is critical because the causes and treatments differ significantly.

| Feature | Dementia | Delirium | Depression |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | Gradual (months–years) | Acute (hours–days) | Gradual (weeks–months) |
| **Course** | Progressive, chronic | Fluctuating | Persistent; may improve with treatment |
| **Consciousness** | Alert (until late stage) | Altered (clouded, fluctuating) | Alert |
| **Attention** | Preserved early; impaired late | Severely impaired | Mildly impaired |
| **Memory** | Recent memory impaired early | Impaired during episode | Impaired (especially concentration) |
| **Reversible?** | No (generally progressive) | Yes (with treatment of cause) | Yes (with treatment) |
| **Cause** | Neurodegeneration | Underlying medical condition (infection, metabolic) | Psychiatric/neurobiological |

**Clinical pearl:** A patient with dementia can ALSO develop delirium (called "delirium superimposed on dementia") — this is common and often triggered by infection (UTI, pneumonia), medications, or pain. Always evaluate for a medical cause of acute worsening in a dementia patient.

### Q3. What therapeutic communication strategies are effective with a confused or agitated dementia patient?

**Core principles:**

1. **Approach calmly** — slow movements, soft voice, non-threatening posture (approach from the front, get at eye level)
2. **Introduce yourself every time** — do not assume he remembers you even if you just met 10 minutes ago
3. **Use short, simple sentences** — one instruction at a time; avoid complex choices
4. **Speak slowly and clearly** — allow extra time for processing; do not rush or raise your voice
5. **Validation therapy** — meet the patient in his emotional reality rather than correcting disorientation; e.g., if he says he needs to go home to care for his children, validate the feeling: "It sounds like your family is very important to you."
6. **Redirection** — when the patient is agitated or attempting to wander, redirect to a safe, engaging activity: "Mr. Liang, let's look at these pictures together."
7. **Non-verbal communication** — gentle touch (if culturally acceptable and patient is not startled by touch), calm facial expression, reassuring tone
8. **Do NOT argue or reality-orient aggressively** — correcting a confused patient ("No, you're in the hospital, not your house") typically increases agitation

**Cultural consideration for Ri Liang (Chinese-American):**
- Family may play a central decision-making role — involve family respectfully
- Some Chinese-American patients and families may have stigma around mental illness/dementia diagnosis — approach sensitively and without judgment
- Ask about preferred language; a professional interpreter should be used for complex discussions

### Q4. What is memantine and what are the nursing implications?

**Memantine (Namenda):**

- **Drug class:** NMDA (N-methyl-D-aspartate) receptor antagonist
- **Mechanism:** Blocks excessive glutamate activity at NMDA receptors — glutamate overstimulation causes neuronal excitotoxicity contributing to cognitive decline in AD
- **Indication:** Moderate-to-severe Alzheimer disease (may be combined with donepezil)
- **Expected effect:** Slows cognitive decline; may modestly improve function — does NOT reverse or cure AD
- **Dose forms:** Tablets or extended-release capsules; oral solution available

**Nursing Implications:**
- Administer with or without food; extended-release capsule may be opened and sprinkled on food for patients with swallowing difficulty
- Monitor for: dizziness, headache, constipation, confusion (paradoxically may increase confusion in some patients initially)
- Assess baseline cognition and monitor for any change
- Educate family: this medication manages symptoms and slows progression — it is not a cure
- Do not crush extended-release tablets

**Donepezil (Aricept) — also ordered:**
- Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor; increases availability of acetylcholine (neurotransmitter depleted in AD)
- Usually given at **bedtime** (may cause vivid dreams, GI upset)
- Monitor: bradycardia (cholinergic effect), nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, syncope

### Q5. What are wandering precautions and how does the nurse implement environmental safety for a dementia patient?

**Why patients with dementia wander:**
- Disorientation — looking for a familiar place (home, past workplace)
- Unmet needs — hunger, thirst, pain, need to use bathroom
- Restlessness, anxiety, sundowning (increased confusion/agitation in late afternoon/early evening)
- Habit patterns from earlier life (e.g., a patient who used to walk to work every morning)

**Wandering Precautions — Nursing Interventions:**

| Intervention | Rationale |
|---|---|
| **Identify patient as wandering risk** (wristband, door alert) | Alerts all staff to monitor patient |
| **Bed/chair alarm** | Alerts staff when patient attempts to get up unassisted |
| **Door alarms or enclosed unit** | Prevents elopement from unit |
| **Regular toileting schedule** | Unmet need for toileting is a common trigger for wandering |
| **Engagement activities** | Redirects restlessness to safe activity (music, pictures, simple tasks) |
| **Consistent daily routine** | Reduces disorientation and anxiety |
| **Assess for pain** | Pain is often unrecognized and may cause agitation and wandering |
| **Low bed, non-skid footwear, clear environment** | Fall prevention |
| **Family at bedside when possible** | Familiar presence reduces agitation |
| **Sundowning management** | Increase lighting in late afternoon; avoid stimulating activities near bedtime; melatonin per order |

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene
2. Approach slowly from the front — make eye contact before speaking
3. Introduce yourself every time: "Hello Mr. Liang, my name is [Name] and I am your nurse today."
4. Speak calmly and slowly — use his name frequently
5. If he is agitated, do not escalate — remain calm, use a low voice, validate his feelings

### Cognitive Assessment (Mini-Mental State Exam / MMSE)
- Orientation: "Can you tell me today's date? What city are we in?" (1 point each)
- Registration: Name 3 objects; ask patient to repeat (3 points)
- Attention/Calculation: Count backwards from 100 by 7s (5 points)
- Recall: Ask for the 3 objects named earlier (3 points)
- Language: Name items, follow 3-step command, read/write simple sentence (8 points)
- Total possible: 30 points; ≤23 suggests cognitive impairment

### Medication Administration — Memantine and Citalopram
- Confirm the 5–9 rights of medication administration
- Assess swallowing ability before giving oral medications — aspiration risk in dementia
- Offer medications with food or small amount of water
- Document administration in EHR

### Family Education Key Points
- Explain what Stage 2 Alzheimer disease means in plain language
- Discuss wandering precautions and home safety modifications (door locks, stove guards)
- Provide 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline and Alzheimer's Association (800-272-3900) resources
- Assess caregiver burden — ask family how they are coping
- Discuss advance directives and goals of care in a sensitive, unhurried manner

### Q2. What is sundowning and how is it managed?

**Sundowning** (also called "late-day confusion") refers to increased confusion, agitation, restlessness, and behavioral disturbances in dementia patients that typically worsen in the late afternoon and early evening.

**Possible causes:** Fatigue, disruption of circadian rhythm, decreased light levels triggering disorientation, overstimulation during the day.

**Nursing Management:**
- Increase lighting levels in the late afternoon and evening
- Maintain a consistent daily routine — meals, activities, and sleep at the same times
- Limit napping during the day to preserve nighttime sleep
- Reduce stimulation and noise in the evening
- Engage in calm, familiar activities (music, looking at family photos)
- Administer melatonin per order at consistent bedtime
- Assess for unmet needs (pain, hunger, need to void) before assuming sundowning is purely behavioral

### Q3. What resources are available for families of patients with dementia?

- **Alzheimer's Association:** 800-272-3900 (24/7 helpline); www.alz.org — information, support groups, care consultations
- **Adult Day Programs:** Structured daytime programming for patients; provides respite for family caregivers
- **Respite Care:** Short-term relief for caregivers; in-home or facility-based
- **Home Health Agency:** Skilled nursing visits, home health aide assistance with ADLs
- **Social Work Referral:** Assists with care coordination, placement options, financial resources, advance directive guidance
- **National Institute on Aging:** www.nia.nih.gov — evidence-based caregiver resources
- **Local Area Agency on Aging:** Connects families with community-based services
