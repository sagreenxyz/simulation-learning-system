## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is Alzheimer disease and what are the stages?

**Alzheimer Disease (AD):** The most common form of dementia (~60–70% of all dementia cases). A progressive, irreversible neurodegenerative disease characterized by accumulation of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles → neuronal death → cognitive decline.

**Stages of Alzheimer Disease:**

**Early (Mild) — Stage 1:**
- Forgetfulness beyond normal aging (recent events, names)
- Difficulty with problem-solving, planning
- May get lost in familiar places
- Personality changes (irritability, anxiety)
- Manages most daily activities independently
- Often not yet diagnosed

**Middle (Moderate) — Stage 2 (Ri Liang's stage):**
- **Increasing memory loss** — may forget names of family members
- **Confusion** about time, place, and familiar events
- **Wandering** — significant safety risk
- **Communication difficulties** — word-finding, following conversation
- Needs assistance with ADLs (dressing, bathing, toileting)
- Behavioral symptoms: agitation, paranoia, hallucinations, sundowning
- Cannot live safely alone

**Late (Severe) — Stage 3:**
- Complete loss of short and long-term memory
- Minimal verbal communication
- Fully dependent for all ADLs
- Immobility, dysphagia, incontinence
- Susceptible to infections (aspiration pneumonia, UTI)

### Q2. What are the signs and symptoms of dementia vs. normal aging?

| Normal Aging | Dementia |
|---|---|
| Occasionally misplacing keys | Putting keys in unusual places and forgetting |
| Forgetting a name briefly | Forgetting people's names entirely |
| Taking longer to learn new things | Unable to learn new things |
| Forgetting occasionally what you came for | Forgetting what common objects are for |
| Sometimes losing the word | Losing words mid-sentence frequently |
| Making occasional poor decisions | Consistently poor judgment; financial exploitation |

**Important distinction:** Normal aging causes slowing; dementia causes qualitative changes in cognition.

### Q3. What communication strategies are used for patients with dementia?

**Principles:**
- **Slow, clear, short sentences** — one instruction at a time
- **Simple vocabulary** — avoid complex language and abstract concepts
- **Non-threatening body language** — approach calmly from the front; get to eye level
- **Calm tone** — speak softly and slowly; anxiety is contagious
- **Validation** — do not argue with or correct the patient's reality
- **Redirection** — when patient is upset or fixated, gently redirect to a different topic or activity
- **Distraction** — use familiar objects, music, favorite activities

**What to AVOID:**
- Correcting or arguing ("That's not right — your wife died 10 years ago")
- Asking questions that test memory ("Do you remember what you had for breakfast?")
- Giving too many choices
- Speaking about the patient as if they aren't there
- Raising voice (escalates agitation)

**Validation Technique:**
When Ri insists something that isn't true, don't correct — validate the feeling:
"It sounds like you're worried about getting home. Tell me more about that."

### Q4. What are wandering precautions?

Wandering is one of the most dangerous behaviors in dementia — patients may leave the building, fall, or become lost.

**Wandering Precautions:**
1. **Alarmed door systems** — door alarms that alert staff when patient approaches exits
2. **Coded/locked unit doors** — patient cannot exit without staff assistance
3. **ID bracelet/ankle band** — wander-guard systems; GPS tracking devices
4. **Photographs** — current photo in chart for identification if patient leaves
5. **Staff awareness** — all unit staff know which patients are at risk
6. **Structured activity** — regular activities to reduce restlessness that drives wandering
7. **Visual barriers** — covering door handles or exits with cloth or stop signs (may reduce exit-seeking)
8. **Night protocols** — bed alarms; more frequent checks; adequate lighting (darkness increases confusion)
9. **Document and reassess** — track frequency and patterns of wandering

### Q5. What medications are used in Alzheimer disease and what do nurses need to know?

**Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors (mild–moderate AD):**
- Donepezil (Aricept), rivastigmine (Exelon), galantamine (Razadyne)
- **Mechanism:** Inhibit breakdown of acetylcholine → more ACh available for remaining neurons
- **Effect:** Modest slowing of cognitive decline; improves daily function
- **Side effects:** GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — especially with dose increases), bradycardia, insomnia
- **Nursing:** Give donepezil at bedtime; do not abruptly stop

**NMDA Receptor Antagonist (moderate–severe AD):**
- Memantine (Namenda)
- **Mechanism:** Blocks NMDA receptors → reduces glutamate excitotoxicity → protects neurons
- **Effect:** Slows moderate-severe cognitive decline; may improve function
- **Side effects:** Dizziness, headache, confusion (paradoxically in some patients)
- **Nursing:** May be used in combination with donepezil

**Antidepressants (for behavioral symptoms and comorbid depression):**
- SSRIs (citalopram, escitalopram, sertraline) — preferred for behavioral symptoms, less anticholinergic
- **Nursing:** SSRIs increase fall risk; monitor orthostatic BP; assess for serotonin syndrome

---

### Q6. How does the nurse provide family education about dementia?

**Key Teaching Areas:**
1. **Disease trajectory** — progressive and irreversible; honest information helps families plan
2. **Communication strategies** — validation, redirection, calm approach (as above)
3. **Safety** — wandering precautions; stove safety; fall prevention; driving cessation
4. **Personal care** — how to assist with ADLs while preserving dignity
5. **Behavioral symptoms** — sundowning (increased confusion at dusk); triggers; de-escalation
6. **Caregiver support** — burnout is common; respite care; support groups; Alzheimer's Association
7. **Advance directives** — discuss while patient still has some capacity
8. **Placement planning** — when home care is no longer safe; types of memory care facilities

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Approach Ri calmly from the front at eye level
2. Introduce yourself each time (he may not remember you from earlier in the shift)
3. Assess orientation: "Mr. Liang, do you know where you are today?" (use gently — don't quiz)
4. Assess safety: Where is he? Is he near exits? Is he agitated?
5. Assess for physical causes of worsening confusion: UTI, dehydration, pain, medication effect

### Calming an Agitated Patient with Dementia
1. Speak in low, calm voice: "Mr. Liang, my name is [Name]. You're safe here."
2. Eliminate stimulation — turn off TV or radio; reduce noise
3. Validate: "You look worried. Can you tell me what's bothering you?"
4. Redirect: "Let's go sit together over here and I'll get you some water."
5. Do not argue or correct
6. Allow space — do not crowd; approach from the front
7. Distraction — offer familiar object, photograph, music, activity

### Administering Medications to a Resistant Patient
- Offer medications with a small amount of preferred food/drink
- "Mr. Liang, it's time for your morning pills. Here's some apple juice."
- If he refuses, document and notify provider — do NOT force medication
- Consider alternative route/formulation per order if consistently refusing

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. Priority safety concern for Ri Liang?
**Wandering** — Stage 2 Alzheimer patients have sufficient mobility to wander but insufficient judgment to recognize danger. Initiate all wandering precautions immediately on admission.

### Q2. Therapeutic response when Ri insists he needs to go home to pick up his children from school?
Do NOT correct or argue. Validate the feeling: "It sounds like your children are very important to you. Tell me about them." Then redirect: "Before we do anything, let's sit down together and have some tea."

### Q3. What is sundowning?
Increased confusion, agitation, and behavioral symptoms in late afternoon and evening. Common in dementia. Causes: fatigue, decreased lighting, shift change disruption. Management: structured activities in afternoon; adequate lighting; calm evening routine; minimize changes.

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Chronic Confusion** r/t Alzheimer disease — AEB: disorientation, memory loss, inappropriate behavior
2. **Risk for Injury (Wandering)** r/t impaired judgment and spatial disorientation — AEB: Stage 2 Alzheimer; wandering behavior
3. **Impaired Verbal Communication** r/t neurological deterioration — AEB: word-finding difficulties, aphasia
4. **Self-Care Deficit** r/t progressive cognitive and functional decline — AEB: needs assistance with ADLs
5. **Caregiver Role Strain** r/t progressive nature of disease and care demands — AEB: family pursuing placement; caregiver burnout
