## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the priority postoperative assessments a nurse should focus on?

Using the **ABCDE mnemonic** for postoperative assessment:

**Airway and Breathing:**
- Respiratory rate, depth, and pattern
- SpO2 — maintain ≥95%
- Lung sounds — listen for crackles (atelectasis), wheezing, or diminished sounds
- Signs of atelectasis: decreased breath sounds, fever, productive cough
- Pain with breathing — may cause splinting and shallow breathing

**Cardiovascular:**
- Blood pressure, heart rate, and rhythm
- Signs of bleeding: hypotension, tachycardia, pallor
- IV site assessment
- Peripheral pulses in operative extremity
- DVT signs: calf pain, redness, swelling, warmth

**Pain:**
- Full pain assessment (see Q2 below)
- Assess before and 30–60 minutes after analgesic administration

**Neurovascular/Extremity Assessment (Hip):**
- The 5 P's: **Pain, Pulses, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Pallor**
- Compare right leg (operative) to left (non-operative)
- Assess for hip dislocation signs (sudden severe pain, external rotation, leg shortening)

**Other:**
- Temperature — postop fever at 24–48 hrs often = atelectasis; after 72 hrs = possible infection
- Urine output — goal ≥30 mL/hr
- Wound — assess dressing: intact, amount of drainage
- Medication reconciliation — levothyroxine and pantoprazole must be continued

### Q2. What are the six components of a complete pain assessment?

Using the **OPQRST framework (or PQRSTU):**

1. **O — Onset** (Timing): When did the pain begin? How long has it lasted? Is it intermittent or constant?

2. **P — Precipitating/Palliating factors**: What makes it better? What makes it worse? Does movement, position, breathing affect it?

3. **Q — Quality**: What does the pain feel like? (Aching, stabbing, throbbing, burning, sharp, dull)

4. **R — Region/Radiation**: Where is the pain? Does it radiate anywhere?

5. **S — Severity**: On a scale of 0–10, how severe is the pain? Use numeric, FACES, or behavioral scale as appropriate

6. **T — Treatment/Impact**: What relief measures have been tried? Does the pain affect function, sleep, mood?

**Additional elements:**
- **Verbal/Behavioral cues:** Facial grimacing, guarding, moaning, restlessness (useful if patient cannot self-report)
- **Relief measures at home:** Does the patient use heat, cold, repositioning, over-the-counter medications?

### Q3. What are ways to manage postoperative pain (pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic)?

**Pharmacologic:**
- **Opioids:** Morphine, hydrocodone, oxycodone — for moderate-to-severe pain; monitor for respiratory depression, sedation, constipation
- **Non-opioid analgesics:** Acetaminophen — mild-moderate pain; adjunct to opioids; reduces opioid requirements
- **NSAIDs:** Ibuprofen, ketorolac — anti-inflammatory; but use with caution in postoperative patients (GI bleeding risk; Boyd is on pantoprazole for GI protection)
- **Patient-Controlled Analgesia (PCA):** Patient self-administers IV opioid within set parameters; promotes autonomy and better pain control
- **Epidural analgesia:** Regional; excellent for lower extremity surgery
- **Local anesthesia / nerve blocks:** Targeted pain relief at surgical site

**Non-pharmacologic:**
- **Repositioning and positioning** — reduces pressure on surgical site
- **Ice/cold therapy** — reduces inflammation and swelling at surgical site (ice packs to hip)
- **Heat therapy** — for muscle spasm (consult provider before applying)
- **Relaxation and guided imagery** — distraction from pain; reduces anxiety
- **Deep breathing exercises** — reduces pain perception and prevents respiratory complications
- **Distraction** — music, TV, conversation
- **TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)** — adjunct therapy
- **Proper splinting** — supporting incision area during movement or coughing

### Q4. What is atelectasis and what interventions help?

**What is atelectasis?**
Collapse or incomplete expansion of alveoli (air sacs) in the lungs. It is the **most common postoperative respiratory complication**, typically occurring within the first **24–48 hours** after surgery.

**Why it happens post-surgery:**
- General anesthesia depresses respiratory effort
- Pain causes patients to breathe shallowly and avoid deep breaths (splinting)
- Immobility reduces normal lung expansion
- Secretion retention blocks airways

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Low-grade fever (24–48 hours post-op)
- Decreased breath sounds in affected area
- Crackles / rales on auscultation
- Decreased SpO2
- Productive cough

**Interventions to prevent and treat atelectasis:**
1. **Incentive spirometry** — have patient inhale slowly and deeply to a target volume, 10 times every 1–2 hours while awake
2. **Deep breathing and coughing exercises** — with splinting of incision (Boyd should hold a pillow against his hip when coughing)
3. **Early ambulation** — even chair sitting promotes lung expansion
4. **Adequate pain control** — patients with uncontrolled pain will not deep breathe; pain management and respiratory care are directly linked
5. **Positioning** — semi-Fowler's to high-Fowler's maximizes lung expansion
6. **Hydration** — thins secretions

### Q5. What complications can occur with a total right hip arthroplasty postoperatively?

**High priority complications:**

| Complication | Signs | Prevention/Action |
|---|---|---|
| **DVT / PE** | Calf pain, swelling, redness; sudden dyspnea, chest pain (PE) | Enoxaparin SQ, early ambulation, SCDs, leg exercises |
| **Hip dislocation** | Sudden severe pain, external rotation, shortened leg | Hip precautions (see below) |
| **Infection / Surgical site** | Redness, warmth, purulent drainage, fever | Sterile technique, wound assessment |
| **Hemorrhage** | Hypotension, tachycardia, excessive drainage | Monitor VS, dressing, Hgb/Hct |
| **Atelectasis / Pneumonia** | Fever, crackles, decreased SpO2 | Incentive spirometry, early mobility |
| **Constipation** | Absence of bowel movements, distension | Bowel regimen with opioid use; pantoprazole already ordered |
| **Pressure ulcer** | Redness, breakdown on heels, sacrum | Positioning, offloading, reposition q2h |
| **Nerve damage** | Numbness, tingling, weakness in operative leg | Neurovascular checks every 4 hours |

### Q6. What are the parasympathetic reactions to pain?

When pain is severe, the autonomic nervous system responds:

**Sympathetic (fight-or-flight) response — more common with acute pain:**
- Tachycardia
- Hypertension
- Diaphoresis
- Pallor (blood shunts away from periphery)
- Dilated pupils
- Dry mouth

**Parasympathetic response — can occur with severe visceral or sustained pain:**
- **Pallor:** Blood supply shifts away from periphery
- **Nausea/Vomiting:** Vagus nerve sends impulses to the chemoreceptor trigger zone in the brain
- **Bradycardia:** Vagal stimulation slows the heart
- **Hypotension:** Vasodilation from prolonged pain response
- **Syncope (fainting):** Combination of bradycardia and hypotension

> **For Boyd:** Uncontrolled postoperative pain can cause both sympathetic and parasympathetic responses. Effective pain management is also cardiac and respiratory protection.

### Q7. Why is pain management so important in postoperative patients?

1. **Respiratory:** Uncontrolled pain → splinting → shallow breathing → atelectasis → pneumonia
2. **Cardiovascular:** Pain → sympathetic activation → tachycardia → increased myocardial oxygen demand
3. **Mobility:** Pain → immobility → DVT risk, pressure ulcers, muscle weakness, delayed recovery
4. **Psychological:** Uncontrolled pain → anxiety, depression, and prolonged hospital stay
5. **Sleep:** Pain disrupts healing and immune function
6. **Patient satisfaction:** Effective pain management is directly linked to patient satisfaction outcomes
7. **Healing:** Adequate pain control allows participation in deep breathing exercises, physical therapy, and ambulation — all essential for recovery

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Hip Replacement Precautions (Must Know!)
To prevent hip dislocation after total hip arthroplasty:

1. **No hip flexion beyond 90 degrees** — do not bend the hip more than a right angle
2. **No adduction** — do not cross legs or bring operative leg across midline
3. **No internal rotation** — do not turn toes inward
4. **Use a raised toilet seat** — prevents excessive hip flexion
5. **Use an abduction pillow** — keeps legs apart when in bed
6. **Rise from chair by scooting to edge first** — then stand, keeping operative hip extended

> **Mnemonic for hip precautions:** No bending, no crossing, no turning in

### Pain Assessment Before Medications
- Ask for numeric pain rating (0–10)
- If pain is ≥ 4, administer ordered analgesic
- Use multimodal approach: offer acetaminophen, ice pack, repositioning in addition to opioids
- Pre-medicate 5–10 minutes before painful procedures (dressing changes, physical therapy)

### Incentive Spirometry Teaching
- "Take a slow, deep breath in through the mouthpiece — as slowly and as deeply as you can"
- Raise the piston to the goal line and hold for 3–5 seconds
- Exhale slowly; rest; repeat 10 times every hour while awake
- Encourage and praise: "That's great — you're helping your lungs stay healthy and open."

### Enoxaparin (Lovenox) SQ Injection — Key Points
- Administer SQ into abdomen (not near umbilicus)
- Do NOT expel the air bubble in the prefilled syringe — it helps prevent bruising
- Do NOT aspirate before injecting
- Do NOT rub the site afterward (causes bruising)
- Monitor for bleeding signs and platelet count (HIT — heparin-induced thrombocytopenia)

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are early vs. late signs of DVT?

- **Early:** Calf tenderness, swelling, warmth, redness; Homan's sign (calf pain with dorsiflexion — not a reliable sign but still sometimes assessed)
- **Late/PE:** Sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, decreased SpO2, hemoptysis
- If PE suspected: call rapid response; oxygen immediately; notify provider

---

### Q2. Why is levothyroxine given in the postoperative patient?

Levothyroxine (Synthroid) replaces thyroid hormone. Boyd has hypothyroidism. Postoperative patients with hypothyroidism who miss doses can develop **myxedema coma** — a rare but life-threatening complication of severely low thyroid hormone. Levothyroxine should be continued postoperatively as scheduled.

**Key nursing note:** Give levothyroxine on an **empty stomach, 30–60 minutes before breakfast** for maximum absorption. Avoid giving with calcium, antacids, or iron supplements.

### Q3. What nursing diagnoses apply to Boyd Dubois?

1. **Acute Pain** r/t surgical incision and tissue manipulation — AEB pain score, guarding, facial grimacing
2. **Impaired Physical Mobility** r/t postoperative hip replacement and pain — AEB inability to ambulate independently
3. **Ineffective Breathing Pattern** r/t postoperative pain splinting — AEB shallow respirations, not performing incentive spirometry
4. **Risk for DVT** r/t immobility, surgical procedure, and altered clotting from Lovenox therapy
5. **Risk for Dislocation** r/t hip arthroplasty — AEB need for hip precautions education
