## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes**:

1. **Recognition of worsening preeclampsia** — identifying severe features and impending eclampsia before and during seizure onset
2. **Eclamptic seizure management** — immediate, prioritized, safe nursing interventions during and after a tonic-clonic seizure
3. **Magnesium sulfate** — understanding the mechanism, dosing, toxicity signs, and antidote of the drug of choice for eclampsia
4. **Family-centered care** — managing an anxious husband at the bedside while maintaining patient safety and providing emotional support

The clinical context:

```
Preeclampsia (BP ≥140/90 + proteinuria + ≥20 weeks gestation)
        ↓
Severe features: BP ≥160/110, headache, visual changes, RUQ pain, low platelets
        ↓
Eclampsia = seizure in a preeclamptic patient (life-threatening emergency)
        ↓
Seizure: left lateral position → oxygen → suction → call for help → time seizure
        ↓
Post-seizure: reassess maternal/fetal status → magnesium sulfate → notify provider
        ↓
Delivery is the definitive treatment for eclampsia
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the diagnostic criteria for preeclampsia, and what distinguishes preeclampsia with severe features?

**Preeclampsia** is a hypertensive disorder of pregnancy diagnosed when ALL of the following are present:
- Blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart
- After 20 weeks of gestation
- Proteinuria (≥300 mg/24 hours, protein:creatinine ratio ≥0.3, or dipstick ≥2+)

**Preeclampsia with severe features** is diagnosed when ANY of the following are present (even without proteinuria):
- Severe-range BP: ≥ 160 systolic OR ≥ 110 diastolic on two occasions 4 hours apart (or sooner if antihypertensives needed)
- Thrombocytopenia (platelets < 100,000/µL)
- Renal insufficiency (creatinine > 1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline)
- Impaired liver function (transaminases 2x normal) or severe RUQ/epigastric pain
- Pulmonary edema
- New-onset headache unresponsive to medication, or visual disturbances

**HELLP syndrome** is a variant of severe preeclampsia: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets — associated with significant maternal morbidity.

**Eclampsia** = new-onset grand mal (tonic-clonic) seizure in a woman with preeclampsia in the absence of other neurologic conditions.

### Q2. What is magnesium sulfate, and how does it work in eclampsia? What are signs of toxicity?

**Magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄)** is the drug of choice for both seizure prophylaxis in preeclampsia with severe features and for treatment of eclamptic seizures.

**Mechanism:** Magnesium acts as a CNS depressant by blocking N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptors and reducing cerebral vasospasm. It raises the seizure threshold.

**Typical dosing:**
- Loading dose: 4–6 g IV over 15–20 minutes
- Maintenance: 1–2 g/hr IV continuous infusion

**Therapeutic level:** 4–7 mEq/L

**Signs of magnesium toxicity (know this order!):**

| Magnesium Level | Signs |
|---|---|
| 4–7 mEq/L | Therapeutic — seizure prophylaxis |
| 7–10 mEq/L | Loss of deep tendon reflexes (patellar reflex lost first) |
| 10–13 mEq/L | Respiratory depression, respiratory rate < 12 |
| > 15 mEq/L | Cardiac arrest |

**Nursing assessments before each dose / every hour:**
- Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) — must be present before continuing
- Respiratory rate — must be ≥ 12 breaths/min
- Urine output — must be ≥ 30 mL/hr (magnesium is renally cleared)
- Level of consciousness

**Antidote:** Calcium gluconate 1 g IV push — have at bedside at ALL times during magnesium infusion.

### Q3. What are the priority nursing interventions during an eclamptic seizure?

**During the seizure (in order of priority):**
1. **Stay with the patient** — never leave
2. **Call for help** — activate emergency response, call provider
3. **Position:** Turn to **left lateral (left side-lying)** position — prevents aorto-caval compression and reduces aspiration risk
4. **Protect airway:** Suction if secretions present; do NOT insert anything into the mouth
5. **Administer oxygen** — 8–10 L/min via face mask to maintain maternal oxygenation and fetal perfusion
6. **Time the seizure** — document onset, duration, and characteristics
7. **Pad side rails / lower bed** — prevent fall injury
8. **Do NOT restrain** limbs — allow seizure to proceed while protecting from injury

**After the seizure:**
- Reorient the patient — expect post-ictal confusion
- Complete maternal reassessment: BP, HR, RR, O₂ saturation, DTRs, LOC
- Reassess fetal heart rate — expect transient bradycardia or late decelerations after seizure
- Administer or re-bolus magnesium sulfate per order
- Notify provider immediately with SBAR report
- Document everything: time, duration, characteristics, interventions, patient response

### Q4. How does the nurse manage the anxious family member (husband) at the bedside during this emergency?

**Principles of family-centered care during an emergency:**
- Acknowledge the husband's fear directly: *"I can see you're scared. So are we — and we are doing everything we can to help her."*
- Assign a dedicated staff member (charge nurse, social worker, or second nurse) to remain with the husband if possible
- Provide brief, honest, simple updates during and after the seizure
- Do not ask the husband to leave unless his presence directly compromises care
- After the seizure, explain what happened in plain language: *"Rona had a seizure, which can happen with high blood pressure in pregnancy. We are treating her and monitoring the baby closely."*
- Offer to contact additional support (chaplain, social worker)
- Document family communication in the EHR

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not ignore the husband — unaddressed anxiety can escalate to disruptive behavior
- Do not provide false reassurance or minimize the seriousness
- Do not use technical jargon without explanation

### Q5. What are the nursing responsibilities related to fetal monitoring in a patient with preeclampsia progressing to eclampsia?

**Continuous electronic fetal monitoring (EFM)** is mandatory for all patients with preeclampsia with severe features.

**Normal fetal heart rate (FHR) baseline:** 110–160 beats per minute

**Reassuring FHR pattern:**
- Moderate variability (6–25 bpm)
- Accelerations present
- No late or variable decelerations

**Expected FHR changes during/after eclamptic seizure:**
- Transient **fetal bradycardia** — caused by maternal hypoxia and uteroplacental insufficiency during seizure
- **Late decelerations** — indicate uteroplacental insufficiency; begin after contraction peak and return after contraction ends
- **Prolonged deceleration** — FHR < 110 for > 2 minutes; may require emergency intervention

**Nursing response to non-reassuring FHR:**
1. Reposition patient to left lateral
2. Increase IV fluid rate (per order/protocol)
3. Administer supplemental oxygen
4. Discontinue oxytocin if infusing
5. Notify provider immediately
6. Prepare for possible emergency cesarean delivery

**Key point:** After the seizure is controlled, delivery is the **definitive treatment** for eclampsia. The nurse should anticipate and prepare for induction of labor or cesarean delivery.

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene
2. Introduce yourself — address her as "Ms. Berends"
3. Verify identity (two identifiers: name + date of birth)
4. Perform initial assessment: BP, HR, RR, O₂ sat, pain scale, FHR strip review
5. Assess for severe features: headache, visual changes, RUQ pain, edema, DTRs

### When Severe Features Are Present
1. Raise side rails; lower bed to lowest position
2. Ensure IV access is patent; confirm magnesium infusion is running
3. Ensure calcium gluconate is at bedside
4. Increase monitoring frequency per protocol
5. Notify charge nurse and provider of change in condition

### When Seizure Begins
1. **Call for help** — "I need help in room [X]!" / activate emergency call system
2. **Stay at bedside** — do not leave
3. **Position:** left lateral, protect airway
4. **Oxygen:** apply 100% non-rebreather mask at 10 L/min
5. **Time the seizure** — note start time; describe tonic-clonic activity
6. **Suction** if secretions or vomiting present
7. **Pad side rails** — do not restrain

### Post-Seizure Care
1. Reassess BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, LOC, DTRs
2. Reassess fetal heart rate pattern
3. Administer magnesium sulfate bolus per provider order
4. Orient patient: *"Rona, you had a seizure. You're safe. I'm your nurse. We are monitoring you and the baby closely."*
5. Address husband with brief, calm explanation
6. Complete SBAR report to provider
7. Document all events, interventions, and patient responses in EHR

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. What nursing diagnoses apply to Rona Berends?

5. **Risk for Aspiration** r/t seizure activity and altered LOC

### Q2. What are the parameters the nurse must assess before continuing the magnesium sulfate infusion?

Before every dose and at minimum hourly, the nurse must verify:
- **Deep tendon reflexes** (patellar reflex) — must be present; absent DTRs = toxicity
- **Respiratory rate** — must be ≥ 12 breaths/min; depression indicates toxicity
- **Urine output** — must be ≥ 30 mL/hr; oliguria = magnesium accumulation risk
- **Level of consciousness** — increasing sedation may indicate toxicity
- **Serum magnesium level** (when ordered) — therapeutic range 4–7 mEq/L

If any parameter is outside safe range: **hold infusion and notify provider immediately.**

---

### Q3. Why is delivery the definitive treatment for eclampsia, and what is the nurse's role in preparing for delivery?

Eclampsia is caused by the pathophysiology of preeclampsia, which originates from **abnormal placentation**. The only cure is removal of the placenta — i.e., delivery.

**Nurse's role in preparing for delivery:**
- Confirm IV access (large bore, 18G or larger)
- Ensure type and screen or crossmatch is complete
- Confirm fetal heart rate monitoring is continuous and documented
- Assist with cervical assessment and Bishop score
- Prepare oxytocin infusion for induction if vaginal delivery planned
- Prepare for operating room (OR) if cesarean delivery indicated
- Ensure consent is obtained for delivery method
- Provide education to patient and husband about delivery plan and what to expect
