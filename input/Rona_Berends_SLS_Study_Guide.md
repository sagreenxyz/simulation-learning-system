## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is preeclampsia and what are the criteria for diagnosis?

**Preeclampsia:** A pregnancy-specific disorder characterized by new-onset hypertension and either proteinuria or end-organ dysfunction after 20 weeks gestation.

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- **Hypertension:** SBP ≥ 140 mmHg OR DBP ≥ 90 mmHg on two occasions at least 4 hours apart
- **Plus one of:**
  - Proteinuria ≥ 300 mg/24-hour collection or protein:creatinine ratio ≥ 0.3
  - Thrombocytopenia (platelets < 100,000)
  - Renal insufficiency (creatinine > 1.1 mg/dL)
  - Impaired liver function (LFTs 2x normal)
  - Pulmonary edema
  - New-onset headache unresponsive to medication OR visual disturbances

**Severe Features:** SBP ≥ 160 or DBP ≥ 110 mmHg; severe headache; visual changes; epigastric/RUQ pain; pulmonary edema; thrombocytopenia; severe renal insufficiency

**Risk Factors (Rona has multiple):**
- Nulliparity (first pregnancy — most significant risk factor)
- Advanced maternal age (≥ 35)
- Multiple gestation
- Chronic hypertension, diabetes, renal disease
- Obesity
- Personal/family history of preeclampsia

---

### Q2. What is eclampsia and how is it different from preeclampsia?

**Eclampsia** = Preeclampsia + **New-onset grand mal (tonic-clonic) seizures** not attributable to other causes

- Seizures can occur before, during, or after delivery
- Can occur in patients with no prior preeclampsia diagnosis
- Life-threatening to both mother and fetus
- Leading cause of maternal mortality in preeclampsia

**Warning Signs of Impending Eclampsia:**
- Severe persistent headache
- Visual disturbances (blurred vision, scotoma — blind spots, flashing lights)
- Epigastric or RUQ pain (liver stretching)
- Hyperreflexia (especially with clonus — rhythmic jerking when ankle dorsiflexed)
- Rapidly rising blood pressure

### Q3. How does the nurse manage an eclamptic seizure?

**DURING the seizure:**
1. **Do NOT restrain** — allow seizure to run its course; prevent injury without restraining
2. **Protect from injury:** pad bed rails; remove hard objects; turn to **left lateral position** if possible (prevents aspiration; relieves aortic compression on IVC → maintains fetal perfusion)
3. **Call for help** — initiate rapid response/obstetric emergency team
4. **Do NOT insert tongue blade or airway device** during seizure (risk of injury)
5. **Time the seizure** — duration, character
6. **O2 via face mask** — apply when safe to do so
7. **IV access** — ensure patent

**IMMEDIATELY AFTER seizure:**
1. **Lateral position maintained** — prevents aspiration
2. **Suction** — airway clearance if needed
3. **O2** — continue
4. **Administer magnesium sulfate IV** per order — prevents recurrence
5. **Notify provider** — SBAR
6. **Monitor fetal heart rate** — fetal bradycardia common during/after maternal seizure; should recover within 3–5 minutes
7. **Vital signs** — frequent BP monitoring (q5 min initially)
8. **Reassure patient** when conscious — she will be confused and frightened

---

### Q4. What is magnesium sulfate and how does the nurse manage it?

**Mechanism:** CNS depressant; decreases neuromuscular excitability → prevents seizures; mechanism not fully understood

**Administration:**
- **Loading dose:** 4–6 g IV over 15–20 minutes
- **Maintenance:** 1–2 g/hr IV continuous infusion

**Therapeutic range:** 4–7 mEq/L (for seizure prophylaxis)

**Magnesium Toxicity Signs (in order of increasing serum level):**
| Serum Level | Signs |
|---|---|
| 4–7 mEq/L | Therapeutic — flushing, warmth, nausea |
| 7–10 mEq/L | Loss of deep tendon reflexes (DTR) → HOLD infusion |
| 10–13 mEq/L | Respiratory depression (RR < 12) → STOP; give antidote |
| > 13 mEq/L | Cardiac arrest |

**Nursing Monitoring (the THREE checks — every hour):**
1. **Deep tendon reflexes (DTR)** — patellar reflex; HOLD if absent
2. **Respiratory rate** — ≥ 12/min required; HOLD if < 12
3. **Urine output** — ≥ 25–30 mL/hr; magnesium excreted by kidneys

**Antidote:** Calcium gluconate 1 g IV slow push — must be at bedside at all times

---

### Q5. What postpartum monitoring is required for preeclampsia/eclampsia patients?

- Preeclampsia can **worsen postpartum** — typically resolves within 6 weeks but can progress to eclampsia in first 48 hours after delivery
- BP monitoring every 4–8 hours postpartum
- Continue magnesium sulfate 24–48 hours postpartum (per order)
- Continue antihypertensives if BP remains elevated
- Monitor for HELLP syndrome: Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets — life-threatening variant
- Educate about outpatient BP monitoring and warning signs (headache, visual changes, epigastric pain)
- Emotional support — this is a traumatic experience; assess for birth trauma/PTSD

---

### Q6. How does the nurse manage the husband during the crisis?

- **Direct to safe location** — outside the room during the seizure; a staff member stays with him
- **Brief honest communication** — "Rona is having a seizure. This is a complication of her blood pressure condition. We are taking care of her and the baby."
- **Involve when stable** — once seizure resolved and Rona is stable, invite husband in; update fully
- **Assess for shock/distress** — support person may also need emotional care
- **Encourage questions** — fear of the unknown is worse than information

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Pre-Seizure — Recognizing Worsening Preeclampsia
- Monitor BP frequently (every 15–30 minutes per order)
- Assess for headache (2+), visual changes, epigastric pain
- Check deep tendon reflexes for hyperreflexia and clonus
- Notify provider of worsening findings BEFORE seizure occurs

### During Seizure Response
1. Do NOT leave patient
2. Position left lateral if possible
3. Call for help (activate emergency)
4. Time the seizure
5. Apply O2 when safe
6. Do NOT insert anything in mouth

### Post-Seizure
1. Assess airway — suction if needed
2. Administer magnesium sulfate per order
3. Apply continuous fetal monitoring — assess FHR
4. Vital signs — especially BP every 5 minutes
5. Reassure patient: "Rona, you're safe. You had a seizure. The baby is being monitored."
6. Prepare for possible delivery (eclampsia = delivery indication)

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Risk for Injury (Maternal)** r/t eclamptic seizure activity — AEB: tonic-clonic seizure; worsening hypertension
2. **Risk for Injury (Fetal)** r/t uteroplacental insufficiency during seizure — AEB: fetal bradycardia during maternal seizure
3. **Ineffective Tissue Perfusion (Cerebral, Placental)** r/t severe hypertension — AEB: BP ≥ 160/110; visual changes; headache
4. **Anxiety (Patient and Partner)** r/t life-threatening pregnancy complication — AEB: fear, confusion, unknown outcome
5. **Risk for Magnesium Toxicity** r/t therapeutic magnesium sulfate infusion
