## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **UTI in the elderly presents atypically** — confusion, not burning or frequency, is often the first and only sign
2. **Polypharmacy caused her UTI** — oxybutynin + loratadine both have anticholinergic effects that caused urinary retention, which led to infection
3. **Geriatric safety** — an 85-year-old who is confused is at extreme fall and injury risk

The cause-and-effect chain to understand:

```
Oxybutynin + Loratadine (anticholinergic effects)
        ↓
   Urinary Retention
        ↓
   Bacterial Growth → UTI
        ↓
   Systemic Infection
        ↓
   Acute Confusion + Fall Risk + Dehydration
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are risk factors for developing a UTI in the elderly population?

- **Female sex** — shorter urethra, closer proximity to the rectum, lower estrogen levels post-menopause cause urethral changes
- **Advanced age** — weakened bladder muscle function, decreased immune response
- **Urinary retention** — allows bacteria to multiply in stagnant urine
- **Catheter use** — allows migration of organisms into the bladder, disrupts normal urine flushing action
- **Polypharmacy/anticholinergic medications** — oxybutynin and loratadine caused urinary retention in Bernadette
- **Incontinence** — increases exposure of perineal skin to bacteria
- **Frequent healthcare facility exposure** — increases exposure to resistant organisms
- **Dehydration** — concentrated urine is more hospitable to bacteria
- **History of prior UTIs** — increases susceptibility to recurrence

---

### Q2. What are the different manifestations between upper and lower urinary tract infections?

**Lower UTI (cystitis — bladder/urethra):**
- Dysuria (painful or burning urination)
- Urinary frequency and urgency
- Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
- Hematuria (blood in urine)
- Suprapubic discomfort or pressure
- In the elderly: these classic symptoms may be absent; confusion, lethargy, or falls may be the only sign

**Upper UTI (pyelonephritis — kidneys):**
- Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
- Chills and rigors
- Flank or lower back pain
- Nausea and vomiting
- All symptoms of lower UTI may also be present
- More serious — can progress to urosepsis

> **Key point:** Bernadette's confusion is an atypical sign of a lower UTI. Upper UTI is more serious and must be ruled out.

---

### Q3. How do we prevent UTIs in the elderly population?

1. **Drink plenty of fluids** — especially water, to flush bacteria from the urinary tract
2. **Proper perineal hygiene** — wipe front to back; hand hygiene before and after peri care
3. **Respond promptly to toileting needs** — do not let the patient hold urine
4. **Avoid unnecessary catheters** — remove as soon as clinically possible
5. **Limit anticholinergic medications** — medications like oxybutynin and loratadine contribute to retention
6. **Avoid bladder irritants** — caffeine, alcohol, carbonated beverages
7. **Review medications regularly** — apply Beers Criteria to identify potentially harmful drugs in the elderly
8. **Encourage regular voiding schedule** — every 2–3 hours during the day
9. **Avoid incontinence products unless needed** — change promptly if used, toilet as quickly as possible

---

### Q4. What other problems can occur if a UTI is not treated promptly?

**1. Pyelonephritis (kidney infection):**
- Bacteria ascend from the bladder to the kidneys
- Symptoms include high fever, chills, flank/back pain, nausea, vomiting
- Can cause permanent kidney damage

**2. Urosepsis / Sepsis:**
- Bacteria enter the bloodstream from the urinary tract
- Life-threatening systemic infection
- Signs: HR > 90, RR > 20, temp > 38°C or < 36°C, altered mental status, decreased urine output
- Can lead to septic shock and death if not treated immediately

**3. Chronic kidney disease:**
- Repeated or untreated kidney infections can cause scarring and long-term impaired kidney function

**4. Worsening confusion/delirium:**
- In elderly patients, untreated infection deepens confusion, increases fall risk, and can lead to permanent cognitive decline

---

### Q5. What is the plan of care as a nurse for a confused patient with a UTI?

1. **Ensure patient safety** — lowest bed position, siderails up, call light within reach, non-slip socks, fall precautions sign
2. **Reorient gently and frequently** — use patient's name, tell them where they are, explain everything you do
3. **Administer ordered medications** — Levofloxacin IV (antibiotic), Acetaminophen (comfort), Normal Saline IV (hydration/rehydration)
4. **Encourage fluid intake** — if able to take oral fluids
5. **Maintain catheter care** — maintain closed drainage system, keep bag below bladder, assess catheter site
6. **Monitor urine output and characteristics** — color, odor, clarity, amount
7. **Involve family** — they help with reorientation and communication
8. **Monitor vital signs** — watch for fever, tachycardia, hypotension (signs of worsening sepsis)
9. **Document thoroughly** in EHR/SimChart
10. **Reassess regularly** — evaluate response to treatment and watch for deterioration

### Q6. What are common symptoms of urinary alteration and the causes for each?

| Symptom | Cause |
|---|---|
| Dysuria | Bladder/urethral inflammation or infection |
| Urgency | Bladder irritation, UTI, overactive bladder |
| Frequency | UTI, overactive bladder, diuretics |
| Urinary retention | Anticholinergic drugs, benign prostatic hyperplasia, neurological conditions |
| Hematuria | UTI, kidney stones, trauma, cancer |
| Incontinence | UTI, weakened sphincter, neurological impairment |
| Cloudy/malodorous urine | Infection, concentrated urine, dehydration |
| Confusion in elderly | Often the only presenting sign of UTI in older adults |

### Q7. What are common diagnostic tests of the urinary tract and nursing considerations for each?

**1. Urinalysis (UA):**
- Analyzes chemical and physical properties of urine
- Nursing considerations: Collect midstream clean-catch; send promptly to lab; note color, clarity, odor

**2. Urine Culture and Sensitivity:**
- Identifies the specific organism and which antibiotics are effective
- Nursing considerations: Use sterile technique; send before starting antibiotics if possible

**3. Bladder Scan (Ultrasound):**
- Measures post-void residual urine volume
- Nursing considerations: Patient may need a full bladder; do not schedule within 24 hours of IV pyelogram

**4. CT Scan / Intravenous Pyelogram (IVP):**
- Identifies structural abnormalities of the urinary tract
- Nursing considerations: Check for contrast dye allergy; patient must lie still; may feel claustrophobic; encourage fluids after to flush dye

**5. Renal Bladder Ultrasound:**
- Imaging using sound waves; identifies masses, obstruction, hydronephrosis, calculi
- Nursing considerations: Non-invasive; no radiation; verify preprocedural orders

### Q8. What assessment data is necessary to ask a patient with a suspected UTI?

- "Which problems are you having with passing urine?"
- "Do you feel like you are completely emptying your bladder?"
- "Does it hurt or burn when you urinate?"
- "How often are you urinating?"
- "Have you noticed any blood in your urine?"
- "Do you have any pain in your lower abdomen, back, or sides?"
- "Have you had a UTI before?"
- "What medications are you currently taking?" (screen for anticholinergics)
- "How much fluid are you drinking each day?"
- For confused patients — direct questions to family/caregiver as needed

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
- Perform **hand hygiene** before touching the patient
- Introduce yourself: "Hello, Mrs. Jackson, my name is ___, I am your nurse today."
- Verify patient identity with **two identifiers** (name + date of birth or wristband)
- Conduct **environmental safety assessment (SWIPE)**

### SWIPE Safety Assessment
- **S** — Siderails up (2–3 rails, appropriate for patient)
- **W** — Wristband on and matches patient information
- **I** — IV lines — check site, patency, rate, and label
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach
- **E** — Equipment — bed in lowest position, non-skid socks on, clear path to bathroom

### Head-to-Toe Assessment (Including Focused GU)
- General appearance, level of consciousness, orientation (person, place, time, event)
- Vital signs — temperature, BP, HR, RR, SpO2
- Neurological — assess confusion level, speech, pupil response
- Cardiovascular — heart rate and rhythm (important with digoxin)
- Respiratory — lung sounds, rate, effort
- Abdomen — assess for distension, tenderness (especially suprapubic)
- GU focused assessment:
  - Urine characteristics: color, clarity, odor, amount
  - Catheter site and tubing integrity
  - Bladder distension
  - Perineal skin integrity

### Therapeutic Communication with a Confused Patient
- Use **simple, short sentences** — one idea at a time
- Speak **slowly and calmly** — do not raise your voice or rush
- **Reorient gently**: "You are in the hospital, Mrs. Jackson. You are safe."
- Use her **preferred name** throughout
- Maintain **eye contact** and approach from the front
- **Explain every action** before you do it: "I am going to check your blood pressure now."
- **Do not argue** with the patient if disoriented
- Include **family** in communication and the care plan when present

### Medication Administration — Rights of Medication
1. Right **Patient** — verify with two identifiers
2. Right **Drug** — check MAR against medication label
3. Right **Dose** — verify dosage range (see below)
4. Right **Route** — IV, PO, etc.
5. Right **Time** — check frequency and last administration
6. Right **Reason** — know why the patient is receiving this medication
7. Right **Documentation** — chart in SimChart immediately after

### Delegation
**Can delegate to UAP/CNA:**
- Measuring and recording urine output
- Assisting with ADLs (bathing, feeding)
- Repositioning and ambulation assistance
- Emptying urinary catheter drainage bag
- Recording vital signs (after initial assessment by RN)

**Cannot delegate:**
- Assessment and clinical judgment
- Medication administration
- Patient/family education
- IV line management
- SBAR communication to provider

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Bernadette's urinalysis shows a urine specific gravity of 1.03. What does this indicate?

A specific gravity of 1.03 indicates **concentrated urine**, which is a sign of **dehydration**. Normal specific gravity is 1.005–1.030. At the upper end of or above the normal range, the kidneys are retaining water because the body is dehydrated. This is consistent with Bernadette's presentation — her UTI and urinary retention, combined with inadequate fluid intake, have caused dehydration. Treatment with IV Normal Saline addresses this.

---

### Q2. Bernadette has started to exhibit confusion. Which statement is most likely correct regarding her mental status change?

Confusion in elderly patients is a classic and often the **only** presenting symptom of a UTI. Unlike younger adults who experience burning and urgency, older adults frequently present with sudden-onset confusion, agitation, lethargy, or falls without the classic urinary symptoms. This is because aging-related changes in the immune system result in a blunted inflammatory response. The UTI-related infection causes systemic changes that affect brain function, leading to **acute delirium** superimposed on baseline cognition.

---

### Q3. The nurse recognizes the catheter will be used short-term. Which catheter type is most appropriate?

For **short-term use**, an **intermittent (straight) catheter** is preferred over an indwelling (Foley) catheter because it:
- Reduces the risk of catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI)
- Allows normal voiding to resume between catheterizations
- Minimizes bacterial colonization

However, if continuous drainage monitoring is needed (as in Bernadette's case with urinary retention and monitoring of output), a short-term **indwelling Foley catheter** may be placed initially, with the goal of early removal.

---

### Q4. Review the indications for each type of catheterization.

**Intermittent/Straight catheter:**
- Urinary retention (to drain the bladder once)
- Obtaining a sterile urine specimen
- Prolonged immobilization
- Bladder training programs

**Indwelling/Foley catheter:**
- Urinary retention requiring continuous drainage
- Undergoing surgery
- Accurate monitoring of urine output (critically ill patients)
- Urinary incontinence causing skin breakdown
- Bladder outlet obstruction

**Suprapubic catheter:**
- Urethral trauma or obstruction
- Post-urological surgery
- Long-term urinary retention when urethral catheterization is not feasible
- Urinary incontinence with urethral complications

---

### Q5. What steps can be taken in catheter care to prevent worsening of Bernadette's infection?

1. **Maintain a closed drainage system** at all times — never disconnect tubing unnecessarily
2. **Keep the drainage bag below bladder level** — prevents backflow of urine
3. **Do not allow the tubing to kink or loop** — ensures unobstructed urine flow
4. **Perform perineal/catheter care** with soap and water daily and after bowel movements — clean away from the meatus
5. **Secure the catheter** to the thigh to prevent tugging and urethral trauma
6. **Empty the drainage bag regularly** (when 2/3 full) using a separate container for each patient; avoid the spigot touching the container
7. **Monitor for signs of CAUTI** — fever, new onset confusion, cloudy urine, pain at catheter site
8. **Reassess daily** for need of catheter — remove as soon as clinically possible

---

### Q6. What specific risk factors does Bernadette have for UTI? Explain how each contributed.

1. **Female sex** — Women have shorter urethras (approximately 4 cm) and the urethral meatus is closer to the rectum, making it easier for bacteria (especially E. coli) to enter the urinary tract.

2. **Advanced age (85 years)** — Aging causes bladder muscle weakness, decreased immune response, and reduced ability to completely empty the bladder, all of which increase UTI susceptibility.

3. **Oxybutynin use** — This anticholinergic medication relaxes bladder smooth muscle, which in Bernadette caused urinary retention. Stagnant urine became a breeding ground for bacteria.

4. **Loratadine (OTC antihistamine) use** — Though milder, loratadine also has anticholinergic properties that compounded the effect of oxybutynin, worsening retention.

5. **Urinary retention** — The 620 mL drained on catheterization confirms significant retention. Bacteria thrive in retained urine, and the normal flushing action of voiding was lost.

6. **Catheter use** — Once placed, a urinary catheter introduces a pathway for bacteria to migrate into the bladder, increasing CAUTI risk.

---

### Q7. Bernadette has mixed urinary incontinence. Which statement indicates an accurate understanding?

**Mixed urinary incontinence** is the combination of both **stress incontinence** and **urge incontinence**:
- **Stress incontinence** — involuntary leakage during periods of increased abdominal pressure (coughing, sneezing, lifting)
- **Urge incontinence** — involuntary loss of urine after a sudden, strong urge to void

The correct understanding is: *"The patient's incontinence results from involuntary leakage during periods of abdominal pressure AND the involuntary loss of urine after a strong urge to void."*

---

### Q8. What vital signs must be assessed before giving digoxin or a beta-blocker?

Before administering **digoxin** or a **beta-blocker**, assess:
1. **Blood pressure** — hold if systolic BP < 90 mmHg (or per facility policy)
2. **Heart rate (apical pulse)** — hold digoxin if apical HR < 60 bpm; hold beta-blocker if HR < 60 bpm
3. **Respiratory rate** — hold beta-blocker if RR is significantly abnormal
4. **Digoxin level** (when available) — therapeutic range is 0.5–2.0 ng/mL; toxicity occurs above 2.0 ng/mL

> **Clinical note:** Dehydration increases the risk of digoxin toxicity. Bernadette's dehydration makes checking her digoxin level especially critical before administration.

### Q9. Complete the ADPIE/Clinical Judgment Template for Bernadette Jackson

**Recognition of Cues (Assessment Data):**
- *Subjective:* Patient reports weakness, lethargy, and abdominal discomfort
- *Objective:* Acute confusion, cloudy and foul-smelling urine, 620 mL drained by catheter, urinalysis positive for bacteria, specific gravity 1.03
- *Historical:* Started oxybutynin 1 week ago; self-started loratadine 5 days ago; history of urinary incontinence; allergy to Penicillin

**Priority Nursing Diagnosis:**
Impaired Urinary Elimination related to urinary tract infection and urinary retention, as evidenced by cloudy/foul-smelling urine, acute confusion, urinary retention with 620 mL drained, weakness, and lethargy.

**SMART Goal:**
Patient will demonstrate improved urinary elimination, as evidenced by clear, yellow, non-foul-smelling urine output of at least 30 mL/hour and a decrease in confusion within 24 hours of initiating antibiotic therapy.

**Nursing Interventions (Generate Solutions):**
1. Administer Levofloxacin 500 mg IV every 12 hours as ordered — to treat the underlying bacterial infection causing UTI
2. Administer 0.9% Normal Saline IV as ordered — to rehydrate the patient, dilute and flush bacteria from the urinary tract, and restore circulating volume
3. Maintain closed indwelling catheter system and perform daily catheter care — to promote drainage, prevent CAUTI, and monitor urine output

**Rationale (Take Action):**
- Levofloxacin is a fluoroquinolone antibiotic effective against common UTI pathogens including E. coli; IV route is used for serious infection and when the patient cannot reliably take oral medications (Potter & Perry, 2021)
- IV Normal Saline restores hydration and supports renal perfusion; adequate hydration helps flush bacteria from the urinary tract (Potter & Perry, 2021)
- Maintaining a closed catheter system and performing sterile perineal care reduces CAUTI risk; monitoring output allows evaluation of treatment response (Potter & Perry, 2021)

**Evaluate Outcomes:**
- Urine output ≥ 30 mL/hour, urine becoming clear and yellow
- Patient reports decreased abdominal discomfort
- Confusion improving — patient more oriented to person, place, time
- Vital signs stable — no fever, no tachycardia
- No signs of sepsis progression

### Q10. What are 3 patient education points for UTI prevention?

1. **Drink plenty of fluids**, especially water (aim for 6–8 glasses/day), to flush bacteria from the urinary tract
2. **Wipe front to back** after using the toilet and practice proper perineal hygiene; perform hand hygiene before and after perineal care
3. **Urinate regularly** and completely empty the bladder — do not hold urine; if a catheter is in use, discuss the plan for removal with the provider as early as possible

## 💊 POLYPHARMACY & BEERS CRITERIA

### What is Polypharmacy?
The concurrent use of **5 or more medications**. Bernadette exemplifies polypharmacy — she takes digoxin, oxybutynin, loratadine, and other medications simultaneously.

**Risk factors for polypharmacy:**
- Multiple chronic conditions
- Multiple prescribers and pharmacies
- Use of OTC medications without provider knowledge
- Transitions of care (hospital to home)

**Problems caused by polypharmacy:**
- Drug-drug interactions
- Increased fall risk
- Cognitive impairment
- Adverse drug events
- Higher risk of mortality

### What is Beers Criteria?
The **American Geriatrics Society (AGS) Beers Criteria** is a list of medications considered **potentially inappropriate for adults age 65 and older** due to increased risk of harm.

**Key drugs on the Beers List relevant to Bernadette:**

| Medication | Why It's on Beers List |
|---|---|
| **Oxybutynin** | High anticholinergic burden → confusion, urinary retention in elderly |
| **Digoxin** | Narrow therapeutic window; toxicity risk increases with dehydration or renal impairment |
| **First-gen antihistamines (e.g., diphenhydramine)** | High anticholinergic burden; note loratadine is 2nd-gen but still has mild anticholinergic effects |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Increased fall risk, cognitive impairment |
| **NSAIDs** | Increased GI bleeding, kidney injury risk in elderly |

**How nurses help prevent polypharmacy problems:**
- Perform thorough **medication reconciliation** during all transitions of care
- Identify anticholinergic burden using tools like the Beers Criteria
- Assess patient understanding of all medications including OTC drugs
- Collaborate with the interdisciplinary team to deprescribe when appropriate

## ⚠️ SEPSIS — Know When to Escalate

A UTI not treated promptly can progress to:

**Pyelonephritis → Urosepsis → Septic Shock**

**Signs of sepsis — watch for these in Bernadette:**
- Heart rate > 90 bpm
- Respiratory rate > 20
- Temperature > 38°C (100.4°F) or < 36°C (96.8°F)
- Worsening confusion / altered mental status
- Decreased urine output despite catheter
- Hypotension (SBP < 90 mmHg)

> If you see these signs, **escalate immediately using SBAR**.
