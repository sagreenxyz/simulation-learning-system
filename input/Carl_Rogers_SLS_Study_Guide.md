## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes**:

1. **Diabetic wound care** — correct technique for assessing and dressing a Stage II non-healing foot ulcer

3. **Patient education** — teaching Carl about wound care, blood glucose monitoring, and diabetic foot care
4. **Social determinants of health** — Carl's social circumstances (financial barriers, housing instability in some versions) affect his ability to manage diabetes at home

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Compare and contrast the onset, peak, and duration of insulin types.

| Insulin Type | Examples | Onset | Peak | Duration | Key Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rapid-acting** | Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (Novolog), Glulisine (Apidra) | 15–30 min | 30–90 min | 3–5 hrs | Given within 15 min of a meal |
| **Short-acting (Regular)** | Humulin R, Novolin R | 30–60 min | 2–5 hrs | 5–8 hrs | Given 30–45 min before a meal; more hypoglycemia risk |
| **Intermediate-acting** | NPH (Humulin N, Novolin N) | 1–2 hrs | 4–12 hrs | 14–24 hrs | Cloudy; roll to mix (don't shake) |
| **Long-acting** | Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) | 1–1.5 hrs | Little to no peak | 20–24 hrs | Once daily; do NOT mix with other insulins |
| **Ultra long-acting** | Degludec (Tresiba) | 6 hrs | No peak | 42 hrs | Once daily |

> **High-alert medication:** Insulin is a HIGH ALERT medication — always require independent double-check with another nurse before administration.

---

### Q2. What are the rules for mixing insulins?

When mixing **NPH (cloudy)** with **Regular (clear)** insulin:
1. Draw up the **clear (regular) insulin FIRST** — prevents contamination of clear with cloudy
2. Then draw up the **cloudy (NPH) insulin**
3. Mnemonic: **"Clear before cloudy"**

**Rationale:** If NPH enters the regular insulin vial, it contaminates the entire vial, altering the action of the regular insulin.

> **Never mix long-acting insulins (glargine, detemir)** with any other insulin — they must be given separately.

### Q3. Describe best practices for wound care of a diabetic foot ulcer.

**Assessment:**
- Assess wound stage, size (LxWxD), color, exudate (amount, type), odor, surrounding skin
- A Stage II wound = partial-thickness skin loss involving dermis and/or epidermis; shallow open ulcer or intact/ruptured blister
- Check for signs of infection: redness, warmth, swelling, purulent drainage, fever, elevated WBC
- Assess perfusion to the foot: pulses (pedal, posterior tibial), capillary refill, skin temperature and color
- Check for osteomyelitis signs in deep or chronic wounds

**Wound Care Technique:**
1. **Premedicate** — administer analgesic 5–10 minutes BEFORE wound care (e.g., acetaminophen or hydrocodone per order)
2. Perform hand hygiene; don gloves
3. Remove old dressing gently — moisten if adhered
4. Assess wound characteristics
5. **Irrigate** wound with normal saline (room temperature)
6. Pat wound bed gently with sterile gauze
7. Apply appropriate dressing per wound stage and provider order (moist wound environment promotes healing; too wet or too dry delays healing)
8. Secure dressing without constricting circulation
9. **Offload pressure** — ensure no pressure is placed on the ulcer
10. Document wound assessment

**Offloading pressure is critical** — this is the most important intervention for diabetic foot ulcers.

### Q4. What dietary teaching should the nurse provide for Type II Diabetes?

**The ABC's of Diabetes Teaching:**
- **A — A1C:** Goal < 7% for most patients; reflects average blood glucose over 2–3 months
- **B — Blood pressure:** Goal < 130/80 mmHg
- **C — Cholesterol (LDL):** Goal < 100 mg/dL (or < 70 in high cardiovascular risk)

**Dietary guidance:**
- Consistent carbohydrate intake at each meal — carbs directly affect blood glucose
- Emphasize: non-starchy vegetables (broccoli, greens, tomatoes), fruits, whole grains, lean protein, healthy fats
- Avoid: fried foods, saturated/trans fats, added sugars, sugary beverages, excessive alcohol
- 30 minutes of moderate exercise most days (e.g., walking)
- Regular blood glucose monitoring
- Foot inspection DAILY — especially in patients with neuropathy (Carl may not feel wounds developing)

### Q5. What signs and symptoms differentiate hypoglycemia from hyperglycemia?

| | **Hypoglycemia** (Low BG < 70 mg/dL) | **Hyperglycemia** (High BG > 180 mg/dL) |
|---|---|---|
| **Symptoms** | Shakiness, diaphoresis, confusion, dizziness, tachycardia, headache, pale skin | Thirst, polyuria, polydipsia, dry skin, fatigue, blurred vision, fruity breath (DKA) |
| **Onset** | Rapid | Gradual |
| **Treatment** | 15 g fast-acting carbs (15/15 rule); glucagon or D50W if unconscious | Insulin per sliding scale; IV fluids; monitor electrolytes and ketones |
| **Carl's risk** | At risk any time insulin is given; eating disrupted | Currently hyperglycemic (BG in 300s) |

> **15/15 Rule for mild hypoglycemia:** Give 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates (4 oz juice, glucose tablets), wait 15 minutes, recheck BG. If still < 70, repeat.

### Q6. What is the correct technique for subcutaneous insulin injection?

1. Verify provider's order (insulin type, dose, time)
2. Perform blood glucose check before administering insulin
3. Verify insulin with a second nurse (HIGH ALERT medication) per facility policy
4. If mixing insulins: draw clear before cloudy
5. Check insulin vial: correct name, expiration date, appearance
6. Gently roll cloudy (NPH) insulin — never shake
7. Perform hand hygiene; don gloves
8. Select injection site: **abdomen** (fastest absorption), upper arm, thigh, buttock
9. Rotate sites systematically — avoid same site repeatedly
10. Clean site with alcohol swab; allow to dry
11. Pinch skin (for thin patients); insert at **90° angle** (45° if very thin)
12. Inject insulin slowly; wait a few seconds before withdrawing needle
13. Do **NOT** massage injection site (alters absorption)
14. Dispose of needle in sharps container
15. Document: insulin type, dose, site, time, blood glucose reading

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. Carl Rogers' heel ulcer is Stage II. Which description accurately describes this?

**Partial-thickness skin loss involving the dermis, epidermis, or both.** The wound bed is viable, pink or red. May present as a shallow open ulcer or intact/ruptured serum-filled blister. No slough or bruising is present.

---

### Q2. When mixing NPH and regular insulin, why is regular insulin drawn first?

To **prevent contamination of the regular insulin vial with NPH (cloudy) insulin.** If NPH enters the regular insulin vial, it permanently alters the action time of that vial.

---

### Q3. Analgesic administration before wound care — when?

**5–10 minutes before** beginning potentially painful wound care treatments. This ensures the medication is at peak effect when the painful procedure begins.

---

### Q4. What teaching topics should the nurse address for Carl's wound?

- Signs and symptoms of infection to report (redness, warmth, swelling, pus, fever, increased pain)
- Daily foot inspection (use a mirror if needed for the bottom of the feet)
- Do NOT soak feet — soaking softens skin and increases infection risk
- Appropriate footwear — no open-toed shoes; well-fitting diabetic shoes
- Keep blood glucose in target range — hyperglycemia impairs wound healing
- Keep wound clean and dressed per instructions
- Eating a nutrient-rich diet promotes healing (protein, vitamin C, zinc)
- Handwashing before and after wound care

## 💊 KEY INSULIN FACTS FOR SIMULATION

### NPH Insulin (Cloudy — Intermediate-acting)
- Must be **rolled** to resuspend — never shaken
- **Draw up LAST** when mixing with regular
- Administered SQ; site rotation required

### Insulin Lispro (Humalog) — Rapid-acting
- Give within 15 minutes of a meal
- Fastest onset — patient must have food ready before or immediately after injection
- Never mix with long-acting insulins

### HIGH ALERT — Insulin Safety
- Always double-check with another nurse
- Verify: right patient, right insulin type, right dose, right time, right route
- Check expiration date and appearance
- Keep rapid-acting insulins available at mealtime
- Monitor for hypoglycemia after administration
