## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **Acute pancreatitis management** — pain (often severe), NPO status, bowel rest, fluid replacement, and monitoring for complications
2. **Alcohol withdrawal** — when a chronic alcohol user suddenly stops drinking, withdrawal begins within 6–24 hours and can progress to life-threatening seizures (delirium tremens); managed with the CIWA-Ar tool and benzodiazepines
3. **Therapeutic communication** — working with a patient who is addicted to alcohol requires non-judgmental, empathetic communication; addressing the patient's substance use sensitively

The dual pathology:

```
Chronic Alcohol Abuse
        ↓
Pancreatitis: alcohol damages pancreatic acinar cells →
              premature activation of digestive enzymes →
              autodigestion of the pancreas
        ↓
Alcoholic Cirrhosis: chronic liver damage → scarring → portal hypertension
        ↓
Admitted to hospital → Alcohol intake STOPPED abruptly
        ↓
Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome begins within 6–24 hours:
  • Stage 1 (6–24h): Anxiety, tremor, diaphoresis, tachycardia
  • Stage 2 (24–48h): Hallucinations, confusion
  • Stage 3 (48–72h): Seizures, delirium tremens (LIFE-THREATENING)
        ↓
CIWA-Ar assessment → Lorazepam to prevent withdrawal progression
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the signs and symptoms of acute pancreatitis?

**Classic Presentation:**
- **Abdominal pain** — hallmark symptom; severe, steady, boring pain in the epigastric region or left upper quadrant; often radiates to the back; worsens with eating; may improve slightly when leaning forward
- **Nausea and vomiting** — common; does NOT relieve pain (distinguishes from other causes)
- **Abdominal tenderness** — diffuse epigastric tenderness; guarding
- **Fever** — low-grade initially; high fever suggests complications (abscess, necrosis)
- **Tachycardia** — from pain and fluid losses
- **Hypotension** — if severe third-spacing of fluid into retroperitoneum
- **Abdominal distension** — ileus from inflammation
- **Absent or hypoactive bowel sounds** — paralytic ileus

**Severe Signs (late/complications):**
- **Cullen's sign** — periumbilical ecchymosis (hemorrhagic pancreatitis)
- **Grey Turner's sign** — flank ecchymosis
- **Jaundice** — if bile duct obstruction (especially in Janet with cirrhosis)
- Signs of organ failure (renal failure, ARDS)

**Lab Values:**
- **Amylase** elevated (> 3x upper limit of normal)
- **Lipase** elevated (more specific than amylase)
- **WBC** elevated (inflammation)
- **BMP** — hyperglycemia (beta cell damage), hypocalcemia (fat saponification)
- **LFTs** — elevated if liver involved (especially in Janet with cirrhosis)
- **Hematocrit** — elevated if hemoconcentration (third-spacing)

### Q2. What are the clinical manifestations of pancreatitis?

*(Expanded from Q1 above)*

**Acute Pancreatitis vs. Chronic Pancreatitis:**

**Acute pancreatitis:**
- Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain radiating to the back
- Nausea/vomiting, fever
- Elevated amylase and lipase
- Pain is the dominant symptom

**Chronic pancreatitis:**
- Recurrent episodes of mild-to-moderate abdominal pain (often after alcohol use or eating)
- Upper abdominal pain worse after eating
- Steatorrhea (fatty stools) — malabsorption from pancreatic enzyme deficiency
- Weight loss
- Diabetes mellitus (from beta cell destruction)
- Malnutrition

---

### Q3. What is alcoholic cirrhosis and how is it related to pancreatitis?

**Alcoholic Cirrhosis:**
- Chronic, progressive liver disease resulting from prolonged alcohol abuse
- Alcohol directly damages hepatocytes → inflammation → fibrosis → scarring (cirrhosis)
- The scarring replaces functional liver tissue → impaired liver function
- Leads to: portal hypertension, esophageal varices, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, coagulopathy, impaired drug metabolism

**Relationship to Pancreatitis:**
- Prolonged alcohol use causes both alcoholic cirrhosis AND pancreatitis (through different mechanisms)
- Alcohol causes increased, viscous pancreatic secretions that plug small pancreatic ducts
- Alcohol also causes premature activation of digestive enzymes within acinar cells → autodigestion
- Coexistence of pancreatitis and alcoholic cirrhosis is serious — both organs are compromised
- Janet's cirrhosis affects how she metabolizes medications (especially lorazepam — hepatic metabolism)

---

### Q4. What is the focus of care for patients with pancreatitis?

1. **Pain management** — adequate analgesia (morphine); patient comfort is a priority
2. **NPO (bowel rest)** — the pancreas needs rest; no oral intake; NG tube if vomiting is severe
3. **IV fluid replacement** — aggressive fluid resuscitation; third-spacing causes significant losses
4. **Monitoring for complications:**
   - Respiratory: ARDS, pleural effusion (position HOB 30–45°)
   - Cardiac: Shock from fluid losses
   - Renal: AKI from hypotension and inflammation
   - Metabolic: Hyperglycemia, hypocalcemia
5. **Nutritional support** — enteral nutrition (jejunal feeding) if prolonged NPO; TPN if jejunal access not possible
6. **Treat the underlying cause** — alcohol abuse counseling; ERCP if gallstones
7. **Alcohol withdrawal prevention and management** — CIWA-Ar assessment and benzodiazepines

---

### Q5. What is the CIWA-Ar tool and how is it used?

**CIWA-Ar = Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol, Revised**

This is a validated 10-item scale used to assess the severity of alcohol withdrawal syndrome (AWS) and guide benzodiazepine treatment.

**10 Items Assessed (each scored 0–7 except one scored 0–4):**
1. Nausea/vomiting
2. Tremor
3. Paroxysmal sweats
4. Anxiety
5. Agitation
6. Tactile disturbances (formication — "bugs crawling on skin")
7. Auditory disturbances (hallucinations)
8. Visual disturbances
9. Headache/fullness in head
10. Orientation/clouding of sensorium (scored 0–4)

**CIWA-Ar Score Interpretation:**
- **Score < 8:** Mild withdrawal — monitor closely; may not need medication
- **Score 8–14:** Moderate withdrawal — start lorazepam per protocol
- **Score ≥ 15:** Severe withdrawal — aggressive lorazepam; risk for seizures, delirium tremens

**How nurses use it:**
- Assess CIWA-Ar every 1–4 hours per protocol
- If score meets threshold (e.g., ≥ 8 or ≥ 10 per facility), administer lorazepam
- Continue monitoring until score < 8 for 24 hours or protocol complete
- Seizure precautions must be in place for all patients in withdrawal

---

### Q6. What is the alcohol withdrawal timeline?

| Time After Last Drink | Signs/Symptoms | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| 6–24 hours | Anxiety, tremors, insomnia, tachycardia, diaphoresis, hypertension | Mild |
| 12–48 hours | Hallucinations (visual, auditory, tactile — "bugs crawling") — patient may know they are not real | Moderate |
| 24–48 hours | Seizures (generalized tonic-clonic) — peak risk | Severe |
| 48–72 hours | **Delirium tremens (DTs)** — agitation, confusion, hallucinations, fever, severe autonomic instability, seizures | Life-threatening (mortality 5–15% if untreated) |

**Janet's risk:** She was admitted for pancreatitis. Her alcohol intake stopped abruptly at admission. If she is a regular, heavy drinker, she is at HIGH RISK for alcohol withdrawal and must be assessed with CIWA-Ar immediately upon admission.

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Two Possible Scenarios

**Scenario Version 1 (Pancreatitis pain/nausea):**
- Assess pain: location, severity, character, radiation to back
- Position: Semi-Fowler's or leaning forward (reduces pancreatic tension)
- Administer morphine for pain per order
- Administer ondansetron for nausea
- Ensure NPO status maintained
- IV fluids running per order (½NS + multivitamins)

**Scenario Version 2 (Alcohol withdrawal — patient removes IV):**
- Patient has removed IV — immediate priorities: safety, IV reestablishment
- Assess CIWA-Ar score — document and compare to threshold
- Do NOT restrain without order and exhausting alternatives
- Apply seizure precautions: padded bed rails, suction available, O2 available
- Administer lorazepam per CIWA-Ar score per protocol
- Reduce stimulation: quiet environment, dim lights
- Establish new IV access
- Reorient patient calmly

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails padded (seizure precautions) — up
- **W** — Wristband — verify identity
- **I** — IV: patient may have removed it — reestablish; check remaining IV site
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach
- **E** — Equipment: suction at bedside; oral airway available; O2 available (seizure precautions)

### Therapeutic Communication — Alcohol Abuse
Non-judgmental, empathetic communication is essential:
- **Never shame or lecture:** "That's what happens when you drink too much"
- **Use therapeutic statements:** "It sounds like alcohol has become something you really rely on. We want to help you get through this safely."
- **Be factual about withdrawal:** "Your body has gotten used to alcohol and now that it's stopped suddenly, it's reacting. We have medicine to help with that."
- **Address pain non-judgmentally:** Pain from pancreatitis is REAL regardless of the cause
- **Refer to social work/addiction counselor** — not the nurse's role to deliver addiction treatment

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Morphine Sulfate — IV
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Opioid analgesic — Schedule II |
| **Mechanism** | Binds mu-opioid receptors → reduces pain signal transmission |
| **Indication** | Severe abdominal pain from acute pancreatitis |
| **Adverse effects** | Respiratory depression ⚠️, sedation, constipation, N/V, hypotension |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor RR and sedation; naloxone at bedside; assess pain q30–60 min after dose; note: some older textbooks advise against morphine for pancreatitis (concern about sphincter of Oddi spasm — current evidence supports morphine use) |

---

### Lorazepam (Ativan)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Benzodiazepine — Schedule IV |
| **Mechanism** | Enhances GABA-A receptor activity → CNS depression → reduces withdrawal severity |
| **Indication** | Alcohol withdrawal management; dose guided by CIWA-Ar score |
| **Antidote** | Flumazenil (Romazicon) |
| **Onset** | IV: 20–30 min / Peak: 2 hours / Duration: 6–8 hours |
| **Adverse effects** | Respiratory depression ⚠️, sedation, confusion, addiction, memory impairment |
| **Hold criteria** | RR < 10; excessive sedation; withdrawal symptoms have resolved (score < 8) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor respiratory status; assess CIWA-Ar before each dose; watch for paradoxical excitation; hepatic metabolism — caution in Janet with cirrhosis |
| **Patient teaching** | This medication prevents dangerous withdrawal symptoms; report feeling excessively drowsy or having difficulty breathing |

---

### Ondansetron (Zofran)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | 5-HT3 serotonin receptor antagonist; antiemetic |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks serotonin receptors in GI tract and chemoreceptor trigger zone → reduces nausea |
| **Indication** | Nausea/vomiting from pancreatitis and opioid use |
| **Adverse effects** | Headache, constipation, QT prolongation (cardiac monitoring if high doses) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess bowel function; monitor ECG if QT prolongation concern |

---

### ½ Normal Saline with Multivitamins
- **Rationale:** Alcoholic patients are commonly deficient in B vitamins, especially **thiamine (Vitamin B1)**
- **Thiamine deficiency + IV glucose** without thiamine first can precipitate **Wernicke's encephalopathy** (confusion, ataxia, ophthalmoplegia) → if untreated → **Korsakoff's syndrome** (permanent amnesia)
- **Always administer thiamine BEFORE or with glucose in alcoholic patients**

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Acute Pain** r/t pancreatic inflammation and autodigestion secondary to pancreatitis
   - AEB: epigastric pain, pain radiating to back, guarding, elevated amylase/lipase

2. **Risk for Alcohol Withdrawal Syndrome** r/t abrupt cessation of alcohol intake during hospitalization
   - AEB: history of chronic alcohol abuse, alcoholic cirrhosis, CIWA-Ar monitoring required

3. **Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements** r/t NPO status, nausea, and chronic alcohol use
   - AEB: NPO, weight loss, B-vitamin deficiency risk

4. **Risk for Injury (Seizures)** r/t alcohol withdrawal progression
   - AEB: alcohol abuse history, hospitalization without alcohol, CIWA-Ar monitoring needed

5. **Ineffective Denial** r/t substance use disorder and minimization of alcohol problem
   - AEB: social history indicating chronic alcohol abuse; may resist addiction counseling
