## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is cellulitis? Describe the pathophysiology.

**Definition:** Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the skin and underlying soft tissues (dermis and subcutaneous tissue). It most commonly affects the lower extremities but can occur anywhere, including the arms (as in Kyle's case from a puncture wound).

**Pathophysiology:**
- Bacteria (most commonly *Staphylococcus aureus* or *Streptococcus pyogenes*) enter through a break in the skin (puncture, cut, bite, etc.)
- Once inside the dermis, bacteria multiply and trigger an **inflammatory response**
- Inflammatory mediators cause vasodilation → warmth, redness, and swelling
- Tissue edema and WBC infiltration cause pain and tenderness
- If untreated, infection can spread to lymph nodes (lymphangitis — red streaks) or bloodstream (bacteremia/sepsis)

**Signs and symptoms of cellulitis:**
- **Redness (erythema)** — clearly demarcated spreading area
- **Warmth** — affected skin is warm to touch
- **Swelling (edema)** — tissue edema from inflammation
- **Pain and tenderness** — localized
- **Low-grade fever** — systemic response to infection
- May progress to: lymphangitis (red streaks extending from wound), lymphadenopathy, abscess formation, bacteremia

### Q2. What are the components of a complete nursing assessment?

**Head-to-Toe Assessment Framework:**

**General appearance:** Level of consciousness, alertness, orientation, mood, affect; overall appearance (well-nourished, comfortable?)

**Vital signs:** Temperature, BP, HR, RR, SpO2, pain (0–10)

**Neurological:** Orientation (person, place, time, event); pupils equal and reactive to light (PERRL); speech clear; grip strength equal bilaterally

**Head, Eyes, Ears, Nose, Throat (HEENT):** Scalp, eyes (sclera, conjunctiva), ears (hearing), nose, oral mucosa (moisture), throat (redness, exudate)

**Cardiovascular:** Heart rate, rhythm (regular?); S1 and S2 present; peripheral pulses; capillary refill (< 2 sec); edema assessment

**Respiratory:** Rate and depth; lung sounds (clear, crackles, wheezes, rhonchi); SpO2; respiratory effort

**Gastrointestinal:** Abdomen soft/firm; bowel sounds all quadrants; nausea, vomiting, last BM; appetite

**Genitourinary:** Urine output, color, clarity; any difficulty voiding

**Musculoskeletal:** Range of motion; strength; gait; fall risk

**Integumentary:** Skin color, temperature, turgor, integrity; wound assessment (Kyle's forearm)

**Wound Assessment (Cellulitis Site):**
- Location and size of affected area — draw borders to track spread
- Color: redness fading (improving) or spreading?
- Warmth: decreasing (improving) or worsening?
- Drainage: any purulent discharge?
- Swelling: improving?
- Signs of lymphangitis (red streaks extending proximally)

### Q3. What are the principles for managing unit distractions during patient care?

In the simulation, the nurse is expected to handle distracting events (other staff asking for help, call lights, phones, etc.) while maintaining focus on Kyle:

**Prioritization framework:**
- Use **CURE** or **SBAR** thinking: What is most urgent and cannot be delegated?
- **Kyle's assessment and discharge preparation** are the primary task
- Interruptions should be acknowledged briefly, handled if urgent (safety issue), or delegated if possible

**Principles:**
1. **Delegate appropriately** — routine tasks (VS recheck, routine chart work) can be delegated to UAP
2. **Brief acknowledgment then refocus** — "I'll be right with you" keeps care focused on current patient
3. **Do not compromise patient safety** — never leave a vulnerable patient unattended due to a distraction
4. **HIPAA compliance** — conversations about other patients in Kyle's room violate privacy; redirect outside

### Q4. What are essential discharge teaching points for cellulitis?

Kyle is being discharged home — comprehensive teaching reduces readmission risk:

**Wound care at home:**
- Keep the wound clean and dry
- Change dressing as instructed — wash hands before and after
- Do not use hydrogen peroxide or iodine (delays healing)
- Keep the arm elevated above heart level when possible to reduce swelling
- Avoid immersing the wound in water (no swimming, soaking)

**Signs of infection worsening (Return Precautions):**
- Increasing redness, warmth, swelling, or pain
- Red streaks extending from the wound (lymphangitis — go to ER immediately)
- Fever > 101°F (38.3°C)
- Pus or increased drainage from wound
- If you feel flu-like, develop chills, or feel very unwell

**Medications:**
- Complete the full antibiotic course — even if feeling better
- Take medication at even intervals
- Do not share antibiotics
- Take oral antibiotics with food if GI upset occurs

**Prevention of future cellulitis:**
- Keep skin moisturized — dry, cracked skin is an entry point for bacteria
- Clean and cover any cuts, scrapes, or puncture wounds promptly
- Wash hands frequently
- Seek care promptly if any skin break becomes red or warm

**Follow-up:**
- Follow-up appointment with provider in [X] days
- Know when to go to the emergency department vs. calling the provider

### Q5. What is HIPAA and why is it important in nursing?

**HIPAA** (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) requires national standards to protect sensitive patient health information (PHI — Protected Health Information) from being disclosed without the patient's consent or knowledge.

**Nursing implications:**
- Never discuss patient information in public areas (hallways, elevators)
- Do not access medical records of patients not under your care
- Use minimum necessary information when sharing PHI
- Do not discuss one patient's information in another patient's room
- Social media — never post any patient information, even without names
- Violations can result in disciplinary action, loss of nursing license, or legal consequences

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Priority Task: Complete Systematic Assessment
Kyle is nearly ready to discharge — but the nurse must still complete a thorough assessment before discharge can proceed:
1. Verify patient identity (name + DOB)
2. Perform SWIPE environmental safety check
3. Head-to-toe assessment with focused wound assessment (forearm)
4. Review current vital signs and trending
5. Review medication administration record (MAR) — have all antibiotics been given?
6. Check IV site if still present — site assessment before discontinuation

### Managing Distractions (In Simulation)
When distractions occur:
- Stay calm; do not become flustered
- Address the distraction briefly: "I'll be right with you" or "Can someone else take that?"
- Return focus to Kyle — "I'm sorry about the interruption, Mr. Miller. Now let me finish checking..."
- If a safety-related interruption occurs (another patient emergency), use clinical judgment about what takes priority

### Wound Assessment for Kyle's Forearm Cellulitis
- Note: if antibiotics have been effective, you expect to see: **borders not spreading, redness fading, decreased warmth, decreased swelling, temperature normalizing**
- Mark wound borders with a marker (or note them) — compare to previous documentation
- Check for red streaks (lymphangitis) — very important
- Assess IV site (if still in place) for phlebitis, infiltration, or infection
- Plan IV discontinuation before discharge if applicable

### Discharge Communication
With Kyle:
- "Mr. Miller, I want to go over your discharge instructions to make sure you know exactly what to watch for at home."
- Use teach-back: "Can you tell me back what you should do if you see red streaks coming from the wound?"
- Ensure Kyle has prescriptions, follow-up appointment, and written discharge instructions
- Document teaching and patient's understanding

### Q1. What nursing diagnoses apply to Kyle Miller?

2. **Acute Pain** r/t cellulitis inflammation — AEB pain at wound site
3. **Risk for Infection Spread** r/t inadequate treatment or non-adherence to antibiotics
4. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t management of cellulitis, wound care, and medication adherence — (discharge focus)

### Q2. What are signs that cellulitis is spreading and requires emergency care?

- **Red streaks (lymphangitis)** extending from wound toward the trunk — URGENT
- Fever > 103°F; chills, rigors
- Rapid spread of redness
- Bullae (fluid-filled blisters) — indicates deeper infection
- Signs of sepsis (altered mental status, hypotension, tachycardia)
- Numbness or weakness in the limb

### Q3. Post-simulation quiz: Gerontologic considerations for an older adult with pneumonia

*(This question appears in post-simulation quizzes for both Kyle Miller and Wallace Peterson scenarios)*

Older adults with pneumonia present differently than younger patients:
- May NOT have classic high fever — often present with confusion, decreased functional status, or fall
- Dyspnea and decreased appetite may be the only initial signs
- Lung sounds may be less dramatic due to reduced respiratory muscle strength
- Dehydration risk is higher (decreased thirst perception)
- Drug clearance is slower — antibiotic dosing must be adjusted for renal function
- Immunization status is critical — pneumococcal and influenza vaccines recommended
