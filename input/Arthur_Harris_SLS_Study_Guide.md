## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **Hospitalized diabetic patients become hyperglycemic** — stress of hospitalization, NPO status changes, steroid use, reduced activity, and IV dextrose all disrupt glucose control
2. **Insulin administration causes hypoglycemia** — if given without adequate food intake or with a calculation error, insulin drives blood sugar dangerously low
3. **Severe hypoglycemia is an emergency** — unresponsive patient with blood sugar < 50 mg/dL requires immediate IV dextrose and rapid response

The cause-and-effect chain:

```
T2DM (home: oral antihyperglycemics) → Hospitalized for wound infection
        ↓
Hospital stress response + IV antibiotics → Hyperglycemia
        ↓
Insulin prescribed (sliding scale) to manage hyperglycemia
        ↓
Insulin given → Patient doesn't eat enough / NPO / dose too high
        ↓
Blood glucose drops critically → SEVERE HYPOGLYCEMIA
        ↓
Patient: shaky, sweaty, confused, unresponsive
        ↓
EMERGENCY: Check blood glucose → Administer D50 IV → Notify provider
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Two reasons why Type 2 Diabetic patients who are normally well-controlled on oral antihyperglycemics frequently become hyperglycemic when hospitalized.

**Reason 1: Physiological Stress Response**
Hospitalization — especially for infection (as in Arthur's case) — activates the body's stress response: cortisol, epinephrine, glucagon, and growth hormone are released. These **counter-regulatory hormones** increase hepatic glucose production and decrease insulin sensitivity (insulin resistance). The result is **stress hyperglycemia** — elevated blood glucose even without eating. This is a well-documented phenomenon that requires insulin therapy in most hospitalized patients with diabetes, even those normally controlled on oral medications.

**Reason 2: Medications and IV Fluids**
- **Corticosteroids** (if prescribed) dramatically increase blood glucose — often used in hospitalized patients for inflammation, COPD exacerbations, or other conditions
- **IV dextrose-containing fluids** (D5W, D5½NS) directly deliver glucose into the bloodstream
- **Oral antihyperglycemics** are often held in hospitalized patients due to NPO status, risk of lactic acidosis (metformin), or hemodynamic instability
- **Reduced physical activity** in hospitalized patients decreases glucose utilization

### Q2. List the symptoms (and vital sign changes) of hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia.

**Hypoglycemia (Blood glucose < 70 mg/dL):**
- **Early (adrenergic — sympathetic response):**
  - Shakiness, trembling
  - Diaphoresis (sweating — often cold/clammy)
  - Palpitations, tachycardia
  - Anxiety, nervousness
  - Pallor
  - Hunger
  - Headache
- **Late (neuroglycopenic — brain deprived of glucose):**
  - Confusion, disorientation
  - Slurred speech
  - Behavioral changes, irritability
  - Seizures
  - Loss of consciousness
  - Coma (blood glucose < 40 mg/dL)
- **Vital signs:** Tachycardia, diaphoresis; BP and RR may be relatively normal initially

**Hyperglycemia (Blood glucose > 180 mg/dL):**
- Polydipsia (excessive thirst)
- Polyuria (frequent urination)
- Polyphagia (increased hunger)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Blurred vision
- Slow wound healing
- Fruity breath (ketones in DKA)
- Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing) — in DKA
- **Vital signs:** May have tachycardia if dehydrated; Kussmaul breathing if DKA present

### Q4. Describe the pathophysiology of Type 2 Diabetes.

**Type 1 DM:** Complete autoimmune destruction of beta cells in the islets of Langerhans → absolute insulin deficiency → requires exogenous insulin for survival

**Type 2 DM (Arthur's type):**
- **Phase 1:** Insulin resistance — target cells (muscle, fat, liver) become less sensitive to insulin. The pancreas compensates by producing MORE insulin (hyperinsulinemia)
- **Phase 2:** Beta cell exhaustion — the pancreas cannot keep up with demand; insulin secretion decreases
- **Result:** Hyperglycemia — elevated blood glucose because cells cannot take up glucose effectively AND the liver continues producing glucose (gluconeogenesis) without adequate suppression

**Risk factors for T2DM:**
- Obesity (especially central/abdominal adiposity)
- Sedentary lifestyle
- Family history
- Age > 45
- History of gestational diabetes
- Prediabetes
- Certain ethnicities (African American, Hispanic, Native American — as in Arthur's case)

---

### Q5. What are the 3 Ps of diabetes?

1. **Polyuria** — frequent, excessive urination (glucose in urine acts as osmotic diuretic)
2. **Polydipsia** — excessive thirst (from dehydration from polyuria)
3. **Polyphagia** — increased hunger (cells starving despite high blood glucose — glucose cannot enter without insulin)

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Recognizing Severe Hypoglycemia — THE PRIORITY
**Classic triad:** Diaphoresis + Shakiness + Confusion

**Emergency Response Sequence:**
1. **If awake and able to swallow:** Give 15–20 g fast-acting carbohydrates (4 oz juice, glucose tablets, 4 oz regular soda) — "Rule of 15"
2. **If confused or unable to swallow:** Do NOT give anything orally (aspiration risk)
3. **STAT fingerstick blood glucose** — confirm hypoglycemia
4. **If unconscious/unresponsive AND IV access available:**
   - **Administer Dextrose 50% (D50) IV direct** as ordered
   - Verify patent IV (D50 causes tissue necrosis if infiltrated)
   - Administer slowly (3 mL/min; 50 mL total)
   - Recheck blood glucose in 15 minutes
5. **If no IV access:** Glucagon 1 mg IM or SC (wake patient; then give oral carbohydrates)
6. **Notify provider** via SBAR
7. **Continue monitoring** blood glucose q15–30 min until stable ≥100 mg/dL

### Before Administering Insulin (Sliding Scale)
1. **Check the blood glucose** — verify the reading
2. **Identify the correct insulin type** (regular, glargine, aspart) — sound-alike/look-alike risk
3. **Calculate the correct dose** per sliding scale
4. **Double-check with another RN** — INSULIN IS HIGH ALERT
5. **Verify patient has eaten** (or is about to eat) before giving short-acting insulin
6. **Document** the dose, route, site, and blood glucose reading

### Wound Care — Sacral Wound
- Assess wound: size (LxWxD), stage/depth, color, drainage (type, amount), odor, periwound skin
- Use aseptic technique for wound care
- Send wound culture before starting NEW antibiotics (if not yet done)
- Monitor response to IV antibiotics (Pip/Tazo, Vancomycin)
- Pressure relief: turn and reposition q2h; pressure-redistributing mattress
- Nutrition consult: adequate protein, vitamin C, zinc for wound healing

### Vancomycin Monitoring
- Draw **trough level** 30 minutes BEFORE the next dose
- Therapeutic trough: 15–20 mcg/mL (varies by protocol)
- Hold dose and notify if trough > 20 mcg/mL (toxicity risk)
- Monitor BUN and creatinine (nephrotoxicity)
- Monitor for Red Man Syndrome (flushing, rash) — slow infusion rate

### Q2. What is the rationale for including carbohydrates AND proteins in the treatment of hypoglycemia?

- **Carbohydrates (fast-acting):** Immediately raise blood glucose (juice, glucose tablets, soda)
- **Protein (follow-up):** Slows the subsequent release of glucose, preventing rebound hypoglycemia
- If only carbohydrates are given, blood glucose may spike then drop again
- After acute treatment with fast carbs (rule of 15), follow up with a protein + complex carbohydrate snack (e.g., peanut butter crackers, cheese and crackers)

---

### Q3. If Arthur's acute hypoglycemic episode had not been treated immediately, what could have happened?

- **Progressive neuroglycopenia** — brain cells starve without glucose
- **Seizures** — neuronal excitability from hypoglycemia
- **Loss of consciousness** — brain unable to function
- **Respiratory depression** — CNS depression
- **Cardiac dysrhythmias** — hypoglycemia affects cardiac conduction
- **Permanent brain damage** — if prolonged severe hypoglycemia
- **Death** — if untreated

---

### Q4. What is the correct nursing action when preparing to administer insulin?

1. Verify the correct insulin TYPE (look-alike/sound-alike: NovoLog vs Novolin — very different!)
2. Verify the correct DOSE from the sliding scale
3. Check patient blood glucose (must have current reading)
4. Have a SECOND nurse verify the drug, dose, and calculation (HIGH ALERT)
5. Administer via correct ROUTE at correct SITE; rotate sites
6. Ensure patient is about to eat before rapid-acting insulin
7. Document and monitor for response

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Dextrose 50% (D50) — HIGH ALERT
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Hypertonic glucose solution |
| **Mechanism** | Provides immediate glucose to rapidly raise blood glucose |
| **Indication** | Severe hypoglycemia when patient cannot take oral glucose |
| **Route** | IV DIRECT (slow push) |
| **Dosage** | 20–50 mL IV direct |
| **Rate** | Approximately 3 mL/min (3000 mg/min) |
| **Adverse effects** | Hyperglycemia, glucosuria, phlebitis/tissue necrosis if extravasation ⚠️ |
| **Nursing precautions** | VERIFY IV is patent before giving (causes tissue damage if infiltrated); check blood glucose before and 15 min after; HIGH ALERT — double check |
| **Follow-up** | Recheck BG in 15 min; give complex carb + protein snack when patient awakens |

---

### Vancomycin 1g IV q12h — HIGH ALERT
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Glycopeptide antibiotic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis; covers gram-positive organisms including MRSA |
| **Indication** | Infected sacral wound with suspected MRSA |
| **Adverse effects** | **Nephrotoxicity** (monitor BUN/Cr), **ototoxicity** (hearing loss), **Red Man Syndrome** (if infused too quickly — flushing, rash, hypotension) |
| **Monitoring** | Trough level 30 min before next dose (goal 15–20 mcg/mL) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Infuse over at least 60 min (longer if 1g+ dose); monitor BUN/Cr; check trough levels; assess for hearing changes |
| **Patient teaching** | Report ringing in ears, hearing changes, decreased urine output |

---

### Piperacillin-Tazobactam (Pip/Tazo, Zosyn) 3g IV q6h
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Extended-spectrum beta-lactam + beta-lactamase inhibitor |
| **Mechanism** | Piperacillin inhibits cell wall synthesis; tazobactam protects from beta-lactamase enzymes |
| **Indication** | Broad-spectrum coverage for mixed wound infection (gram-negative, gram-positive, anaerobes) |
| **Adverse effects** | GI disturbance, hypersensitivity (cross-reactive with penicillin — ARTHUR IS ALLERGIC TO CEPHALEXIN, not penicillin; assess cross-reactivity carefully), hypokalemia |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess allergy history carefully; infuse over 30 min–4 hours per protocol; monitor K+ |
