## 🔑 The Big Picture

Agnes's scenario focuses on recognizing and managing a **surgical wound infection** in a frail, malnourished elderly patient post-hip surgery:

```
Left hip fracture → ORIF surgery
        ↓
Postoperative period — wound healing compromised by malnutrition + age
        ↓
Wound infection develops:
  Increased warmth, redness, swelling, purulent drainage, odor, pain
        ↓
Nurse: Assess wound → Notify provider → Wound culture → Antibiotic therapy → Wound care
        ↓
Monitor for progression to systemic infection (early sepsis)
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the signs and symptoms of a surgical wound infection?

**Local Signs (REEDA + more):**
- **R**edness (erythema) — spreading beyond wound edges
- **E**dema — swelling, induration around wound
- **E**cchymosis — bruising (may indicate tissue damage)
- **D**rainage — purulent (yellow, green, tan, or brown); foul odor; increased amount
- **A**pproximation — wound edges separating (dehiscence)
- Increased warmth at wound site
- Increasing pain at wound site (beyond expected postoperative level)

**Systemic Signs (infection spreading):**
- Fever > 38°C (100.4°F)
- Elevated WBC
- Tachycardia
- General malaise, fatigue
- Confusion (especially in elderly — often first sign)

**Lab Indicators:**
- WBC > 12,000/mm³
- Elevated CRP, ESR
- Positive wound culture

---

### Q2. What factors make Agnes Taylor particularly vulnerable to wound infection?

1. **Malnutrition** — inadequate protein, vitamin C, and zinc severely impair immune function and wound healing; malnourished tissue cannot mount an effective inflammatory response
2. **Advanced age (81 years)** — immune senescence; impaired neutrophil function; slower healing
3. **Surgical wound** — any break in skin integrity creates an entry point for pathogens
4. **Hip surgery** — complex surgical site; hardware (internal fixation) provides a surface for biofilm formation
5. **Immobility** — reduces perfusion to wound site; promotes bacterial colonization
6. **Possible comorbidities** — diabetes, vascular disease, or other conditions common in elderly patients

---

### Q3. What is the correct procedure for collecting a wound culture?

1. **Collect BEFORE administering new antibiotics** — antibiotics alter the bacterial flora; culture must reflect actual infecting organisms
2. Explain procedure to patient
3. Don clean gloves; use sterile technique
4. **Irrigate the wound** with normal saline first — removes surface contaminants (skin flora) that would give false results
5. Using a sterile swab, **rotate the swab over a 1 cm² area of viable wound tissue** using the Levine technique (press firmly and rotate) — culture the wound bed, NOT the surface exudate
6. Place swab in **appropriate culture transport medium** immediately
7. Label specimen correctly — patient name, DOB, wound location, date/time, provider name
8. Send to lab promptly
9. Document collection and time

---

### Q4. What wound care interventions are priority for Agnes's infected wound?

**Wound Assessment (every shift):**
- Measure wound: length × width × depth (cm)
- Assess wound bed: color (red/yellow/black), presence of slough or necrosis
- Assess drainage: type (serous, serosanguineous, purulent), amount, odor
- Assess wound edges: approximated or separating?
- Assess periwound skin: maceration, erythema, induration

**Wound Care Interventions:**
1. **Gentle irrigation** — normal saline or prescribed cleanser; removes debris and biofilm
2. **Debridement** — if necrotic tissue present (per wound care nurse/order)
3. **Appropriate dressing selection:**
   - Infected wound: antimicrobial dressing (silver-containing); wet-to-dry if ordered
   - Draining wound: absorbent foam or alginate
4. **Sterile technique** — ORIF wounds require sterile dressing changes to prevent hardware contamination
5. **Wound care nurse consult** — WOCN for complex wound management
6. **Pain management** — pre-medicate before wound care

---

### Q5. What is the role of nutrition in wound healing?

**Key nutrients for wound healing:**
- **Protein** (1.25–1.5 g/kg/day) — necessary for tissue synthesis, collagen formation, and immune function; Agnes's malnutrition makes this deficient
- **Vitamin C** — essential for collagen synthesis; deficiency causes fragile, poorly healing wounds
- **Zinc** — supports immune function and cell proliferation
- **Vitamin A** — supports epithelialization and immune response
- **Adequate hydration** — wound healing requires moist tissue environment and adequate perfusion

**Nursing interventions:**
- Dietitian consultation
- Monitor albumin and prealbumin (markers of nutritional status)
- Encourage high-protein foods or supplements
- Document intake accurately
- Consider enteral nutrition if oral intake severely inadequate

---

### Q6. When and how should the nurse notify the provider about a wound infection?

**Notify immediately when:**
- New purulent drainage is noted
- Wound culture results indicate specific organism requiring targeted antibiotic
- Signs of systemic infection (fever, elevated WBC, new confusion, tachycardia)
- Wound is dehiscing (edges separating)
- Pain at wound site is increasing disproportionately

**SBAR communication:**
- Situation: "I am calling about Agnes Taylor's surgical wound — I have noted increased purulent drainage, erythema, and warmth."
- Background: "She is postoperative day [X] from hip ORIF. She has a history of malnutrition. A wound culture has been obtained."
- Assessment: "I believe she is developing a wound infection. Her temperature is [value], WBC from this morning was [value]."
- Recommendation: "I am requesting antibiotic orders, wound care instructions, and wound care nurse consultation."

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Wound Assessment Priority
1. Perform hand hygiene; don gloves
2. Carefully remove dressing — note amount and type of drainage on old dressing
3. Inspect wound: REEDA assessment
4. Assess periwound skin
5. Measure wound dimensions
6. Assess patient's pain (pre-medicate for future wound care changes)
7. Obtain wound culture BEFORE administering ordered antibiotics (if first antibiotic dose)
8. Apply new dressing per order
9. Reposition patient — pressure off hip area

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up; hip precautions maintained (abductor pillow)
- **W** — Wristband verified
- **I** — IV patent; antibiotic ready per order
- **P** — Pull cord within reach
- **E** — Wound care supplies available at bedside

### Hip Precautions — Maintain Throughout
- No hip flexion > 90°
- No internal rotation
- No crossing legs
- Abductor pillow between legs
- Call for assistance before getting up

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. Priority action when wound infection is suspected?
Notify the provider and obtain a wound culture BEFORE starting new antibiotics — culture results guide targeted therapy.

### Q2. Why is Agnes particularly high-risk for wound infection complications?
Malnutrition impairs immune function and wound healing. Advanced age causes immune senescence. ORIF hardware creates a surface for biofilm. Combined, these factors significantly increase infection risk and severity.

### Q3. Correct wound culture technique?
Irrigate wound with NS first → culture viable wound bed (not surface exudate) using Levine technique → transport medium → lab promptly → collect BEFORE new antibiotics.

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t surgical wound infection — AEB: purulent drainage, erythema, wound not healing
2. **Risk for Sepsis** r/t wound infection in immunocompromised elderly patient — AEB: malnutrition, age, ORIF hardware
3. **Acute Pain** r/t infected wound — AEB: increased pain at surgical site
4. **Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements** r/t malnutrition — AEB: malnutrition diagnosis; albumin low
5. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t wound care, infection signs, and nutrition — AEB: needs discharge teaching
