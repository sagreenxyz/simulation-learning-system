## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes**:

1. **Colostomy care mechanics** — proper technique for emptying, changing the pouching system, and stoma assessment
2. **Body image and psychosocial adjustment** — Jesus is only 28 and has resisted accepting his colostomy; this is a major emotional challenge
3. **Dehydration management** — a common complication of colostomies (especially transverse colostomies) due to altered fluid absorption
4. **Patient teaching for independent self-care** — Virginia is returning to work; Jesus must learn to care for his colostomy himself

The cause-and-effect chain:

```
Ulcerative colitis (chronic inflammatory bowel disease)
        ↓
Partial colectomy required → Transverse colostomy created
        ↓
Altered GI anatomy → Watery output → Fluid/electrolyte losses → Dehydration
        ↓
Jesus refuses to participate in colostomy care → Virginia doing everything
        ↓
Virginia returns to work → Jesus cannot care for himself independently
        ↓
Nurse must: manage dehydration + teach colostomy care + address body image
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are common signs and symptoms of dehydration?

**Mild dehydration:**
- Increased thirst
- Dry mouth and mucous membranes
- Decreased urine output; concentrated (dark yellow) urine
- Mild headache
- Feeling tired or lethargic

**Moderate dehydration:**
- Increased heart rate (tachycardia)
- Decreased blood pressure (may progress to orthostatic hypotension)
- Poor skin turgor (skin tents when pinched)
- Sunken eyes
- Decreased urine output significantly
- Dizziness, lightheadedness

**Severe dehydration:**
- Marked tachycardia
- Hypotension
- Confusion, disorientation
- No urine output (anuria)
- Extreme weakness
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Possible hypovolemic shock if untreated

**Lab indicators:**
- Elevated BUN and creatinine (concentrated)
- Elevated hematocrit (hemoconcentration)
- Elevated urine specific gravity (>1.030)
- Elevated serum osmolality

> **For Jesus:** His transverse colostomy produces watery, liquid stool — this accelerates fluid and electrolyte losses compared to normal bowel function, making dehydration a persistent risk.

---

### Q2. What are complications you may see as a result of poor nutrition?

Poor nutrition has widespread effects on recovery after bowel surgery:

- **Impaired wound healing** — protein and vitamin C are essential for collagen synthesis
- **Increased infection risk** — malnutrition suppresses immune function
- **Electrolyte imbalances** — sodium, potassium, and magnesium losses affect cardiac and neuromuscular function
- **Muscle weakness and fatigue** — inadequate caloric intake leads to muscle wasting
- **Dehydration** — inadequate fluid intake or excessive output from colostomy
- **Anemia** — iron deficiency from poor intake or altered absorption
- **Poor skin integrity** — protein deficiency impairs skin healing around stoma site
- **Vitamin deficiencies** — altered absorption in partial colectomy patients
- **Delayed recovery and longer hospitalization**

### Q3. Describe nursing care and interventions for a patient with a colostomy.

**Stoma Assessment (every shift):**
- Color — healthy stoma should be **pink to red and moist** (like the inside of a cheek)
  - Pale: possible poor circulation
  - Dark red/purple: possible ischemia — notify provider immediately
  - Black: necrosis — emergency
- Size — stoma is largest immediately post-op and shrinks over 6–8 weeks
- Peristomal skin — should be intact; no redness, excoriation, or breakdown
- Stoma output — color, consistency, amount

**Emptying the Colostomy Pouch:**
- Empty when **1/3 to 1/2 full** to prevent leakage and weight pulling on the seal
- Hold pouch open over toilet or measuring container
- Rinse inside of pouch tail with water
- Close clamp securely
- Document output: amount, consistency, color

**Changing the Pouching System:**
- Change the entire pouching system every **3–5 days** (or sooner if leaking)
- Timing: change when stoma output is minimal (before meals or 1–2 hours after)
- Steps:
  1. Gather supplies (new pouch, skin barrier/wafer, scissors, ostomy paste, washcloth)
  2. Gently remove old pouching system (push down on skin while lifting wafer — do not pull)
  3. Clean peristomal skin with warm water and pat dry (no soap with additives)
  4. Measure stoma size and cut barrier opening to fit (1/8 inch larger than stoma)
  5. Apply barrier paste if needed
  6. Apply new pouching system, centering over stoma
  7. Press firmly to ensure seal
  8. Secure bottom clamp

**Patient Teaching — Self-Care at Home:**
- How to empty and change the pouch independently
- When to contact provider: no output for >4 hours, black or very dark stoma, fever, severe abdominal pain, skin breakdown
- Dietary considerations (see below)
- Odor management
- Activity restrictions — avoid heavy lifting in early postoperative period

---

### Q4. What are 3 nursing interventions for a postoperative patient?

1. **Pain assessment and management** — assess using numeric scale; administer analgesics per order; reassess within 30–60 minutes; use non-pharmacological methods (positioning, ice, relaxation)
2. **Respiratory interventions** — encourage incentive spirometry, deep breathing and coughing every 1–2 hours; monitor for signs of atelectasis or pneumonia (decreased breath sounds, fever, productive cough)
3. **DVT prevention** — encourage early ambulation; ensure sequential compression devices (SCDs) are applied; administer anticoagulants if ordered; teach leg exercises; monitor for calf pain, redness, or swelling

### Q5. What are the dietary considerations for a patient with a colostomy?

**Foods that may thicken stool output (helpful for liquid stool from transverse colostomy):**
- Bananas, applesauce, rice, toast (BRAT diet elements)
- Peanut butter, yogurt, cheese

**Foods that may cause odor (patient should be warned):**
- Onions, garlic, fish, eggs, beans, asparagus, cruciferous vegetables

**Foods that may cause gas (patient should be warned):**
- Beans, carbonated beverages, broccoli, cabbage, corn

**Foods/habits to encourage:**
- Adequate fluid intake (8–10 glasses/day) to prevent dehydration
- Chew food well
- Eat regular meals at consistent times
- Introduce new foods one at a time to identify problem foods

### Q6. What are the psychosocial considerations for a 28-year-old man with a new colostomy?

Body image disturbance is a major concern for young patients with an ostomy:

- A 28-year-old man faces concerns about **sexuality, intimacy, body image, self-concept, and independence**
- Jesus is already showing avoidance behavior — refusing to participate in care
- This is a normal grief response to altered body function; the nurse should **validate, not dismiss** his feelings
- Motivational interviewing approaches: "What concerns you most about managing this on your own?"
- Connect Jesus with a **Wound, Ostomy, and Continence (WOC) nurse** — specialized expertise
- Referral to an **ostomy support group** or peer visitor program (United Ostomy Associations of America)
- Educate about **the wide variety of products** available that are discreet and odor-resistant
- Sexuality and intimacy: address openly with sensitivity; encourage honest conversation with Virginia
- Emphasize that many people live full, active lives with an ostomy

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Priority Assessment on Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene; introduce yourself
2. Verify patient identity with two identifiers
3. Assess VS — HR, BP, temp (signs of dehydration)
4. Assess hydration status: mucous membranes, skin turgor, cap refill
5. Assess IV access and IV fluid infusion rate
6. Inspect the colostomy pouch and stoma

### Key Communication with Jesus
- **Acknowledge his feelings** first before teaching: "It sounds like this has been really hard to adjust to. That makes complete sense."
- Ask open-ended questions: "What has it been like for you since surgery?"
- **Do not lecture** — use motivational interviewing
- Frame teaching as empowerment: "Learning this will give you more freedom and control."
- Include Virginia when she is present, but focus teaching on Jesus — he must learn independence

### Teaching the Colostomy Change (During Simulation)
- Use teach-back method: "Can you show me how you would empty the pouch?"
- Break steps into small pieces — do not overwhelm
- Demonstrate first, then supervise Jesus doing it
- Praise every step: "You did that exactly right."

### Healthy vs. Abnormal Stoma — Key Assessment Points

| Finding | Normal | Abnormal → Action |
|---|---|---|
| Color | Pink to red, moist | Pale, dark purple, black → notify provider |
| Moisture | Moist, glistening | Dry → assess hydration |
| Peristomal skin | Intact, no redness | Excoriation, rash, bleeding → skin barrier products |
| Output | Varies by stoma type | No output >4 hours → notify provider |
| Stoma bleeding | Small amount with touch | Bright red bleeding → assess further |

### Q1. What nursing diagnoses are most appropriate for Jesus Garcia?

1. **Deficient Fluid Volume** r/t excessive colostomy output and inadequate oral intake — AEB tachycardia, dry mucous membranes, dark urine
2. **Disturbed Body Image** r/t colostomy creation and altered bowel function — AEB refusing to participate in colostomy care, avoidance behaviors
3. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t colostomy self-care management — AEB has not performed colostomy care since discharge; Virginia does everything
4. **Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t colostomy effluent contact with peristomal skin
5. **Ineffective Coping** r/t altered body function and lifestyle changes post-colostomy

---

### Q2. What is a transverse colostomy, and how does it differ from other types?

| Type | Location | Output | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ascending (cecostomy)** | Right side | Very liquid, enzyme-rich | Temporary; drains liquid stool |
| **Transverse** | Mid-abdomen | Semi-formed to liquid | May be temporary or permanent (Jesus has this) |
| **Descending** | Left side | Semi-formed to formed | Often permanent |
| **Sigmoid** | Lower left | Formed, most like normal stool | Most common; often permanent |

> **Jesus has a transverse colostomy** — output is watery to semi-formed, increasing the risk of dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.

---

### Q3. What are the signs of stoma ischemia or necrosis the nurse must watch for?

- **Dark purple or bluish stoma**: venous ischemia — notify provider
- **Black stoma**: necrosis — EMERGENCY; notify provider immediately
- Stoma that appears dry or dull rather than moist
- Foul smell beyond normal
- Stoma that appears to be retracting into the abdominal wall

---

### Q4. What patient education points are essential for Jesus before discharge?

1. **When to call the provider:** black or dark purple stoma, no output for >4 hours, fever, abdominal pain/distension, leakage around the seal, signs of dehydration, skin breakdown
2. **Colostomy routine:** empty when 1/3–1/2 full; change pouching system every 3–5 days
3. **Diet:** adequate hydration; foods to avoid; dietary log to identify triggers
4. **Activity:** no heavy lifting (>10 lbs) for 6–8 weeks postoperatively
5. **Resources:** WOC nurse, ostomy support groups, product suppliers
6. **Sexual health:** colostomy does not prevent intimacy; discuss concerns with provider

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Mesalamine (Asacol) 800 mg PO
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Aminosalicylate / GI anti-inflammatory |
| **Indication (Jesus)** | Treat and maintain remission of ulcerative colitis |
| **Mechanism** | Local anti-inflammatory effect in colon mucosa; inhibits prostaglandins |
| **Adverse effects** | GI: diarrhea, flatulence, nausea, vomiting; GU: interstitial nephritis, renal failure (rare); pancreatitis |
| **Contraindications** | Hypersensitivity to sulfonamides or salicylates (check allergy history) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess for salicylate allergy; monitor renal function; do not crush or chew enteric-coated tablets; can be given via G-tube if liquid form used |

---

### Ondansetron (Zofran) 4 mg IVP
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Antiemetic (5-HT3 receptor antagonist) |
| **Indication (Jesus)** | Prevention and treatment of postoperative nausea and vomiting |
| **Route** | IV push; administer undiluted over 2–5 minutes |
| **Adverse effects** | Headache, constipation, diarrhea, QT prolongation (cardiac monitoring if high doses) |
| **Contraindications** | Hypersensitivity; caution in patients with prolonged QT interval |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor EKG if prolonged QT risk; assess effectiveness (nausea relief within 30 min) |

---

### IV Fluids (0.9% Normal Saline / Lactated Ringer's)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Isotonic crystalloid |
| **Indication (Jesus)** | Treatment of dehydration and fluid replacement from colostomy losses |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor urine output (goal ≥30 mL/hr); daily weights; assess for fluid overload (rare in young patients); IV site assessment |

## 🗺️ CONCEPT MAP

**Primary Problem:** Deficient Fluid Volume r/t excessive transverse colostomy output

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Assessment Cues** | Tachycardia, dry mucous membranes, decreased BP, decreased urine output, dark concentrated urine, weakness, lightheadedness |
| **Related Factors** | Transverse colostomy with watery output, inadequate oral intake, refusal to participate in colostomy care |
| **SMART Goal** | Patient will demonstrate adequate fluid balance as evidenced by urine output ≥30 mL/hr, moist mucous membranes, and HR <100 bpm within 24 hours of IV fluid initiation |
| **Interventions** | IV fluid rehydration; encourage oral intake; monitor I&O; daily weights; electrolyte monitoring; teach Jesus to monitor colostomy output volume |
| **Rationale** | Isotonic fluids restore extracellular fluid volume; monitoring I&O quantifies losses; daily weights detect fluid changes; patient education prevents future episodes |
| **Evaluation** | Urine output ≥30 mL/hr; moist mucous membranes; HR normalizes; patient demonstrates colostomy emptying technique |
