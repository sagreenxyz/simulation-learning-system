## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Describe the pathophysiology of Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus.

**Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM)** is an **autoimmune disease** characterized by the destruction of pancreatic beta cells, leading to absolute insulin deficiency.

**Pathophysiology:**
1. **Autoimmune destruction** of beta cells in the islets of Langerhans — mediated by T-lymphocytes; often triggered by genetic predisposition + environmental factors (viral infection)
2. Progressive beta cell loss → **absolute insulin deficiency**
3. Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells → **hyperglycemia**
4. Cells starved for glucose → body initiates **gluconeogenesis** (liver produces glucose) and **lipolysis** (breakdown of fat)
5. Lipolysis produces **free fatty acids** → liver converts to **ketone bodies** → **ketoacidosis** if severe
6. Hyperglycemia → **osmotic diuresis** (glucose spills into urine, pulling water with it) → polyuria → polydipsia → dehydration

**Classic presentation ("3 Polys"):**
- **Polyuria** — frequent urination
- **Polydipsia** — excessive thirst
- **Polyphagia** — increased hunger despite eating

**Why Ricky is different from Type 2:**
- T1DM requires **exogenous insulin** — no alternative; no diet or oral medication can replace insulin
- Onset typically in childhood/adolescence
- Prone to **diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)** without insulin

### Q2. What are the signs and symptoms of hyperglycemia versus hypoglycemia, and how is each managed?

| Feature | Hyperglycemia | Hypoglycemia |
|---|---|---|
| **Blood glucose** | > 180 mg/dL (symptomatic > 250 mg/dL) | < 70 mg/dL |
| **Onset** | Gradual (hours–days) | Rapid (minutes) |
| **Symptoms** | Polyuria, polydipsia, fatigue, blurred vision, headache, nausea, fruity breath (DKA) | Shakiness, diaphoresis, pallor, tachycardia, anxiety, confusion, seizure (severe) |
| **Skin** | Warm, dry, flushed | Cool, clammy, diaphoretic |
| **LOC** | Gradual deterioration | Can rapidly progress to unconsciousness |
| **Management** | Administer insulin per sliding scale; increase hydration; notify provider | "Rule of 15": give 15g fast-acting carbs; recheck in 15 minutes; repeat if needed |
| **Severe management** | IV insulin drip + IV fluids + electrolyte monitoring (DKA protocol) | IV dextrose (D50) or IM glucagon if unconscious or cannot swallow |

**Fast-acting carbohydrate sources (15g) for hypoglycemia:**
- 4 oz (½ cup) fruit juice or regular soda
- 3–4 glucose tablets
- 1 tablespoon honey or sugar

**Ricky's current presentation:** Elevated blood glucose consistent with **hyperglycemia**; nurse must verify glucose level, assess for DKA signs, and administer sliding scale insulin.

### Q3. What are insulin types, their onset/peak/duration, and how is a sliding scale used?

**Insulin Types:**

| Type | Example | Onset | Peak | Duration | Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rapid-acting** | Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog) | 15 min | 1–2 hr | 3–4 hr | Mealtime bolus |
| **Short-acting** | Regular (Humulin R) | 30–60 min | 2–4 hr | 6–8 hr | Mealtime; IV drip |
| **Intermediate** | NPH (Humulin N) | 1–3 hr | 4–12 hr | 16–24 hr | Basal coverage |
| **Long-acting** | Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) | 1–2 hr | Peakless | 20–24 hr | Once-daily basal |

**Sliding Scale Insulin:**
- A **correction dose** of rapid-acting insulin based on current blood glucose reading
- Administered **in addition to** scheduled basal insulin, not as a replacement
- Example scale for Ricky:
  - BG 150–199: 1 unit lispro SQ
  - BG 200–249: 2 units lispro SQ
  - BG 250–299: 3 units lispro SQ
  - BG 300–349: 4 units lispro SQ
  - BG ≥ 350: 5 units lispro SQ + notify provider

**SQ Injection technique:**
- Rotate sites: abdomen (fastest absorption), thigh, upper arm, buttock
- Use 45° or 90° angle depending on needle length and body composition
- Pinch skin if using shorter needle; release pinch before injecting
- Do not rub site after injection
- Inspect sites for lipohypertrophy (fatty lumps from repeated same-site injection) — avoid injecting into these areas

### Q4. What is Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA), how is it recognized, and when must the nurse escalate care?

**DKA** is a life-threatening complication of T1DM caused by profound insulin deficiency + counterregulatory hormone excess.

**Diagnostic criteria:**
- Blood glucose > 250 mg/dL
- Serum bicarbonate < 18 mEq/L (metabolic acidosis)
- Positive serum or urine ketones
- pH < 7.30

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Fruity/acetone breath (ketones exhaled)
- Kussmaul respirations — deep, rapid breathing (compensatory mechanism for acidosis)
- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
- Dehydration: dry mucous membranes, sunken eyes, poor skin turgor
- Altered mental status in severe cases
- Tachycardia, hypotension

**Lab findings:**
- Elevated blood glucose, elevated BUN/Creatinine (dehydration)
- Low pH, low HCO3 (metabolic acidosis), elevated anion gap
- Elevated ketones (serum and urine)
- Hypokalemia or hyperkalemia (potassium shifts with pH changes)

**Escalate immediately if:**
- Blood glucose ≥ 350 mg/dL unresponsive to SQ insulin
- Fruity breath or Kussmaul respirations
- Altered mental status
- Vomiting preventing oral intake
- Signs of dehydration or hemodynamic instability

**DKA Treatment (hospitalization):** IV fluid resuscitation, IV regular insulin drip, electrolyte replacement (especially potassium), frequent glucose and electrolyte monitoring

### Q5. What developmental and psychosocial considerations apply to a 13-year-old managing a chronic disease?

**Developmental stage:** Early adolescence (Erikson: *Identity vs. Role Confusion*; Piaget: *Formal Operational*)

**Psychosocial challenges with chronic illness in adolescence:**
- **Independence vs. parental control** — Ricky wants autonomy; mother's involvement may feel intrusive
- **Peer belonging** — diabetes management (finger sticks, injections, food restrictions) makes Ricky feel different from peers
- **Concrete thinking is giving way to abstract**, but emotional regulation is still developing
- **Invincibility fable** — adolescents may underestimate consequences of poor management ("nothing bad will happen to me")
- **Chronicity fatigue** — Ricky and his mother are frustrated with the constant demands of T1DM management; this is normal and common

**Motivational Interviewing (MI) approach:**
- Express **empathy**: "Managing diabetes every single day is a lot — I can understand why it gets frustrating."
- Explore **ambivalence**: "What things about your diabetes do you wish were different? What would it mean to you to have your glucose more under control?"
- **Avoid lecturing** — information alone does not change behavior in adolescents
- Affirm strengths: "The fact that you came in today shows you care about your health."
- Involve Ricky in **setting his own goals** — more effective than goals set by others

**Communication strategies with mother:**
- Acknowledge her frustration and efforts
- Reframe conflict: help her transition from full manager to **coach/supporter** as Ricky develops self-management skills
- Avoid triangulation — address both Ricky and mother; ensure Ricky hears information directly

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene
2. Introduce yourself — address Ricky directly first, then acknowledge his mother
3. Verify identity using two identifiers
4. Perform SWIPE safety assessment
5. Assess symptoms: "Ricky, how have you been feeling? Any headaches, nausea, blurry vision, or increased thirst recently?"

### Blood Glucose Assessment and Insulin Administration
1. Perform or verify bedside blood glucose using glucometer per protocol
2. Document blood glucose result in EHR
3. Consult sliding scale insulin order — determine correct dose based on current BG
4. Prepare insulin using correct technique: verify insulin type, check expiration, roll (do not shake) cloudy insulin
5. Draw up correct dose; verify with second nurse if required by policy
6. Select appropriate injection site; cleanse with alcohol swab; allow to dry
7. Administer SQ injection using correct angle and technique
8. Document insulin administered (type, dose, site, time) in EHR
9. Reassess blood glucose in 1–2 hours per protocol; educate patient on expected response

### Wound Assessment — Right Great Toe
1. Don gloves; assess right great toe wound
2. Verbalize findings: size, depth, wound bed color, exudate (amount, color, consistency), surrounding tissue (erythema, warmth, edema, induration)
3. Assess for signs of systemic infection: fever, lymphadenopathy, spreading cellulitis
4. Confirm antibiotic order is active; assess adherence to antibiotic course
5. Irrigate wound per order with normal saline; apply appropriate dressing
6. Document wound assessment in EHR

### Diabetic Teaching
1. Assess Ricky's current knowledge: "Tell me what you know about why your blood sugar runs high."
2. Validate and build on existing knowledge; correct misconceptions without judgment
3. Discuss **connection between high blood sugar and wound healing**: "When blood sugar is high, it makes it harder for your body to fight infection and heal. That's actually part of why your toe is having trouble."
4. Review insulin technique with Ricky — offer to watch him draw up a practice dose
5. Discuss glucose monitoring frequency and targets; address barriers honestly
6. Engage mother: "What's been the hardest part of helping Ricky with his diabetes at home?"

### Q2. How does hyperglycemia impair wound healing?

Hyperglycemia disrupts wound healing through multiple mechanisms:

- **Impaired neutrophil function** — high glucose reduces the ability of white blood cells to migrate to and destroy pathogens
- **Impaired angiogenesis** — new blood vessel formation required for tissue repair is inhibited
- **Reduced collagen synthesis** — structural protein essential for wound closure is produced in lower amounts
- **Peripheral neuropathy** (with chronic hyperglycemia) — reduces sensation, so wounds may go unnoticed and untreated longer
- **Peripheral vascular disease** (with chronic hyperglycemia) — reduced blood flow to extremities limits oxygen and nutrient delivery to wound sites
- **Glycosylation of proteins** — glycated proteins function poorly, impairing immune cell activity

**Clinical implication for Ricky:** His right great toe wound is not healing because his blood glucose has been consistently elevated, reducing his immune response and tissue repair capacity. Improving glycemic control is a direct intervention for wound healing.

### Q3. What are sick-day management rules for a patient with Type 1 Diabetes?

Illness causes **physiologic stress**, which raises blood glucose due to release of stress hormones (cortisol, glucagon, epinephrine). Key sick-day rules:

- **Never stop taking insulin** — even if not eating; insulin requirements often **increase** during illness
- **Check blood glucose every 2–4 hours**
- **Check ketones** if blood glucose > 240 mg/dL — urine or blood ketone strips
- **Stay hydrated** — sip sugar-free fluids if nauseous; if vomiting persists, seek care
- If unable to eat: substitute carbohydrate intake with regular soda, juice, or popsicles to maintain carbohydrate intake; adjust insulin doses accordingly
- **Contact provider or go to ER if:**
  - Blood glucose > 350 mg/dL not responding to correction
  - Moderate or large ketones
  - Vomiting that prevents any oral intake
  - Signs of DKA (fruity breath, rapid deep breathing, confusion)
  - Illness lasting more than 1–2 days without improvement
