## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Compare Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes mellitus.

| Feature | Type 1 DM | Type 2 DM |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Autoimmune destruction of beta cells → absolute insulin deficiency | Insulin resistance + progressive beta cell dysfunction |
| **Onset** | Usually childhood/adolescence (can occur at any age) | Usually adulthood (increasingly in adolescents) |
| **Insulin** | Required for survival | May not be needed initially |
| **Body type** | Often normal/thin | Often overweight/obese |
| **Ketosis risk** | High — DKA common | Low (HHNS more common) |
| **Treatment** | Insulin always required | Lifestyle + oral medications + insulin if needed |

**Ricky has Type 1 DM** — he requires insulin for survival and for every glucose management scenario.

---

### Q2. What are the signs, symptoms, and treatment of hyperglycemia?

**Hyperglycemia:** Blood glucose > 180 mg/dL in hospital setting (> 126 mg/dL fasting)

**Signs and Symptoms (the 3 Ps + more):**
- **Polyuria** — frequent urination (osmotic diuresis)
- **Polydipsia** — excessive thirst (dehydration from polyuria)
- **Polyphagia** — increased hunger (cellular starvation)
- Fatigue, weakness
- Blurred vision
- Headache
- Slow wound healing
- Fruity breath (ketones — developing DKA)
- Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid) — DKA

**Treatment:**
- Correct insulin administration (per sliding scale; confirm type and dose)
- IV fluid replacement if dehydrated
- Monitor blood glucose q1–4h
- Treat underlying cause (infection worsens hyperglycemia via stress hormones)
- If DKA: IV insulin infusion, aggressive IV fluids, electrolyte replacement

---

### Q3. What are the signs, symptoms, and treatment of hypoglycemia?

**Hypoglycemia:** Blood glucose < 70 mg/dL

**Signs and Symptoms:**
- Shakiness, trembling
- Diaphoresis (cold, clammy sweat)
- Palpitations, tachycardia
- Pallor
- Anxiety, irritability
- Hunger
- **Late:** Confusion, altered consciousness, seizure, coma

**Treatment:**
- **If conscious and able to swallow:** 15 g fast-acting carbohydrates (4 oz juice, 3–4 glucose tablets, regular soda)
- **Recheck in 15 minutes** — repeat if still < 70 mg/dL (Rule of 15)
- **If unconscious:** Glucagon IM/SQ or IV dextrose (D50 in adults; D25 in children; D10 in neonates)

### Q4. What insulin types does Ricky need to know about?

| Insulin Type | Examples | Onset | Peak | Duration | When Given |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rapid-acting** | Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog) | 10–15 min | 1–2 hr | 3–5 hr | 0–15 min before meals |
| **Short-acting (Regular)** | Humulin R, Novolin R | 30–60 min | 2–4 hr | 5–8 hr | 30–45 min before meals |
| **Intermediate** | NPH | 1–2 hr | 4–12 hr | 12–18 hr | Twice daily |
| **Long-acting (Basal)** | Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) | 1–4 hr | No peak | 20–24 hr | Once daily (any time, consistent) |

**Key teaching for Ricky:**
- "Rapid-acting insulin (Humalog) should be taken right before eating — not 30 minutes before"
- "Lantus (glargine) is your basal insulin — you take it once a day at the same time every day"
- "Never skip doses even if you're not eating well — adjust with your doctor's guidance"

### Q5. How should the nurse communicate with a frustrated adolescent and mother?

**Adolescent-Specific Communication:**
- **Respect autonomy** — Ricky is 13; involve him directly, not just the parent
- **Address him first** — then include mother: "Ricky, I'd like to talk to you first about your diabetes, and your mom can listen too."
- **Non-judgmental tone** — "I can hear that managing diabetes has been really frustrating."
- **Acknowledge difficulty** — T1DM management as a teenager is genuinely hard: social pressures, wanting to be "normal," privacy concerns
- **Find his motivation** — sports, activities, future plans: "What do you like to do? Good control means you can keep doing those things."
- **Provide privacy** — offer to speak with Ricky alone (developmentally appropriate)
- **Avoid lecturing** — short, clear teaching points; check understanding

**Working with a Frustrated Mother:**
- Validate her concern: "I can see you're worried about Ricky."
- Acknowledge the challenge: "Managing diabetes in a teenager is really difficult — you're not alone in this."
- Involve her without undermining Ricky's autonomy
- Discuss referral to diabetes educator and support groups

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION

### On Entry
1. Check blood glucose (STAT fingerstick)
2. Assess wound on right great toe: size, depth, drainage, signs of infection
3. Vital signs — fever? Tachycardia from infection?
4. Assess for signs of DKA: Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath, abdominal pain
5. Review most recent labs (glucose, CBC, BMP, urinalysis for ketones)
6. Administer insulin per sliding scale for hyperglycemia (after confirming with second nurse — HIGH ALERT)
7. Begin teaching when Ricky is more comfortable

### Insulin Administration Safety
1. Verify correct INSULIN TYPE — common error; lispro vs. glargine look alike
2. Verify correct DOSE — recalculate from sliding scale
3. Double-check with second nurse (HIGH ALERT medication)
4. Rotate injection sites — abdomen, outer thigh, upper arm
5. Document site, time, dose, and blood glucose reading

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. Why does infection worsen hyperglycemia?
Infection triggers release of counter-regulatory stress hormones (cortisol, epinephrine, glucagon) → increase hepatic glucose production → hyperglycemia even without eating.

### Q2. Priority concern with insulin administration?
Correct insulin TYPE and DOSE — insulin errors (wrong type, wrong dose) are among the most common medication errors. Always double-check with second nurse.

### Q3. What teaching is most important for Ricky at discharge?
Sick day rules: continue insulin even when ill; check glucose more frequently when sick; monitor for ketones; when to call provider or go to ED.

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Unstable Blood Glucose** r/t insufficient insulin and wound infection — AEB: hyperglycemia, HbA1c elevated
2. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t infected right great toe wound — AEB: open infected wound; hyperglycemia impairing healing
3. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t insulin management, sick day rules, and glucose monitoring — AEB: poorly controlled DM; frustrated patient and parent
4. **Ineffective Self-Health Management** r/t adolescent developmental challenges and disease burden — AEB: non-adherence, frustration
