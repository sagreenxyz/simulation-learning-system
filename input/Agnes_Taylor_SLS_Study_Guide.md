## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **Postoperative ORIF monitoring** — hip fracture surgery carries major risks; the student must know what to monitor including neurovascular assessment, VTE prevention, and wound healing
2. **Wound infection progressing to early sepsis** — on postoperative day 4, Agnes develops a wound infection that is beginning to spread systemically; early recognition of sepsis is critical
3. **Elderly + malnutrition = high complication risk** — Agnes's age (81) and malnutrition impair her immune response and wound healing, making her especially vulnerable to infection and sepsis

The cascade:

```
Left hip fracture → ORIF surgery
        ↓
Postoperative day 4
        ↓
Wound infection develops:
  • Incision: redness, warmth, swelling, purulent drainage
  • Pain increasing beyond expected postoperative level
  • Fever
        ↓
Bacteria enter bloodstream from wound → EARLY SEPSIS:
  • SIRS criteria met (fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, elevated WBC)
  • Early confusion (especially notable in elderly)
        ↓
Nurse: Notify provider → Blood cultures x2 → Wound culture → IV antibiotics → Fluid resuscitation
        ↓
Septic shock risk if not treated: Hypotension + organ failure
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Discuss all important monitoring during postoperative management of a patient who has undergone ORIF.

**Neurovascular Assessment (5 P's) — q2–4h:**
1. **Pain** — assess location, character; pain out of proportion may indicate compartment syndrome
2. **Pulses** — check distal pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial) bilaterally; compare sides
3. **Pallor** — assess skin color and capillary refill (< 2 seconds normal)
4. **Paresthesias** — assess for numbness or tingling (nerve compromise)
5. **Paralysis/Paresis** — assess motor function; can patient move toes/foot?

**Additional Monitoring:**
- **Vital signs** — q4h; monitor for fever (infection), hypotension (bleeding, sepsis), tachycardia
- **Wound** — assess incision q shift: redness, swelling, warmth, drainage (color, amount, consistency), odor, dehiscence
- **Drains** — if present: record amount, color, and character of drainage
- **Bleeding** — excessive drainage on dressing; bright red blood in drain > 100 mL/hr → notify provider
- **Urinary output** — ≥30 mL/hr; urinary retention common post-hip surgery
- **DVT/PE prevention** — assess for calf pain, swelling, Homans' sign (not reliable); administer anticoagulants per order; TEDs/SCDs; early mobility
- **Fat embolism** — restlessness, sudden dyspnea, chest pain, hemoptysis, petechiae (1–3 days post-fracture)
- **Pulmonary embolism** — sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia, decreased SpO2
- **Compartment syndrome** — severe pain uncontrolled by analgesics, tightness, paresthesia, pallor (EMERGENCY — compartment pressure > 30 mmHg)
- **Positioning** — abduction of the hip required; assess proper positioning; abductor pillow/wedge

### Q2. Describe the pathophysiological process of sepsis.

**Definition:** Sepsis is a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.

**Pathophysiology:**
1. **Infection** — bacteria (gram-negative most common in surgical wounds — E. coli, Pseudomonas; gram-positive — Staph, Strep; MRSA) enter the body
2. **Localized inflammatory response** — immune system activates locally
3. **Systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS)** — inflammation "spills over" into the systemic circulation
4. **Mediator release** — cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6), prostaglandins, histamine, and other mediators are released systemically
5. **Endothelial damage** — capillary leak → third-spacing of fluid → decreased intravascular volume
6. **Vasodilation** — widespread → decreased vascular resistance → hypotension
7. **Microthrombi** — DIC risk; microvascular obstruction → organ ischemia
8. **Organ failure** — multiple organ dysfunction syndrome (MODS) if progression unchecked

### Q3. What are the diagnostic criteria for sepsis and early manifestations?

**SIRS Criteria (2 or more required for SIRS; in sepsis, must have infection source):**
- Temperature > 38°C (100.4°F) or < 36°C (96.8°F)
- Heart rate > 90 bpm
- Respiratory rate > 20 breaths/min or PaCO2 < 32 mmHg
- WBC > 12,000/mm³ or < 4,000/mm³ or > 10% bands

**Current Sepsis-3 Criteria (2016):**
- Suspected infection PLUS
- Acute increase in SOFA score ≥ 2 points (organ dysfunction)

**Quick SOFA (qSOFA) — Bedside screening:**
2 or more of:
- Altered mental status
- Respiratory rate ≥ 22 breaths/min
- Systolic BP ≤ 100 mmHg

**Early Manifestations of Sepsis:**
- **Fever** (or hypothermia in elderly/immunocompromised)
- **Tachycardia** (HR > 90)
- **Tachypnea** (RR > 20)
- **Confusion** (especially in elderly — often first sign)
- Flushed, warm skin (early — vasodilation)
- Elevated WBC
- Increased respiratory rate
- Hypotension may be ABSENT in early sepsis (develops in septic shock)

### Q4. Compare and contrast compensatory and progressive stages of shock.

**Compensatory Stage (Stage 1):**
- The body attempts to maintain homeostasis in response to decreased cardiac output
- **Mechanisms:** SNS activation → tachycardia, increased cardiac contractility; vasoconstriction to maintain BP; RAAS activation → fluid retention; increased respiratory rate
- **Signs:** Tachycardia, mild anxiety, cool/pale skin, decreased urine output (< 30 mL/hr), normal or slightly decreased BP
- **Key:** Patient is still perfusing vital organs; aggressive intervention at this stage is most effective

**Progressive Stage (Stage 2):**
- Compensatory mechanisms BEGIN TO FAIL; tissue perfusion decreasing
- **Signs:** BP dropping (systolic < 90), severe tachycardia (HR > 130), worsening confusion/disorientation, cool/clammy skin, oliguria (< 20 mL/hr), metabolic acidosis (lactic acid building), bowel sounds decreased
- **Key:** Oxygen deprivation causing confusion, weakness, organ dysfunction; urgent intervention required

**Refractory Stage (Stage 3):**
- Irreversible organ damage; intervention no longer reverses shock
- Multiple organ failure
- **Signs:** Persistent hypotension despite vasopressors, anuria, coma, dysrhythmias
- **Key:** Very high mortality; prevent by identifying sepsis early

### Q5. Discuss initial collaborative care measures for a patient with sepsis.

**Sepsis Bundles (based on Surviving Sepsis Campaign):**

**Within 1 hour of sepsis recognition (HOUR-1 BUNDLE):**
1. **Measure lactate level** — elevated lactate (> 2 mmol/L) indicates tissue hypoperfusion
2. **Blood cultures x2** — obtain BEFORE starting antibiotics (from two different sites)
3. **Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics** — within 1 hour of recognition
4. **Administer crystalloid fluid** — 30 mL/kg IV (isotonic — 0.9% NS or LR) for hypotension or lactate ≥ 4
5. **Vasopressors** — norepinephrine first-line if hypotension persists after fluid resuscitation

**Ongoing Care:**
- Wound culture — before antibiotics if wound is source
- Oxygen therapy — maintain SpO2 ≥ 94%
- Foley catheter — monitor hourly urine output (goal ≥ 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
- Reassess volume status and tissue perfusion frequently
- Source control — debridement, drainage of abscess if indicated
- Glycemic control — maintain glucose 140–180 mg/dL
- Nutritional support — early enteral nutrition

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Recognizing Early Sepsis in Agnes
Watch for the **QSOFA** criteria plus wound infection signs:

**Wound Assessment:**
- Increasing redness, warmth, swelling around incision
- Purulent (yellow/green, thick) drainage
- New or worsening pain at wound site
- Foul odor

**Systemic Signs of Early Sepsis:**
- Temperature rising (> 38°C) or falling (< 36°C in elderly)
- HR > 90
- RR > 20
- New confusion or worsening mental status (ESPECIALLY notable in Agnes at 81)
- Elevated WBC on labs
- Lactate elevated

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up; hip precautions — abductor wedge in place
- **W** — Wristband — two identifiers
- **I** — IV: two antibiotics (levofloxacin + vancomycin) — verify compatibility, timing, rates
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach
- **E** — Equipment: SCDs (sequential compression devices) on for DVT prevention? O2 available?

### Hip Precautions (ORIF) — Educate Agnes and Family
- **No hip flexion > 90 degrees** (do not bend hip past a right angle)
- **No internal rotation** of the operative leg
- **No crossing legs or ankles**
- **Abduction** must be maintained (abductor pillow between legs)
- **Call for assistance** before getting up

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Levofloxacin 500 mg IV Daily
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Fluoroquinolone antibiotic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits bacterial DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV |
| **Indication** | Broad-spectrum wound infection coverage |
| **Adverse effects** | Tendon rupture (Beers Criteria concern in elderly), QT prolongation, peripheral neuropathy, C. diff |
| **Nursing precautions** | Infuse over 60–90 minutes; assess for tendon pain; monitor ECG; caution in elderly |

---

### Vancomycin 1000 mg IV q12h
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Glycopeptide antibiotic |
| **Indication** | MRSA wound infection coverage |
| **Monitoring** | Trough level before 4th dose (goal 15–20 mcg/mL); BUN and creatinine (nephrotoxicity) |
| **Adverse effects** | Nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, Red Man Syndrome (if infused too quickly) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Infuse over at least 60 min; monitor trough levels; assess hearing |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Risk for Septic Shock** r/t wound infection progressing to systemic inflammatory response
   - AEB: fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, elevated WBC, wound with purulent drainage

2. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t surgical wound infection secondary to ORIF
   - AEB: redness, swelling, purulent drainage at incision site; malnutrition impairing healing

3. **Acute Pain** r/t surgical wound, inflammation, and infection
   - AEB: verbal report of increasing pain, guarded positioning

4. **Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements** r/t malnutrition secondary to poor intake and infection
   - AEB: malnutrition diagnosis; elevated infection risk; impaired wound healing

5. **Risk for Deep Vein Thrombosis/Pulmonary Embolism** r/t immobility and hip surgery
   - AEB: post-hip surgery; limited mobility; elderly
