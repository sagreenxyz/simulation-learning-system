## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes** that instructors evaluate:

1. **Recognition of end-of-life signs** — identifying physical changes that indicate imminent death
2. **Comfort-focused care, not curative care** — the nurse's role shifts entirely to palliative/hospice principles
3. **Spiritual and cultural sensitivity** — Samuel is Jewish; spiritual care and postmortem practices must respect this
4. **Support for a grieving family** — wife and son are present; communication, presence, and empathy are essential skills

The progression to understand:

```
Inoperable pulmonary adenocarcinoma
        ↓
Last round of chemotherapy → acute decline
        ↓
Patient's choice: Hospice admission (DNR)
        ↓
Comfort care only: Pain (Morphine) + Nausea (Metoclopramide) + Secretions (Glycopyrrolate)
        ↓
Active dying → Postmortem care + Family grief support
        ↓
Cultural/spiritual postmortem practices (Jewish faith)
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the physical manifestations at the end of life?

As a patient approaches death, the body undergoes systematic physical changes:

**Cardiovascular / Circulatory:**
- Mottling (bluish-purple discoloration) of skin, especially knees, feet, and hands
- Peripheral cyanosis (cool, bluish extremities)
- Weak, thready, irregular pulse
- Blood pressure drops progressively
- Extremities become cool and clammy

**Respiratory:**
- Cheyne-Stokes respirations — irregular breathing with periods of apnea (no breathing)
- Kussmaul breathing — deep, labored breathing
- "Death rattle" — noisy, wet breathing caused by secretion pooling in the throat (managed with glycopyrrolate)
- Increasing periods of apnea
- Mouth breathing, jaw relaxation

**Neurological / Consciousness:**
- Decreased level of consciousness → unresponsiveness
- Confusion, delirium, agitation (terminal restlessness)
- Decreased responsiveness to stimuli
- Eyes may remain partially open
- Hearing is believed to be the last sense to go

**Gastrointestinal / Renal:**
- Loss of bowel and bladder control (incontinence)
- Decreased urine output; dark, concentrated urine
- Cessation of oral intake (no hunger or thirst)
- Abdominal distension

**Skin / Musculoskeletal:**
- Pallor; yellowish or waxy appearance
- Skin becomes cool and mottled
- Jaw and facial muscles relax

> **Key insight:** Hearing is the LAST sense to diminish — always assume the patient can hear. Continue speaking to and about the patient with dignity at all times.

### Q2. What are 4 goals for end-of-life care?

1. **Comfort and pain management** — eliminate or minimize pain, dyspnea, nausea, and other distressing symptoms through appropriate pharmacological and non-pharmacological methods
2. **Dignity and respect** — honor the patient's wishes, autonomy, cultural and spiritual values, and sense of identity throughout the dying process
3. **Family support and communication** — provide emotional support, honest communication, and grief counseling to family members during and after the patient's death
4. **Peaceful and meaningful death** — create an environment that allows the patient to die in a manner consistent with their values, surrounded by loved ones, free from unnecessary interventions

---

### Q3. What are 3 legal and ethical considerations in end-of-life care?

1. **Advance Directives / Living Will:**
   - A legal document stating the patient's wishes regarding end-of-life care
   - Specifies what interventions are or are not desired (e.g., mechanical ventilation, CPR, tube feeding)
   - Must be honored by the healthcare team

2. **DNR Order (Do Not Resuscitate):**
   - A physician order based on the patient's wishes to withhold CPR and resuscitation efforts
   - Must be written as a formal order; verbal DNR orders alone are not sufficient
   - Samuel Green's DNR must be documented, accessible, and communicated to all team members
   - A DNR does NOT mean "do not treat" — comfort care continues fully

3. **Durable Power of Attorney for Healthcare (Healthcare Proxy):**
   - A legally designated person who makes medical decisions on the patient's behalf if they lose capacity
   - The nurse must identify the healthcare proxy and include them in all care decisions

**Additional considerations:**
- **Principle of Double Effect:** administering morphine to relieve pain may hasten death as a secondary (unintended) effect — this is ethically acceptable
- **Withholding vs. Withdrawing treatment:** both are ethically permissible when consistent with the patient's wishes
- **Truth-telling and honest communication:** families deserve honest prognosis while maintaining hope and dignity

### Q4. What are appropriate communication techniques when caring for a dying patient and their family?

**With the patient:**
- Speak gently and clearly, even if the patient appears unresponsive — hearing persists
- Tell the patient what you are doing before you do it
- Use therapeutic touch when culturally appropriate
- Acknowledge the patient's dignity: "Mr. Green, I'm going to give you some medicine to make you more comfortable."
- Avoid false reassurances; use honest, compassionate language

**With the family:**
- Identify the family spokesperson and understand the communication dynamics
- Provide a private, quiet space for conversations
- Acknowledge their grief: "This is such a difficult time. I want you to know we are here for all of you."
- Do NOT say "I know how you feel" — instead say "I can only imagine how difficult this is."
- Encourage expression of feelings, fears, and wishes
- Explain what the physical signs of dying mean — normalize and prepare them
- Offer to contact chaplaincy, social work, or a rabbi (for a Jewish family)
- Allow as much time as the family needs with the patient

**Practical phrases:**
- "Is there anything I can do for you right now?"
- "Is it important to you to have someone with Samuel at all times?"
- "Would you like me to call someone?"
- "Samuel can still hear you. It's okay to talk to him and say what's in your heart."

### Q5. What is the difference between hospice and palliative care?

| Feature | Palliative Care | Hospice Care |
|---|---|---|
| **Goal** | Relieve suffering alongside curative treatment | Comfort-focused; cure is no longer the goal |
| **When used** | Any stage of serious illness | Terminal illness with prognosis ≤ 6 months |
| **Curative treatment** | May continue | Discontinued |
| **Setting** | Hospital, clinic, home | Home, inpatient hospice facility, nursing home |
| **Medicare benefit** | May be covered separately | Covered under Medicare Hospice Benefit |

> **Samuel Green is in inpatient hospice** — curative treatment has stopped; all care is comfort-focused.

### Q6. What are Jewish end-of-life and postmortem practices the nurse should know?

Jewish faith has specific traditions surrounding death and dying:

**During dying:**
- A dying person (Goses) should not be left alone — family or a Shomer (watcher) should remain present
- Prayers may be recited at the bedside (Vidui — confessional prayer; Shema — central Jewish prayer)
- The patient may request to face Jerusalem during death

**Immediately after death:**
- Do NOT remove the body immediately — allow family time with the body
- Eyes should be gently closed by a family member if possible
- The body should not be left alone — a Shomer (guardian) may stay with the body
- The body should NOT be washed or prepared by non-Jewish staff without family permission — in traditional Judaism, body preparation is done by the Chevra Kadisha (Jewish burial society)
- Autopsy is generally avoided unless legally required
- Organ donation — views vary; consult family
- Burial should occur as soon as possible, ideally within 24 hours

**Nursing implications:**
- Ask the family if they have specific wishes regarding postmortem care
- Do not assume; ask respectfully: "Are there any religious or cultural traditions you would like us to follow for Mr. Green?"
- Coordinate with the hospital chaplain who can facilitate contact with a rabbi

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
- Knock before entering
- Perform hand hygiene
- Introduce yourself gently and quietly — the patient may be unconscious or near death
- Acknowledge the family immediately: "Mr. and Mrs. Green, I'm [name], your nurse. I want you to know I'm here for all of you."

### Priority Assessments for a Dying Patient
- Level of consciousness — is Samuel responsive?
- Respiratory assessment — rate, rhythm, pattern (Cheyne-Stokes?), secretions (death rattle?)
- Cardiovascular — pulse rate and strength, skin color, mottling
- Pain assessment — behavioral cues (facial grimacing, moaning, restlessness)
- Comfort assessment — is the patient showing signs of distress?
- Family emotional status — how are wife and son coping?

### Spiritual Sensitivity During Simulation
If Mrs. Green and her son are praying when you enter:
- **Wait quietly outside or at the doorway** until they have finished
- Do NOT interrupt prayer; do NOT walk up to the bedside during prayer
- When they finish, acknowledge: "I didn't want to interrupt — please take all the time you need."

### Pain and Symptom Management (During Simulation)
- Administer **Morphine** per order for pain — reassess within 30–60 minutes
- Administer **Metoclopramide** if nausea present
- Administer **Glycopyrrolate** for excessive secretions/death rattle — this reduces distress for the family as well as the patient
- Position for comfort — semi-Fowler's or lateral position to facilitate breathing
- Provide mouth care — keep lips and mouth moist (glycerin swabs)
- Reposition every 2 hours (or as tolerated) to prevent skin breakdown
- Minimize unnecessary interventions — do not wake patient for routine vitals if they are actively dying

### Postmortem Care Steps
After death is confirmed by the provider:
1. **Notify provider** to certify the death
2. **Document** time of death, provider notification, and all actions
3. **Allow family time** with the body — do not rush postmortem care
4. **Ask about cultural/religious wishes** before preparing the body
5. Wear gloves; prepare body with dignity:
   - Close eyes gently
   - Remove tubes and lines per facility policy (unless coroner case)
   - Position body supine with hands at sides or folded
   - Provide hygiene care
   - Place identification tags
6. **Support the family through grief** — offer chaplaincy, social work
7. **Document:**
   - Time and date of death
   - Name of provider certifying death
   - People notified (family, provider, charge nurse)
   - Actions taken
   - Disposition of the body
   - Patient's belongings and valuables

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. You are caring for a terminally ill patient from a different culture. Discuss the implications of culturally competent care.

Culturally competent end-of-life care requires the nurse to:
- **Ask, don't assume:** "What is most important to you right now?" "What are your beliefs about death and dying?"
- Recognize that expressions of grief vary widely across cultures — weeping, silence, prayer, ritualistic behaviors are all valid
- Patients' faith may alter their response to illness, treatment decisions, DNR status, organ donation, and postmortem practices
- Language barriers require interpreter services (never use family members as medical interpreters)
- Be present — active listening, silence, and therapeutic touch (when culturally appropriate) are the most valuable tools a nurse has
- Consult chaplaincy and culturally competent resources
- Ask: "Who provides the most support for you?" and "Which rituals or practices are important to you?"

---

### Q2. After completing postmortem care, what does the nurse document?

Required documentation after a patient's death:
- **Time and date of death** and all actions taken in response to impending death
- **Name of the provider certifying the death**
- People notified (family, provider, charge nurse, chaplain, coroner if applicable)
- **Condition of the body** at time of death
- **Postmortem care performed** and who performed it
- **Disposition of the body** (transferred to funeral home, morgue, etc.)
- **Personal belongings and valuables** — documented and transferred to family with signature
- **Family notification** — who was notified and at what time
- Any **cultural or religious accommodations** provided

### Q3. A nurse is developing a plan of care for Samuel Green. When addressing grief, which goal statement is most appropriate?

The most appropriate goal statement is one that is **patient/family-focused, realistic, and measurable**. For example:
- "Family will verbalize understanding of what to expect during the dying process by end of shift."
- "Patient will demonstrate signs of comfort (relaxed facial expression, absence of moaning) within 30 minutes of analgesic administration."
- "Family will identify at least one available support resource (chaplain, social worker) before the end of shift."

---

### Q4. Upon entering the room, the nurse hears Mrs. Green praying. What should the nurse do?

**B. Wait quietly outside the room until they are done praying.**

Prayer is a sacred act and should never be interrupted by routine nursing care. Standing quietly outside until prayer is finished demonstrates respect for the family's spiritual practices without abandoning the patient. Walking up to the bedside without invitation during prayer would be intrusive.

---

### Q5. What are Kübler-Ross's stages of grief?

The five stages identified by Dr. Elisabeth Kübler-Ross (**DABDA**):
1. **Denial** — "This can't be happening." Refusal to accept the diagnosis or situation.
2. **Anger** — "Why me? Why us?" Directing anger at providers, God, themselves, or others.
3. **Bargaining** — "If I can just make it to my granddaughter's wedding..." Attempting to negotiate more time.
4. **Depression** — Withdrawal, crying, mourning the upcoming loss.
5. **Acceptance** — Coming to terms with death; "I'm ready."

> **Key nursing point:** People do not move through these stages in a set order, and may move back and forth between stages. A nurse should meet the family where they are — not where you think they should be.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Morphine Sulfate (1 mg/mL) — IV/SQ
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Opioid analgesic (Schedule II) |
| **Indication (Samuel)** | Pain control and dyspnea relief in terminal pulmonary cancer |
| **Mechanism** | Binds opioid receptors in CNS; alters pain perception and response |
| **Principle of Double Effect** | Adequate doses to control pain are ethical even if they may secondarily hasten death — the intent is comfort, not euthanasia |
| **Adverse effects** | Respiratory depression, sedation, constipation, nausea, hypotension |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess pain and respiratory status before each dose; have naloxone available; in hospice, goal is comfort — respiratory depression is acceptable secondary to comfort |

---

### Metoclopramide
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Antiemetic / Dopamine antagonist / Prokinetic |
| **Indication (Samuel)** | Nausea and vomiting management; gastroparesis from cancer or opioids |
| **Adverse effects** | Extrapyramidal symptoms (restlessness, involuntary movements), drowsiness, diarrhea |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor for extrapyramidal effects; avoid in bowel obstruction |

---

### Glycopyrrolate
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Anticholinergic agent |
| **Indication (Samuel)** | Reduce excessive oral and respiratory secretions causing "death rattle" |
| **Mechanism** | Blocks muscarinic receptors → reduces secretion production |
| **Why it matters** | Death rattle is distressing for the family; glycopyrrolate reduces secretions without sedating the patient |
| **Adverse effects** | Dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, tachycardia, blurred vision |
| **Nursing precautions** | Does not dry up existing secretions; repositioning also helps; educate family that this sound does not indicate pain |

## 🕊️ HOSPICE PRINCIPLES — Key Concepts

**What hospice provides:**
- Pain and symptom management
- Spiritual care
- Emotional support for patient AND family
- Caregiver support and respite care
- Bereavement support after death

**What hospice does NOT provide:**
- Curative or aggressive life-prolonging treatments
- Resuscitation (per DNR)

**Interdisciplinary hospice team includes:**
- RN
- Physician / Medical Director
- Social Worker
- Chaplain / Spiritual Care
- Bereavement Counselor
- Home Health Aide
- Volunteers

## ⚠️ SIGNS OF IMMINENT DEATH (Actively Dying — Final Hours/Days)

- **Eyes:** Glazed, partially open; loss of blink reflex
- **Breathing:** Irregular, Cheyne-Stokes; death rattle; prolonged apnea
- **Circulation:** Mottled, cyanotic extremities; absent radial pulse; pallor or grayish skin
- **Consciousness:** Unresponsive or minimally responsive
- **Temperature:** Extremities cool, central body may be warm or feverish
- **Urine:** None or dark, minimal output
