## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Classify burns by depth and describe the appearance of partial-thickness burns.

**Burn Depth Classification:**

| Depth | Old Term | Appearance | Sensation | Healing |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Superficial** | First-degree | Red, dry, no blisters; sunburn-like | Painful | 3–5 days; no scarring |
| **Superficial Partial-Thickness** | Second-degree | Moist, pink/red, blistering; "dewdrop" blisters | Very painful (nerve endings intact) | 7–21 days; minimal scarring |
| **Deep Partial-Thickness** | Deep second-degree | Pale, mottled, wet; may have blisters; may need skin graft | Decreased sensation | 3–6 weeks; likely scarring |
| **Full-Thickness** | Third-degree | Leathery, white/brown/black, dry; eschar | Painless (nerves destroyed) | Does not heal without grafting |
| **Full-Thickness + Subdermal** | Fourth-degree | Charred; muscle, bone visible | Painless | Requires amputation/grafting |

**Dylan's partial-thickness burns:** Moist, blistered, painful; nerve endings intact; he WILL feel pain during dressing changes.

### Q2. What are the principles of burn wound care and dressing changes?

**Goals of Burn Wound Care:**
1. Prevent infection (most significant cause of burn morbidity/mortality)
2. Promote healing (moist wound environment)
3. Minimize pain
4. Prevent contracture formation (especially important for hand burns — affects function)

**Dressing Change Procedure:**
1. **Pre-medicate** — administer analgesic 30–60 minutes before dressing change; Dylan needs adequate pain control before the procedure begins
2. Gather all supplies before starting — minimize procedure time
3. Don sterile gloves; use sterile technique
4. **Gently remove old dressings** — soak if adherent (use NS to loosen)
5. **Wound assessment:** color, depth, size, drainage, signs of infection (increasing redness, odor, purulent exudate, fever)
6. **Clean wound** — gently irrigate with NS or prescribed wound cleanser
7. **Apply topical agent** (silver sulfadiazine or prescribed agent) — apply with sterile gloved hand or tongue depressor
8. **Apply non-adherent primary dressing** (Mepitel, Adaptic)
9. **Secondary dressing** — absorptive layer (gauze, foam)
10. **Secure dressing** — wrap fingers individually to maintain function; do not wrap too tightly (assess circulation)
11. Reassess pain after procedure

### Q3. How do you engage a reluctant 9-year-old in wound care?

**Child Life Specialist involvement** — invaluable for procedure preparation and support

**Nursing Strategies for School-Age Children (6–12 years):**
- **Explain beforehand** — school-age children understand and fear the unknown; preparation reduces anxiety
- "Dylan, I need to change the dressing on your hand. I'm going to give you medicine first so it doesn't hurt as much."
- "You can help me by holding very still. If it hurts, you can squeeze your mom's hand and tell me and we'll stop for a moment."
- **Give choices where possible** — "Do you want me to start with this finger or that one?" — control reduces anxiety
- **Distraction** — tablet/phone watching favorite video during procedure; music; conversation about interests
- **Guided imagery** — "Imagine you're at your favorite place..."
- **Counting** — "Let's count to 10 together"
- **Praise and reward** after: "You did an amazing job. That was really hard and you were so brave."
- **Never shame or threaten** — "Stop crying" or "Be a big boy" are never therapeutic

---

### Q4. How do you involve the mother in care?

**Why Family Involvement Matters:**
- Mother will need to change dressings at home after discharge — teach NOW while supported
- Parental anxiety can be transferred to child; a calm, informed parent helps calm the child
- Helps build trust in the healthcare team

**How to Involve Mother:**
- "Mrs. DiSabatino, I'd like to show you how we change Dylan's dressing today. You'll need to do this at home."
- Have her observe first, then participate progressively (handing supplies, holding Dylan's arm, then applying dressing)
- Written instructions to take home
- Signs of infection to watch for
- When to call the provider or return to the ED

**Addressing Guilt:**
- Parents often feel guilty about burn injuries regardless of circumstances
- "Accidents happen — what's important is that you got him help right away and you're here supporting him."
- Non-judgmental language throughout

---

### Q5. What complications are associated with hand burns?

1. **Infection** — breaks in skin → bacterial entry; monitor for signs
2. **Contracture** — scar tissue formation → loss of hand mobility if not treated with splinting and physical therapy
3. **Hypertrophic scarring** — raised, thick scars; more common in partial and deep burns
4. **Compartment syndrome** — from excessive edema (especially circumferential burns)
5. **Functional impairment** — loss of fine motor function if burns not managed optimally

**Prevention:**
- Appropriate dressings to maintain moist wound environment
- Occupational therapy (OT) consultation for hand burns — splinting, ROM exercises
- Pressure garments after healing to reduce hypertrophic scarring

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### Before Dressing Change
1. **Pre-medicate** — confirm pain medication administered and effective (reassess pain 30 min after giving medication)
2. Gather ALL supplies before entering room — minimize interruptions
3. Explain procedure to Dylan and mother in age-appropriate terms
4. Identify distraction strategy — what does Dylan like?
5. Position: sitting comfortably; arm supported

### During Dressing Change
1. Don sterile gloves
2. Soak old dressing if adherent
3. Remove slowly while engaging Dylan in conversation or distraction
4. Assess wound: document appearance, progress toward healing
5. Clean wound gently — NS irrigation
6. Apply silver sulfadiazine and non-adherent dressing
7. Wrap fingers individually (allows movement)
8. Monitor Dylan's pain continuously — pause if needed

### After Dressing Change
1. Praise Dylan genuinely
2. Reassess pain level
3. Begin discharge teaching with mother
4. Document wound assessment findings

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Priority action before a burn dressing change?
**Administer pain medication** 30–60 minutes before the procedure. Burn dressing changes are among the most painful procedures patients experience. Adequate pre-medication is both humane and necessary for a successful dressing change.

### Q2. What are signs of infection in a burn wound?
Increasing redness spreading beyond wound edges, purulent drainage, foul odor, fever, increasing pain, elevated WBC.

### Q3. Why does Dylan need occupational therapy?
Hand burns risk **contracture formation** — scar tissue can permanently limit finger and wrist mobility. OT provides splinting and range-of-motion exercises to preserve function during healing.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Silver Sulfadiazine (Silvadene) — Topical
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Topical antimicrobial |
| **Mechanism** | Releases silver ions → broad-spectrum bactericidal activity |
| **Indication** | Prevention and treatment of burn wound infection |
| **Application** | Apply 1/16-inch layer with sterile gloved hand; cover with non-adherent dressing |
| **Adverse effects** | Leukopenia (monitor WBC with prolonged use), sulfa sensitivity, skin discoloration |
| **Contraindications** | Sulfa allergy; near term pregnancy; premature infants |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess for sulfa allergy before use; WBC monitoring with prolonged use |
