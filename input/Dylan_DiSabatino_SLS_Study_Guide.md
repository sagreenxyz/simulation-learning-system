## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Describe the classification of burn wounds and how they differ.

Burns are classified by the **depth of tissue destruction**:

**Superficial (First-Degree):**
- Involves only the **epidermis**
- Appearance: red, dry, no blisters; painful
- Example: sunburn
- Heals in 3–5 days; no scarring

**Partial-Thickness (Second-Degree):**
- Involves the **epidermis and part of the dermis**
- *Superficial partial-thickness:* blisters, moist, bright pink/red, very painful; heals in 10–21 days
- *Deep partial-thickness:* pale/white, less painful (nerve damage), may require grafting; heals in 21–35 days
- **Dylan's burns are partial-thickness** — affecting the right hand and fingers; presence of blistering and significant pain confirms this classification

**Full-Thickness (Third-Degree):**
- Destroys the **entire epidermis and dermis**; may involve subcutaneous tissue
- Appearance: white, brown, or black; leathery; **painless** (nerve endings destroyed)
- Requires skin grafting; scarring expected

**Fourth-Degree:**
- Extends into **muscle, fascia, or bone**
- Always requires surgical intervention

### Q2. How is burn size estimated, and what is the pediatric modification?

**Rule of Nines (Adults):**
- Head = 9%; Each arm = 9%; Anterior trunk = 18%; Posterior trunk = 18%; Each leg = 18%; Perineum = 1%

**Pediatric Modification — Lund and Browder Chart:**
- In children, the **head is proportionally larger** and the **legs are smaller** compared to adults
- The Lund and Browder chart adjusts percentages by age:
  - In infants: head = 19%; each leg = 13%
  - As the child grows, head percentage decreases and leg percentage increases
- For a 9-year-old: head ≈ 13%; each leg ≈ 16%

**Palm Method:**
- The patient's palm (including fingers) = approximately **1% TBSA**
- Useful for estimating small, irregular burns like Dylan's hand injury

**Clinical significance:**
- TBSA guides fluid resuscitation decisions (**Parkland Formula**: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA in first 24 hours; half given in first 8 hours)
- Dylan's hand burns are relatively small in TBSA; fluid resuscitation is less of an acute concern at day 4, but wound care and infection prevention are primary

### Q3. What is the correct procedure for a sterile burn wound dressing change?

**Pre-procedure:**
1. Assess current pain score; administer ordered pre-procedural analgesia **30–60 minutes before** the procedure
2. Gather all supplies before entering the room — minimize interruptions
3. Explain the procedure to Dylan and his mother in age-appropriate terms
4. Offer a distraction strategy (tablet, music, conversation)
5. Perform hand hygiene; don PPE

**Procedure:**
1. Remove the old dressing gently — **soak with saline if adherent** to prevent tearing healing tissue
2. Assess wound: color, moisture, granulation tissue, blistering, signs of infection (odor, purulent exudate, increasing erythema, warmth)
3. Gently cleanse wound with normal saline or mild soap and water per protocol
4. Pat dry with sterile gauze
5. Apply thin layer of **silver sulfadiazine 1% cream** (or prescribed topical agent) using sterile gloves and sterile tongue depressor or gauze
6. Cover with non-adherent primary dressing (e.g., Mepitel, Adaptic)
7. Apply secondary absorbent dressing layer
8. Secure with rolled gauze (Kerlix) and tape or cohesive wrap — **avoid circumferential tightness** (risk of neurovascular compromise)
9. Document: wound appearance, dressing applied, pain score before and after

**Post-procedure:**
- Reassess pain score
- Praise Dylan specifically for cooperation
- Provide discharge/home care instructions to mother

### Q4. What are the signs of wound infection in a burn patient, and when should the provider be notified?

**Normal wound healing signs (expected at day 4):**
- Moist wound bed with pink granulation tissue
- Clear to slightly yellow serous drainage
- Mild surrounding erythema (1–2 cm from wound edges)
- Wound margins beginning to epithelialize

**Signs of wound infection (report immediately):**
- **Purulent or green/yellow thick exudate** — bacterial colonization
- **Increasing erythema, warmth, edema** beyond wound edges
- **Foul odor**
- **Fever** (>38.5°C / 101.3°F in a child)
- **Wound color changes** — darkening, pale gray, or black eschar forming in previously partial-thickness wound
- **Conversion** of burn depth (partial → full-thickness) — suggests infection or poor perfusion
- **Increased or disproportionate pain** uncontrolled by analgesia

**Common organisms in burn wounds:**
- *Staphylococcus aureus* (including MRSA)
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*
- *Streptococcus pyogenes*

**Notify provider if:** fever, purulent drainage, wound conversion, or systemic signs (tachycardia, irritability, decreased intake) are present.

### Q5. What developmental considerations apply to a 9-year-old (school-age child) in a healthcare setting?

**Developmental stage:** School-age (Erikson: *Industry vs. Inferiority*; Piaget: *Concrete Operational*)

**Cognitive:**
- Understands cause and effect — can grasp *why* wound care is necessary
- Thinks in concrete terms; benefits from demonstration and visual aids
- Can understand and follow multi-step instructions

**Psychosocial:**
- Sense of **mastery and control** is essential — feeling powerless increases fear and resistance
- Peer approval is important; emphasize getting back to school/activities/sports
- May show bravery around others but feel frightened internally

**Communication strategies:**
- Use clear, honest, simple language — avoid minimizing ("it won't hurt at all")
- Give **limited choices** to restore sense of control (e.g., "Do you want me to start with the thumb or the pinky?")
- Negotiate and involve Dylan: "What would make this easier for you?"
- Use distraction actively: tablet, headphones, storytelling, deep breathing
- Praise specific behaviors: "You held really still — that was very helpful"

**Pain:**
- Use age-appropriate pain scale (Numeric 0–10 scale is appropriate for age 9+)
- Pre-medicate adequately — procedural pain in burns is significant
- Do NOT proceed with dressing change if pre-procedural analgesia has not had time to take effect

**Mother's role:**
- Encourage mother to remain in the room if Dylan wishes
- Coach mother on how to support (distract, hold hand, avoid projecting anxiety)
- Teach mother dressing change technique for home; return demonstration expected

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene
2. Introduce yourself to Dylan and his mother — address Dylan directly first ("Hi Dylan, I'm your nurse today")
3. Verify identity using two identifiers
4. Perform SWIPE safety assessment
5. Assess pain score immediately using numeric scale — "Dylan, on a scale of 0 to 10, where 0 is no pain and 10 is the worst pain ever, where is your pain right now?"

### Pre-Procedure Steps
1. Confirm pre-procedural analgesia has been given and has had adequate time to work (30–60 minutes)
2. If analgesia not yet given or ineffective — **do not begin dressing change**; notify provider
3. Gather all dressing supplies before returning to room
4. Explain procedure to Dylan in simple, concrete language: "I'm going to take the old bandage off your hand, clean the burn, and put on a fresh bandage. It might sting for a few minutes but I'll try to make it as comfortable as possible."
5. Offer distraction: "Would you like to watch something on a tablet while I work?"
6. Give Dylan a limited choice: "Is it okay if we start now, or do you need one more minute?"

### Dressing Change Steps
1. Don PPE; open supplies using sterile technique
2. Remove old dressing slowly — soak with saline if adherent; do not pull
3. Assess wound — verbalize findings: healing progress, presence/absence of infection signs
4. Cleanse wound gently with saline; pat dry
5. Apply silver sulfadiazine or ordered topical agent using sterile technique
6. Apply non-adherent primary layer, absorbent secondary layer, and wrap with Kerlix
7. Check neurovascular status of fingers after wrapping: cap refill, sensation, movement, color, warmth
8. Document wound findings and procedure in EHR

### Teaching Points for Mother
- How to change dressing at home (return demonstration)
- Timing of pre-medication before home dressing changes
- Signs of infection to report immediately
- Importance of protecting the hand (no submersion in water; no rough play)
- Follow-up appointment schedule

### Q2. What neurovascular checks are performed after wrapping a burned extremity?

After applying a dressing to the hand and fingers, assess the **5 P's of neurovascular status**:

- **Pain** — increasing pain may indicate compartment syndrome or impaired circulation
- **Pallor** — compare color to unaffected hand; pallor or cyanosis indicates poor perfusion
- **Pulselessness** — palpate radial pulse; assess capillary refill (< 2 seconds normal)
- **Paresthesia** — ask Dylan if his fingers feel numb or tingly
- **Paralysis** — ask Dylan to wiggle his fingers; inability may indicate nerve compromise

Notify provider immediately if dressing is too tight or neurovascular compromise is detected — loosen dressing as needed while awaiting orders.

### Q3. What are the key elements of discharge teaching for Dylan and his mother?

- **Dressing changes:** Perform every 24–48 hours or as ordered; pre-medicate Dylan 30–60 minutes before; use sterile technique; return demonstration completed before discharge
- **Infection signs:** Fever >101°F, increased redness/swelling/warmth beyond wound, green/yellow discharge, foul odor — go to ER or call provider
- **Activity restrictions:** No submersion (no baths, pools, water play) until wound is fully healed; protect hand from trauma
- **Diet/hydration:** Adequate protein and fluid intake supports wound healing
- **Follow-up:** Return to clinic in 48–72 hours for wound reassessment; notify provider sooner if concerns arise
- **Emotional support:** It is normal for Dylan to feel anxious; maintain consistent routines and praise cooperation
