## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **PCA management** — understanding how PCA works, who can press the button (ONLY the patient), monitoring for respiratory depression, and managing side effects
2. **Postoperative colon resection care** — wound care, GI recovery assessment, fluid management, early mobilization, and patient education
3. **Fluid volume deficit (MED-24)** — recognizing signs of dehydration/hypovolemia in the postoperative period and intervening appropriately

The postoperative care priorities:

```
Colon resection surgery completed
        ↓
Postoperative Period — Patient in bed with PCA running
        ↓
Assess: Pain (PCA effectiveness) + Respiratory status (opioid depression risk)
        ↓
Assess: Wound (incision integrity, drainage)
        ↓
Assess: GI recovery (bowel sounds, passage of gas, nausea)
        ↓
Assess: Fluid/Electrolyte status (I&O, hydration, wound drainage)
        ↓
Educate: PCA use, wound care, early ambulation, diet progression
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is a Patient-Controlled Analgesia (PCA) pump and how does it work?

**Definition:** PCA is a computerized, programmable drug delivery system that allows patients to self-administer small, preset doses of opioid analgesic (morphine, hydromorphone, fentanyl) by pressing a button.

**How it works:**
- Connected directly to the patient's IV line
- The physician programs the parameters:
  - **Demand (bolus) dose:** Amount delivered when button is pressed (Lou Thao: 1 mg morphine)
  - **Lockout interval:** Minimum time between doses (10 minutes for Lou Thao) — prevents overdose
  - **Background/continuous infusion:** Optional continuous low-dose infusion (not always ordered)
  - **1-hour and 4-hour limits:** Maximum amount deliverable in a time period
- When the patient presses the button during the lockout period, NO medication is delivered

**Advantages over scheduled nursing medication administration:**
- Patient has control over timing — can press button BEFORE pain becomes severe or before activities
- Faster onset than waiting for nurse to prepare and deliver PRN medication
- Studies show PCA provides better pain control and higher patient satisfaction
- Reduces staff burden and delays
- Patient self-titrates to their individual needs

---

### Q2. Who can press the PCA button? Why is this critical?

**ONLY THE PATIENT** can press the PCA button. This is one of the most important safety rules with PCA.

**Why:**
- The safety mechanism of PCA is the **patient's level of sedation** — if the patient becomes too sedated (from too much opioid), they will NOT be able to press the button → doses stop automatically
- If a family member, nurse, or caregiver presses the button when the patient is asleep or unable to respond appropriately, the natural safety mechanism is bypassed
- This "PCA by proxy" has caused respiratory depression, cardiac arrest, and death

**Nurse responsibility:**
- Educate patient AND family clearly: "ONLY Lou should press this button. If she is asleep, do not press it for her."
- Post signage at the bedside
- Document education

### Q3. What assessments must the nurse perform for a patient on PCA?

**Respiratory Assessment (PRIORITY — opioid overdose risk):**
- Respiratory rate (normal: 12–20 breaths/min)
- Quality of respirations — depth, effort
- SpO2 — maintain ≥94%
- Level of sedation — use POSS or Ramsay Sedation Scale
- **Sedation precedes respiratory depression** — monitor for drowsiness before RR drops
- **HOLD PCA and notify provider if:**
  - RR < 10 breaths/min
  - SpO2 < 90%
  - Sedation score indicates difficult to arouse
  - Have **naloxone (Narcan)** at bedside for emergency reversal

**Pain Assessment:**
- Numeric pain scale (0–10) or FACES scale
- Location, character, radiation, aggravating/alleviating factors
- Reassess 30–60 minutes after dose (IV onset 5–15 min, peak 30 min)
- Determine if PCA is achieving adequate pain control

**GI Assessment (post-colon resection):**
- Bowel sounds in all 4 quadrants
- Has patient passed flatus? (First sign of GI recovery — before oral diet can resume)
- Nausea/vomiting (opioid side effect; also impairs oral intake)
- Abdominal distension

**Wound Assessment:**
- Inspect abdominal incision: approximated, intact, signs of infection (redness, swelling, drainage, warmth, odor)
- Type, amount, and character of any wound drainage
- Dressing integrity

**Fluid Status:**
- I&O (input vs output balance)
- Skin turgor, mucous membrane moisture
- Urine output ≥ 30 mL/hr
- Daily weight
- IV site integrity

### Q4. What are the nursing diagnoses for a patient post-colon resection with PCA?

1. **Acute Pain** r/t surgical tissue disruption secondary to colon resection
   - AEB: patient reports pain, guarded positioning, reluctance to cough/deep breathe

2. **Risk for Respiratory Depression** r/t opioid administration via PCA
   - AEB: morphine PCA in use; sedation monitoring required

3. **Risk for Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t surgical incision and immobility
   - AEB: abdominal incision present; limited mobility due to pain

4. **Risk for Infection** r/t surgical wound
   - AEB: abdominal incision; bowel surgery (contamination risk)

5. **Risk for Fluid Volume Deficit** (MED-24) r/t NPO status, wound drainage, third-spacing
   - AEB: postoperative; limited oral intake; surgical blood loss

6. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t PCA operation, wound care, activity restrictions, and diet progression
   - AEB: first postoperative experience; new PCA user

---

### Q5. What are the common side effects of morphine PCA and how does the nurse manage them?

| Side Effect | Management |
|---|---|
| **Respiratory depression** | Monitor RR and SpO2; naloxone at bedside; HOLD PCA if RR < 10 |
| **Constipation** | Most common long-term side effect; start bowel regimen early; encourage ambulation; adequate fluids |
| **Nausea/vomiting** | Administer antiemetic as ordered; assess for ileus; consider switching opioid |
| **Pruritus (itching)** | Diphenhydramine or low-dose naloxone if severe; cool compresses |
| **Urinary retention** | Monitor urinary output; bladder scan if no void in 8 hours; catheterization if needed |
| **Sedation** | Reduce opioid dose; maximize non-opioid adjuncts (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) |
| **Hypotension** | Position change slowly; IV fluids if ordered; monitor BP |

### Q6. Describe the key postoperative nursing interventions for colon resection patients.

**Pain Management:**
- Assess pain q2–4h and PRN; ensure PCA understanding; multimodal analgesia (morphine + acetaminophen)

**Respiratory Care:**
- Incentive spirometry 10 breaths every 1–2 hours while awake
- Cough and deep breathe every 2 hours (splint abdomen with pillow for comfort)
- Position with HOB elevated 30–45 degrees

**Wound Care:**
- Assess incision every shift
- Change dressings per order; sterile technique
- Monitor for signs of infection: REEDA (redness, edema, ecchymosis, discharge, approximation)

**GI Recovery:**
- Monitor for return of bowel function: bowel sounds, passage of gas/stool
- NPO → clear liquids → full liquids → soft diet → regular diet (progression varies)
- Measure abdominal girth if distension suspected
- Nasogastric tube monitoring if present

**Mobility:**
- Dangle at bedside day of surgery if stable
- Ambulate with assistance first time; fall precautions
- Progress ambulation each day
- Patient education: avoid heavy lifting for 6–8 weeks

**Fluid Management:**
- IV fluids until tolerating oral fluids
- Monitor I&O, weight, BMP
- Assess for fluid deficit signs: decreased urine output, dry mouth, tachycardia, hypotension

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up
- **W** — Wristband — two identifiers
- **I** — IV line: check PCA pump settings (demand dose, lockout, 4-hour limit); check IV site
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach — patient must know to call before getting up
- **E** — Equipment: PCA pump functioning? Naloxone at bedside?

### PCA Pump Verification Checklist (Perform with Another Nurse — High Alert Medication)
1. Verify patient identity (two identifiers)
2. Check provider order against pump programming
   - Drug: morphine
   - Concentration: correct per pharmacy label
   - Demand dose: 1 mg
   - Lockout interval: 10 minutes
   - Maximum dose per hour/4 hours
3. Ensure the pump is running; battery functional
4. Check tubing for air bubbles, kinks, proper connections
5. Confirm anti-siphon valve in place
6. Document verification with co-signature

### Pain Management Education for Lou Thao
- "Press the button before pain gets to a 7 or 8 out of 10 — don't wait until it's unbearable"
- "Press it about 10 minutes before you need to cough or move"
- "Do NOT let family press this button — only you"
- "If your pain is not controlled, tell me and we can talk to your doctor about adjusting the dose"
- "Common side effects include feeling sleepy, nausea, constipation, and itching — tell me about any of these"

### Fluid Volume Deficit Recognition (MED-24 scenario)
**Signs:**
- Decreased urine output (< 30 mL/hr)
- Urine is dark yellow/amber and concentrated
- Increased heart rate (tachycardia)
- Decreased blood pressure
- Dry mucous membranes
- Decreased skin turgor
- Weight loss (1 kg = 1 liter of fluid deficit)
- Increased BUN (BUN/creatinine ratio > 20:1)

**Interventions:**
- Notify provider
- Increase IV fluid rate per order
- Assess all sources of output (wound drainage, NG tube, vomiting)
- Obtain labs: BMP, CBC

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Which patient action indicates PCA teaching is effective?
**B.) "I feel less anxiety about the possibility of overdosing."** — The safety features of PCA (lockout interval, 4-hour limit) are designed specifically to prevent overdose. The patient should understand these protect them. They cannot receive medication as frequently as they want (C is wrong). They do not need the nurse to notify them (D is wrong). PCA is typically not the only pain medication needed (A is wrong).

---

### Q2. A patient on PCA is difficult to arouse and has RR of 8. Priority action:
1. **STOP the PCA pump**
2. **Stimulate the patient** — call name, sternal rub
3. **Apply supplemental oxygen**
4. **Assess SpO2 and vital signs**
5. **Administer naloxone (Narcan)** per standing order or as ordered
6. **Call for help / rapid response** if not responding
7. **Document** all findings and interventions

---

### Q3. What is the priority action for a postoperative patient reporting that the PCA is "not working"?
1. Assess the patient's pain level and respiratory status
2. Check the PCA pump — is it powered on? Is the tubing connected? Is there medication in the syringe?
3. Check the demand dose history — is the patient pressing the button? Are doses being delivered?
4. Verify the IV site is patent (infiltration prevents medication delivery)
5. Review the current order — is the dose appropriate?
6. Notify the provider if pain is uncontrolled after troubleshooting

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Morphine Sulfate — PCA
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Opioid analgesic (Schedule II) — HIGH ALERT MEDICATION |
| **Mechanism** | Binds mu-opioid receptors in CNS → inhibits pain signal transmission |
| **Indication** | Moderate to severe postoperative pain |
| **PCA settings** | Demand dose 1 mg; lockout 10 min; 20 mg max/4 hours |
| **Onset/Peak** | IV onset: 5–15 min / Peak: 30 min |
| **Adverse effects** | Respiratory depression ⚠️, sedation, constipation, N/V, pruritus, urinary retention, hypotension |
| **Antidote** | Naloxone (Narcan) 0.4–2 mg IV |
| **Lab monitoring** | Respiratory rate, SpO2, sedation score; no specific lab value |
| **Nursing precautions** | Double-check programming with another RN; only patient presses button; have naloxone at bedside; monitor sedation before RR |

---

### Azithromycin (Zithromax) 500 mg IV Piggyback
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Macrolide antibiotic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits bacterial protein synthesis |
| **Indication** | Prevention or treatment of infection post-colon resection surgery |
| **Route** | IV Piggyback — reconstituted and diluted; infuse over 1 hour (500 mg in 250 mL at 250 mL/hr) or 3 hours (at 1 mg/mL) |
| **Frequency** | Once daily (q24h) |
| **Adverse effects** | Nausea, abdominal pain, diarrhea, QT prolongation (cardiac monitoring may be needed), hepatotoxicity |
| **Nursing precautions** | Infuse over correct time period (do NOT give as IV bolus); monitor for GI side effects; check for cardiac history |
| **Patient teaching** | Report diarrhea; report palpitations; complete full course |
