## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four interlocking themes**:

1. **G-tube emergency** — Alice's G-tube feeding has become disconnected just before the scenario begins; the student must recognize and respond to this immediately
2. **Skin integrity at G-tube site** — the disconnected feeding has caused a saturated dressing and wet bed pad; wound care must be performed
3. **Pediatric fever management** — Alice has an elevated temperature; an antipyretic must be administered via G-tube using correct pediatric dosing
4. **Family-centered care** — Alice's mother is her primary caregiver and the expert on her daily routine; the nurse must partner with her, not override her

The cause-and-effect chain:

```
Esophageal atresia (congenital)
        ↓

        ↓
G-tube feeding becomes disconnected

        ↓

        ↓
```

## 🏥 Background: Esophageal Atresia

**What is esophageal atresia?**
A congenital anomaly in which the esophagus does not develop properly — the upper and lower portions of the esophagus are separated and do not connect. The infant cannot swallow and is at high risk for aspiration. Most cases (85%) involve a tracheoesophageal fistula (TEF), an abnormal connection between the esophagus and trachea.

**Why does Alice have a G-tube?**
Because her esophagus cannot transport food to the stomach normally, a gastrostomy tube (G-tube) was surgically placed to deliver nutrition directly into the stomach, bypassing the esophagus entirely.

**Key facts about esophageal atresia:**
- Congenital — present from birth
- Associated with VACTERL association (Vertebral, Anorectal, Cardiac, Tracheoesophageal fistula, Esophageal atresia, Renal, Limb anomalies)
- Surgical repair is required; some children require lifelong nutritional support
- Prophylactic antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin) are commonly used in the management of esophageal atresia
- Ranitidine addresses gastroesophageal reflux (GERD), which is common in these patients

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is a gastrostomy tube (G-tube) and why is it used?

A **gastrostomy tube (G-tube)** is a medical device surgically inserted through the abdominal wall directly into the stomach. It is used when a patient cannot take nutrition orally due to:
- Swallowing disorders (esophageal atresia, as in Alice)
- Neurological conditions that impair the swallow reflex
- Structural abnormalities of the GI tract
- Inadequate oral intake due to illness, failure to thrive, or prematurity

**For Alice:** Her esophageal atresia means she cannot safely swallow and pass food through her esophagus to her stomach. The G-tube allows her to receive adequate nutrition, hydration, and medications while bypassing the non-functional esophagus.

---

### Q2. How do you care for a G-tube and its insertion site?

**Daily G-tube site care:**
1. Assess the site each shift for signs of irritation, infection, or leakage
2. Clean the site with warm water and mild soap (or per facility protocol)
3. Rotate the tube gently to prevent adhesion to the tract
4. Ensure the external bumper/disc is not too tight — 1–2 finger widths between bumper and skin
5. Apply a clean, dry dressing if ordered (gauze or foam dressing around the stoma)
6. Leave the site **open to air** when not draining — do not cover unless ordered
7. Monitor for:
   - **Redness, warmth, swelling** (infection)
   - **Granulation tissue** (overgrowth of tissue around stoma — common and usually benign)
   - **Leakage** of gastric contents (skin breakdown risk)
   - **Tube displacement** — tube has moved out of position

**Key site assessment findings in Alice:**
- G-tube feeding disconnected → formula leaked → **saturated dressing** and **wet bed pad**
- This moisture exposure can cause skin maceration and breakdown around the stoma
- **Priority action:** Clean and dry the site; apply fresh dressing; reconnect tube feeding

---

### Q3. How do you administer medications via G-tube?

1. **Verify tube placement** — aspirate for gastric contents before medication administration; confirm placement via X-ray if recently placed or if repositioned
2. **Flush first** — flush the tube with 10–30 mL of water (use 5–10 mL for children) before giving medications to ensure patency
3. **Administer medications one at a time** — give each medication separately; flush with water between each medication
4. **Use liquid formulations** whenever possible — avoid crushing enteric-coated or extended-release tablets
5. **Flush after** — flush with 10–30 mL water (5–10 mL for children) after the last medication to clear the tube and ensure full dose delivery
6. **Clamp the tube** for 20–30 minutes after medication administration if connected to suction
7. **Document** the medication, route, time, and patient response

> **For Alice:** Acetaminophen or ibuprofen suspension will be given via G-tube. Confirm the tube is patent, flush before, administer the correct weight-based dose, and flush after.

---

### Q4. How do you reconnect and restart a G-tube feeding?

**Steps to restart a disconnected G-tube feeding:**
1. **Identify what happened** — assess the site and tubing; look for kinks, disconnection points
2. **Clean the site** — wipe away any leaked formula; clean the stoma and surrounding skin
3. **Change the dressing** if saturated (as in Alice's case)
4. **Check residual** (gastric residual volume, or GRV) before restarting — aspirate gently with a syringe; if residual is greater than facility policy (often 100–200 mL in adults or 1–2 hours' worth in children), hold the feeding and notify the provider
5. **Reconnect the feeding tubing** — ensure it is securely attached to the G-tube port
6. **Verify the formula** — correct formula, correct rate, correct concentration
7. **Restart the pump** — set the rate per the order
8. **Position the patient** — head of bed elevated 30–45 degrees to reduce aspiration risk
9. **Document** the reconnection, site assessment, and feeding restart

### Q5. How do you perform age-appropriate assessment and communication with a 7-year-old child?

**Developmental stage:** Alice is a **school-age child (6–12 years)**. According to Erikson, this is the stage of **Industry vs. Inferiority** — children this age are developing competence and want to feel capable and successful. They can cooperate when respected and involved.

**Communication strategies:**
- Use **simple, concrete language** — avoid medical jargon
- **Explain what you are going to do before you do it**: "I'm going to listen to your breathing now. You'll just feel the stethoscope — it might be a little cold."
- **Give choices when possible** to maintain sense of control: "Would you like me to check your belly first or listen to your chest first?"
- **Praise cooperation**: "You are doing a great job. Thank you for being so still."
- **Use dolls or pictures** to explain procedures
- **Allow the child to hold equipment** if appropriate (stethoscope, dressing supplies) — reduces fear
- **Direct questions to Alice first**, then confirm with mother — she is old enough to participate
- Respect **privacy** — school-age children are modest
- **Acknowledge pain and fear honestly** — do not say "This won't hurt" if it will; say "This might feel a little uncomfortable for a second."

**Pain assessment for a 7-year-old:**
- Use the **FACES Pain Scale** (Wong-Baker FACES) — appropriate for children ages 3–7+
- **Numeric scale (0–10)** can also be used for school-age children as they understand numbers
- Assess for **behavioral cues** as well: facial grimacing, guarding, crying, restlessness

### Q6. What is the nurse's role when the parent is the primary caregiver?

**Family-centered care** is the standard of practice in pediatric nursing. Key principles:

- **Respect the parent as the expert** — Alice's mother knows her routines, feeding schedule, medication history, and baseline behavior better than any nurse
- **Partner, do not override** — do not dismiss the mother's knowledge; ask "What does Alice usually do at home when this happens?"
- **Include the mother in assessments and care** — she may perform the G-tube care at home; allow her to demonstrate and then reinforce correct technique
- **Teach and reinforce** — use this opportunity to provide education about signs of infection, fever management, and when to call the provider
- **Explain everything** — mother may be anxious; clear explanations reduce fear and build trust
- **Do not talk about Alice as if she is not in the room** — include Alice in all age-appropriate conversations

> **Key phrase to use in simulation:** "Mrs. Morrison, you know Alice best. Can you tell me how her G-tube feedings usually go at home? Has she ever had the feeding disconnect before?"

### Q7. What are the signs and symptoms of acute bronchitis in a child?

**Acute bronchitis:** Inflammation of the bronchial tubes, usually caused by a viral infection (most commonly)

**Signs and symptoms:**
- **Cough** — productive or dry; may last 1–3 weeks
- **Low-grade fever** (elevated temperature — key finding in this scenario)
- **Increased respiratory rate** (tachypnea)
- **Wheezing** or rhonchi on auscultation
- **Increased mucus production**
- **Sore throat, runny nose** — often precede bronchitis
- **Malaise and fatigue**
- **Decreased appetite**

**What to monitor in Alice specifically:**
- Respiratory rate (normal for 7-year-old: 18–25 breaths/min)
- SpO2 — should be ≥95%
- Temperature — elevated; will need antipyretic via G-tube
- Lung sounds — listen for wheezing, rhonchi, decreased breath sounds
- Work of breathing — nasal flaring, intercostal retractions, accessory muscle use
- Cough — character, frequency, whether it is productive

### Q9. What are weight-based dosing considerations for pediatric medications?

**Pediatric medications are dosed by weight (mg/kg)** — using adult doses in children can cause serious harm or death.

**Acetaminophen (pediatric):**
- Dosage: 10–15 mg/kg per dose PO/G-tube q4–6h PRN
- Maximum: **5 doses in 24 hours** (do not exceed 75 mg/kg/day)
- Available as 100 mg/mL suspension for Alice

**Ibuprofen (pediatric):**
- Dosage: 5–10 mg/kg per dose PO/G-tube q6–8h PRN
- Available as 20 mg/mL suspension
- Do NOT give to children under 6 months
- Caution: can cause GI irritation; give with food/formula in G-tube patients

**Gentamicin (IV):**
- Aminoglycoside — requires careful weight-based dosing and therapeutic drug monitoring
- Narrow therapeutic window — toxicity risk (nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity)
- Monitor: peak and trough levels, BUN, creatinine, urine output, hearing changes

**Ampicillin (IV):**
- Aminopenicillin — broad-spectrum antibiotic
- Covers gram-positive organisms and some gram-negatives
- Commonly paired with gentamicin for esophageal atresia management

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Priority Action
**⚠️ The G-tube feeding is disconnected when you walk in.** This is the immediate issue you must identify and address.

**First priority:** Do NOT walk past a disconnected feeding. Recognize it, address it calmly, and communicate to the mother what you are doing.

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails: Up on appropriate sides (pediatric patients require rails up at all times when unattended)
- **W** — Wristband: Verify Alice's identity with two identifiers (name + DOB or medical record number)
- **I** — IV lines: Check IV access; check Gentamicin IVPB infusion if running
- **P** — Pull cord/call light: Ensure within mother's reach (child may not be able to use it alone)
- **E** — Equipment: Bed in lowest position; ensure G-tube feeding pump is present; bed rails appropriate for child

### Head-to-Toe Assessment — Pediatric Priorities

**General:**
- How does Alice look? Is she in distress? Playful? Lethargic?
- Level of alertness and activity — is she responding appropriately for a 7-year-old?
- Color: pale, flushed (from fever), cyanotic?

**Temperature — Priority Finding:**
- Alice has an **elevated temperature** — recognize this as abnormal
- Assess temperature route (tympanic, axillary, oral, temporal)
- Initiate fever management per order: administer acetaminophen or ibuprofen via G-tube

**Respiratory:**
- **Respiratory rate** — count for a full minute in children
- **Lung sounds** — assess all lung fields; listen for wheezing, rhonchi (expected with bronchitis)
- **Work of breathing** — observe for nasal flaring, intercostal or subcostal retractions, accessory muscle use
- **SpO2** — apply pulse oximeter; maintain ≥95%
- **Cough** — note frequency, character, whether productive

**Abdominal / G-tube Site:**
- Remove wet/saturated dressing — assess skin condition at stoma site
- Look for: redness, excoriation, leakage, granulation tissue, tube displacement
- Note characteristics of any drainage around the site (clear, cloudy, bloody)
- **Clean and redress the site**

**Integumentary:**
- Assess the wet bed pad — child has been lying in leaked formula
- Check all skin areas in contact with moisture for early breakdown

### G-Tube Care Steps (During Simulation)
1. **Wash hands / don gloves**
2. **Explain to Alice and mother** what you are doing: "Alice, I'm going to check your tube and clean around it. It might feel a little cold, okay?"
3. **Remove the saturated dressing** — assess skin around stoma
4. **Clean the stoma site** with warm water or saline per protocol
5. **Apply fresh, dry dressing** — gauze or foam around the stoma base
6. **Reconnect the feeding tubing** — ensure secure connection
7. **Check gastric residual** before restarting feeding
8. **Restart the G-tube feeding** at the ordered rate
9. **Position Alice** at 30–45 degrees (reverse Trendelenburg or head of bed elevated)
10. **Administer antipyretic via G-tube:**
    - Flush tube with water (5–10 mL for child)
    - Draw up correct weight-based dose of acetaminophen suspension
    - Administer via G-tube
    - Flush again with water
11. **Document** all findings and interventions in SimChart/EHR

### Therapeutic Communication — With Alice (Age 7)
| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Get down to her eye level | Stand over her or talk down to her |
| Call her by her name "Alice" | Use generic terms like "sweetheart" without consent |
| Explain before you touch: "I'm going to feel your tummy now." | Touch without warning |
| Give limited choices: "Do you want to sit up or lie down?" | Overwhelm with too many options |
| Use simple words: "I'm going to give your tube some medicine to help your fever." | Use medical jargon |
| Acknowledge her feelings: "I know this feels strange." | Dismiss her feelings |
| Praise cooperation: "You're doing so well!" | Make comparisons to other patients |
| Allow her to hold something (toy, stuffed animal) | Remove all comfort objects |

### Therapeutic Communication — With Mother
| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Introduce yourself and address her by name | Ignore the mother and speak only to Alice |
| Ask about Alice's normal routine at home | Assume you know more than the mother |
| Say: "You are clearly her expert — I want to make sure I'm doing this just right." | Make the mother feel like an obstacle |
| Explain what you are observing and what you are doing | Perform procedures silently |
| Include her in care if she wants to participate | Force her to leave or step back |
| Teach as you go: "Here's how I'm reconnecting this so you can do it at home if needed." | Withhold education until discharge |

### Delegation
**Can delegate to UAP/CNA:**
- Recording vital signs (after RN initial assessment)
- Changing bed linens and bed pad
- Assisting with hygiene (bathing, mouth care)
- Keeping the environment safe and comfortable

**Cannot delegate:**
- Assessing the G-tube site and wound
- Administering medications via G-tube
- Teaching Alice and her mother
- Clinical judgment regarding tube placement and residual
- Documenting assessment findings

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the key abnormal findings the nurse must recognize in this scenario?

1. **Disconnected G-tube feeding** — immediate safety and nutrition concern; formula is leaking
2. **Saturated dressing at G-tube site** — moisture can macerate and break down peristomal skin
3. **Wet bed pad** — patient lying in formula; skin integrity risk
4. **Elevated temperature (fever)** — sign of infection/illness; requires antipyretic via G-tube
5. **Acute bronchitis** — respiratory compromise; tachypnea, wheezing, productive cough possible

> **Priority order of interventions:** Safety (reconnect G-tube) → Skin care (dressing change) → Comfort/fever (administer antipyretic) → Respiratory assessment (bronchitis)

---

### Q2. How do you administer an antipyretic to a child via G-tube?

1. Confirm the correct medication and dose using **weight-based calculation** (mg/kg)
2. Verify patient identity with two identifiers
3. Confirm G-tube placement (aspirate gastric contents; if uncertain, confirm via X-ray)
4. Flush the G-tube with **5–10 mL of water** to confirm patency
5. Draw up the correct volume of acetaminophen suspension using a syringe
6. Administer the medication slowly into the G-tube port
7. Flush again with **5–10 mL of water** to deliver full dose and clear the tube
8. Document medication, time, dose, and route in the EHR
9. Monitor temperature within 30–60 minutes for therapeutic effect
10. Assess for adverse effects

### Q3. What are the nursing diagnoses appropriate for Alice Morrison?

**Priority nursing diagnoses:**
1. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t G-tube feeding disconnection and prolonged moisture exposure
   - AEB: Saturated dressing, wet bed pad, leakage of formula around stoma site

2. **Hyperthermia** r/t acute bronchitis and infection
   - AEB: Elevated temperature on assessment, flushed skin, increased respiratory rate

3. **Ineffective Airway Clearance** r/t increased mucus production secondary to acute bronchitis
   - AEB: Cough, abnormal lung sounds (rhonchi/wheezing), increased respiratory rate

4. **Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements** r/t interrupted enteral feeding
   - AEB: G-tube feeding disconnected; child dependent on G-tube for all nutrition

5. **Risk for Aspiration** r/t G-tube feeding and position
   - AEB: Esophageal atresia, G-tube dependence, bronchitis affecting airway clearance

### Q4. What does the Braden Q Scale assess and how does it apply to Alice?

The **Braden Q Scale** is the pediatric version of the Braden Scale, used to assess pressure ulcer risk in children. It has **7 subscales** (vs. 6 in the adult version):
1. Mobility
2. Activity
3. Sensory perception
4. Moisture
5. Friction and shear
6. Nutrition
7. **Tissue perfusion and oxygenation** (added for pediatric patients)

**Application to Alice:**
- **Moisture** subscale is particularly relevant — the leaked formula and wet bed pad place her at increased risk for skin breakdown at and around the G-tube site
- **Activity** — hospitalized, on IV antibiotics; reduced mobility
- **Nutrition** — feeding was interrupted; nutritional deficit possible

### Q5. What patient and family education is needed for G-tube care at home?

1. **Daily stoma care:**
   - Clean around the G-tube with warm water daily
   - Gently rotate the tube to prevent it from adhering to the stoma tract
   - Keep the site clean and dry
   - Report redness, swelling, unusual discharge, or odor to the provider

2. **Feeding:**
   - Always check tube placement before each feeding
   - Elevate the head of the bed 30–45 degrees during and for 30–60 minutes after feedings
   - Flush the tube before and after feedings and medications with the ordered amount of water
   - Check gastric residual before each feeding; hold feeding if residual is high and notify the provider

3. **Medication administration:**
   - Always use the liquid form of medications when possible
   - Flush before and after each medication
   - Give each medication separately with a flush between each

4. **When to call the provider:**
   - Tube becomes dislodged or falls out completely
   - Signs of infection at the stoma site (redness, swelling, pus, foul odor, fever)
   - G-tube is blocked and cannot be flushed
   - Vomiting after feedings
   - Child appears to be in pain during feedings

5. **Emergency G-tube displacement:**
   - If the G-tube falls out completely, **cover the stoma with a moist gauze** immediately
   - Go to the emergency department right away — a G-tube stoma can close within hours

### Q6. What is the FACES Pain Scale and when is it used?

The **Wong-Baker FACES Pain Scale** uses a series of 6 faces ranging from a smiling face (no pain, score 0) to a crying face (worst pain, score 10). It is used to:
- Assess pain in children aged **3–7+ years** who can point to a picture
- Assess pain in patients who have difficulty verbalizing pain (cognitive impairment, language barriers)

**How to use it with Alice:**
- Show the scale at eye level and say: "Alice, which of these faces shows how you feel right now?"
- Document the number corresponding to the face she selects
- Reassess after interventions (medication, position change, comfort measure)

**Other pediatric pain scales:**
- **FLACC Scale** (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) — for children too young to use FACES or who cannot self-report (0–7 years, or any age with cognitive impairment)
- **Numeric Scale (0–10)** — appropriate for school-age children (7+) who understand numbers

### Q7. What are the nursing priorities for a child with acute bronchitis?

1. **Respiratory assessment** — monitor RR, SpO2, work of breathing, lung sounds q4–8h or per order
2. **Position** — head of bed elevated 30–45 degrees to maximize lung expansion (also required for G-tube feeding)
3. **Hydration** — adequate fluid intake thins mucus and facilitates clearance; delivered via G-tube
4. **Fever management** — antipyretics (acetaminophen or ibuprofen) via G-tube; monitor temperature response
5. **Administer antibiotics as ordered** — Gentamicin and Ampicillin IV as prescribed
6. **Encourage coughing and deep breathing** — age-appropriate (incentive spirometry may be used in older children; blowing bubbles or pinwheels for younger children)
7. **Suctioning** — if secretions excessive and child cannot clear independently
8. **Parental education** — teach signs of worsening respiratory distress to report

---

### Q8. What are the complications of esophageal atresia the nurse should be aware of?

1. **GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease)** — very common; treated with ranitidine/PPIs; aspiration risk
2. **Aspiration pneumonia** — high risk due to dysmotile esophagus and GERD
3. **Esophageal stricture** — narrowing at the repair site; can cause food impaction
4. **Tracheomalacia** — weakness of the tracheal wall; associated with TEF; causes barky cough
5. **G-tube complications** — dislodgement, granulation tissue, infection, leakage
6. **Nutritional deficits** — if feeding is interrupted or inadequate
7. **Dysphagia** — difficulty swallowing even after surgical repair; may persist for life
8. **Recurrent respiratory infections** — aspiration and GERD contribute to repeated pulmonary infections (like Alice's current acute bronchitis)

## 👨‍👩‍👧 FAMILY-CENTERED CARE — Key Concepts

In pediatric nursing, **family-centered care (FCC)** is the gold standard. Key principles include:

1. **Recognition:** Family members are essential partners in care, not visitors
2. **Information sharing:** Provide complete, unbiased, and ongoing information to families
3. **Participation:** Families are encouraged to participate in care to the extent they choose
4. **Collaboration:** Families are treated as equal members of the healthcare team
5. **Respect and dignity:** Honor family perspectives, cultural diversity, and choices

**For Alice's mother specifically:**
- She is the expert on Alice's G-tube care — she manages it daily at home
- Validate her expertise: "You've been managing this tube at home — that's wonderful. Can you walk me through what you usually do?"
- Include her in documentation: "Before I start charting, is there anything about Alice's normal routine I should know?"
- This is also a teaching opportunity: assess whether her home care technique is consistent with best practices

## ⚠️ WHAT TO WATCH FOR — Potential Complications

### Signs of Worsening Respiratory Status (Bronchitis → Pneumonia/Respiratory Failure)
- SpO2 < 95% on room air
- Respiratory rate above 25 or increasing trend
- Intercostal or subcostal retractions
- Nasal flaring
- Cyanosis (late sign)
- Decreased breath sounds (suggests consolidation or atelectasis)
- Child becoming lethargic or unable to maintain effort of breathing

**Escalate immediately using SBAR if these are present.**

### Signs of G-tube Site Infection
- Redness, swelling, warmth at stoma site
- Purulent (yellow/green) drainage
- Foul odor
- Fever (also present from bronchitis — correlate both conditions)
- Increased pain/tenderness at site

### Signs of Gentamicin Toxicity
- Decreased urine output or dark urine (nephrotoxicity)
- Hearing loss, tinnitus (ringing in ears), dizziness (ototoxicity)
- Elevated BUN and creatinine
- Elevated peak or trough levels on therapeutic drug monitoring

## 📞 SBAR TEMPLATE FOR ALICE MORRISON

**S — Situation:**
"I am calling about Alice Morrison, a 7-year-old female admitted for acute bronchitis with a history of esophageal atresia and G-tube dependence. I discovered her G-tube feeding was disconnected on arrival, and she has an elevated temperature."

"Alice has a history of esophageal atresia and relies on her G-tube for all nutrition, hydration, and medications. Her G-tube feeding was disconnected, resulting in a saturated dressing and wet bed pad. Her current medications include Gentamicin IVPB, Ampicillin IV, Ranitidine via G-tube, and acetaminophen PRN. Her mother is present and is her primary caregiver."
