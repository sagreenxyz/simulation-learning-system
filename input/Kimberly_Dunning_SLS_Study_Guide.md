## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four interlocking themes**:

1. **Acute asthma exacerbation management** — recognizing and rapidly treating airway obstruction
2. **Anxiety making everything worse** — anxiety increases respiratory rate and worsens bronchospasm; therapeutic communication is essential
3. **Oxygen delivery** — administering high-flow O2 via non-rebreather mask correctly and monitoring response
4. **Secondary pneumonia** — the trigger for her exacerbation; requires antibiotic treatment alongside asthma management

The pathophysiology cascade:

```
Trigger (pneumonia/infection)
        ↓
Airway inflammation → Bronchoconstriction → Edema → Mucus production
        ↓
Decreased airflow → Air trapping → Hyperinflation
        ↓
V/Q Mismatch → Hypoxemia → Respiratory alkalosis (early) → Acidosis (late)
        ↓
Anxiety worsens → Increased RR → Further air trapping
        ↓
Must: Assess → O2 via NRB → Notify provider → Start IV → Albuterol nebulizer
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is asthma?

Asthma is a **chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways** characterized by:
- **Airway inflammation** — mast cells, eosinophils, T lymphocytes trigger inflammatory response
- **Bronchospasm** — smooth muscle in the bronchi contracts → airway narrowing
- **Increased mucus production** — hypersecretion of mucus → airway plugging
- **Airway hyperresponsiveness** — airways overreact to stimuli (triggers)
- **Airway remodeling** — chronic inflammation leads to permanent structural changes over time

The result: obstruction to airflow that is typically **reversible** (distinguishing it from COPD).

**Common triggers:**
- Respiratory infections (pneumonia, URI — Kimberly's trigger)
- Allergens (dust, pollen, pets)
- Exercise
- Cold air
- Tobacco smoke
- NSAID use (aspirin-exacerbated asthma)
- Emotional stress or anxiety

---

### Q2. Describe the signs and symptoms of an asthma exacerbation.

**Mild-to-Moderate:**
- Shortness of breath, especially with activity
- Chest tightness
- Wheezing (expiratory > inspiratory) — caused by air rushing through narrowed airways
- Dry or productive cough
- Increased respiratory rate
- Anxiety, restlessness

**Severe Exacerbation:**
- Difficulty speaking in complete sentences
- Use of accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid, scalene)
- Intercostal and subcostal retractions
- Pursed-lip breathing
- Marked tachycardia (>120 bpm)
- Diaphoresis
- SpO2 < 90%
- Cyanosis (very late sign)
- **Silent chest** — no wheezing heard on auscultation = air movement so reduced that wheezing stops = MEDICAL EMERGENCY

---

### Q3. What are the early signs and symptoms of inadequate oxygenation (hypoxemia)?

**Early (Compensation):**
- Restlessness and anxiety
- Headache
- Tachypnea (RR > 20)
- Tachycardia (HR > 100)
- Diaphoresis (sweating)
- Confusion, disorientation

**Late (Decompensation):**
- Combativeness
- Cyanosis (lips, fingertips, circumoral)
- Bradycardia (ominous)
- Bradypnea (respiratory failure imminent)
- Loss of consciousness
- Hypotension
- Dysrhythmias

> **ABG interpretation for asthma exacerbation:**
> - **Early:** Respiratory alkalosis — pH ↑, PaCO2 ↓ (hyperventilating to blow off CO2)
> - **Late/Severe:** Respiratory acidosis — pH ↓, PaCO2 ↑ (patient tiring, CO2 retaining) = sign of impending respiratory failure

---

### Q4. What are priority nursing interventions for the management of an asthmatic patient?

**Priority order (ABC approach):**
1. **Assess respiratory status** — RR, depth, rhythm; lung sounds (wheezing, stridor); work of breathing; SpO2; speak to assess ability to finish sentences
2. **Administer oxygen** — via non-rebreather mask (NRB) for acute exacerbation (10–15 LPM); titrate to SpO2 ≥92%
3. **Notify the provider** — report findings and receive orders
4. **Establish IV access** — start IV and IV fluids (NS 125 mL/hr) for hydration; thins secretions
5. **Administer bronchodilator** — Albuterol nebulizer (SABA) — opens airways immediately
6. **Administer corticosteroid** — Prednisone — reduces airway inflammation
7. **Collect blood specimens** — CBC, BMP, blood glucose (steroids elevate glucose)
8. **Position patient** — high Fowler's (sit upright, lean slightly forward if needed — "tripod position") to maximize lung expansion
9. **Anxiety management** — speak calmly; reduce stimulation; therapeutic communication; patient's anxiety worsens bronchospasm
10. **Monitor blood glucose** — corticosteroids cause hyperglycemia
11. **Educate** about inhaler use, triggers, peak flow meter, action plan

### Q5. Interpret the following ABG: pH 7.52, PaO2 70, PaCO2 26, HCO3 24 (on room air)

- **pH 7.52** = alkalosis (normal 7.35–7.45)
- **PaCO2 26** = low (normal 35–45) — this IS the cause of alkalosis
- **HCO3 24** = normal (22–26) — kidney has NOT compensated
- **PaO2 70** = low (normal 80–100) — mild hypoxemia

**Interpretation: Respiratory alkalosis with NO metabolic compensation**
- Cause: Hyperventilation (the patient is breathing too fast, blowing off CO2)
- This is the pattern in EARLY asthma exacerbation — patient is tachypneic and alkalotic
- The low PaO2 = hypoxemia requiring supplemental oxygen

**Best intervention:** Oxygen supplementation (NRB mask) to treat the hypoxia

---

### Q6. Which interventions are NOT helpful during an ACUTE asthma exacerbation?

- ❌ **Incentive spirometry** — requires full inhalation; difficult and exhausting during acute attack; not appropriate acutely
- ❌ **Huff coughing** — technique for clearing secretions; not the priority in acute bronchospasm
- ❌ **Suctioning** (unless secretions are obstructing) — not indicated as first-line
- ✅ **Raise head of bed** — appropriate
- ✅ **Oxygen** — appropriate
- ✅ **Albuterol nebulizer** — appropriate
- ✅ **IV fluids** — appropriate (thins secretions, maintains hydration)

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Assessment Priorities
1. **Look** — Is she in obvious respiratory distress? Tripod position? Using accessory muscles?
2. **Listen** — What do lung sounds reveal? Wheezing on exhalation? Inspiratory stridor? Silent chest?
3. **Measure** — SpO2, RR, HR, BP
4. **Ask** — Can she speak in complete sentences? (If not → severe)

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up
- **W** — Wristband — verify identity
- **I** — IV access established? IV NS running?
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach
- **E** — Equipment: NRB mask available? Albuterol nebulizer set up?

### Non-Rebreather Mask (NRB) — Key Steps
1. Select NRB mask (appropriate for high-flow O2 delivery: 10–15 LPM)
2. Set flow rate at **10–15 LPM** — inflate the reservoir bag before placing on patient
3. The bag must remain inflated (at least 1/3 full) during inspiration — if it collapses completely, flow rate is too low
4. Ensure mask seals properly over nose and mouth
5. The one-way valves on side vents prevent room air from mixing with the O2-enriched bag
6. Monitor SpO2 continuously
7. **Oxygen device comparison:**
   - Nasal cannula: 2–6 LPM → FiO2 24–44%
   - Simple face mask: 6–10 LPM → FiO2 35–50%
   - **Non-rebreather mask: 10–15 LPM → FiO2 60–90%** ← for Kimberly

### Albuterol Nebulizer — Instructions to Patient
1. Sit upright in a comfortable position
2. Place the mouthpiece between your lips and breathe normally
3. Breathe slowly and deeply through your mouth
4. Continue until the medication is gone (5–10 minutes)
5. Report if heart races or feels palpitations — normal side effect of albuterol
6. Wait 5 minutes before prednisone administration

### Therapeutic Communication — Anxious Patient
Kimberly's anxiety is worsening her bronchospasm. Key strategies:

- **Stay calm** — your calm demeanor is therapeutic
- **Speak slowly and clearly** — one instruction at a time
- **Be present** — do not leave the room
- **Acknowledge her fear:** "I can see you're scared. You're in the right place and I'm going to help you."
- **Give her control:** "I need you to try to slow your breathing down with me. Just follow my lead."
- **Use breathing coaching:** "In through the nose... out through the mouth... nice and slow."
- **Avoid dismissing:** Never say "Just calm down" — this increases anxiety
- **Praise progress:** "You're doing great. Your breathing is already looking a little easier."

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. A patient learning to use an ipratropium MDI — which instruction is correct?
**C.) After taking a puff, hold your breath for 10 seconds before exhaling.** Holding the breath allows the medication to deposit in the lower airways. Shaking the inhaler IS recommended (A is wrong). You should breathe out BEFORE positioning the inhaler (B is wrong). A spacer CAN help with MDI technique (D is wrong).

---

### Q2. Which symptom is NOT a sign of inadequate oxygenation?
**A.) Hypertension** — hypertension is not a primary indicator of inadequate oxygenation. Cyanosis, bradycardia (late), and diaphoresis ARE signs of inadequate oxygenation.

---

### Q3. A non-productive cough that began when a new medication was prescribed is likely due to:
**A.) Antihypertensive medications (specifically ACE inhibitors)** — ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril) commonly cause a persistent dry cough as a side effect due to bradykinin accumulation.

---

### Q4. Which statement is incorrect about smoking and respiratory illness?
**C.) If you smoke a pipe or cigars you have a decreased risk of developing lung cancer.** This is FALSE. Pipe and cigar smokers also have significantly increased risk of lung, mouth, throat, and esophageal cancer. All forms of tobacco use carry cancer risk.

---

### Q5. Which medication would a patient with asthma be prescribed based on pathophysiology?
**A.) Corticosteroids** — asthma's pathophysiology is driven by inflammation. Corticosteroids (prednisone, inhaled fluticasone) target the inflammatory process. Beta-blockers worsen bronchoconstriction and are generally contraindicated in asthma.

---

### Q6. Why does Ms. Dunning have an order for blood glucose?
**C.) Steroids increase glucose levels** — corticosteroids (prednisone) cause hyperglycemia by increasing hepatic glucose production and decreasing cellular glucose uptake. Blood glucose must be monitored after corticosteroid administration, especially in diabetic patients or those with prediabetes.

---

### Q7. Asthma is considered a:
**Chronic inflammatory disorder** that causes reversible airway obstruction through bronchoconstriction, edema, and mucus production.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Albuterol (Proventil, Ventolin) — Nebulizer
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Short-acting beta-2 adrenergic agonist (SABA) — Rescue bronchodilator |
| **Mechanism** | Stimulates beta-2 receptors in bronchial smooth muscle → relaxation → bronchodilation |
| **Indication** | Acute bronchospasm in asthma exacerbation |
| **Route** | Nebulizer (2.5 mg/3 mL q20 min x3, then q1–4h) |
| **Onset/Peak/Duration** | Onset: 5–15 min / Peak: 60–90 min / Duration: 4–6 hours |
| **Adverse effects** | Tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia, nervousness |
| **Nursing precautions** | Assess HR before and after; monitor for paradoxical bronchospasm; excessive use indicates poor control |
| **Patient teaching** | Use for acute symptoms ("rescue"); shake before use with MDI; report chest pain or worsening symptoms |

---

### Prednisone (Deltasone) — Oral Corticosteroid
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Systemic corticosteroid |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits inflammatory mediators; reduces airway edema; decreases mucus production; enhances beta-2 receptor response |
| **Indication** | Acute asthma exacerbation to reduce inflammation |
| **Route** | PO (IV methylprednisolone for severe cases) |
| **Adverse effects** | Hyperglycemia ⚠️, hypertension, fluid retention, immunosuppression, mood changes, adrenal suppression (long-term) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor blood glucose; check BP; administer with food to reduce GI irritation; taper dose when discontinuing |
| **Patient teaching** | Take with food; do not abruptly stop; monitor blood sugar if diabetic; report mood changes, swelling |

---

### Normal Saline 0.9% IV at 125 mL/hr
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Isotonic crystalloid IV fluid |
| **Indication** | Hydration; thins mucus secretions; maintains vascular volume |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor IV site; watch for fluid overload; monitor I&O |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Ineffective Breathing Pattern** r/t bronchoconstriction, airway edema, and increased mucus production secondary to acute asthma exacerbation
   - AEB: wheezing, tachypnea, use of accessory muscles, SpO2 < 92%

2. **Impaired Gas Exchange** r/t V/Q mismatch secondary to bronchospasm and atelectasis
   - AEB: SpO2 < 92%, abnormal ABGs (respiratory alkalosis early; acidosis late)

3. **Anxiety** r/t acute respiratory distress and fear
   - AEB: restlessness, verbalization of fear, increased RR worsened by anxiety

4. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t asthma management (medication use, triggers, action plan)
   - AEB: patient on inhaler medications but currently experiencing acute exacerbation
