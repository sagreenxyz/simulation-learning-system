## 🔑 The Big Picture

Grace has **stable (exertional) angina** — chest pain caused by fixed coronary artery narrowing that limits blood flow during increased demand. The scenario focuses on:

1. **Recognizing stable angina** and distinguishing it from unstable angina or MI
2. **Correct rapid response** — stop activity → O2 → calm patient → SL nitroglycerin → notify provider
3. **Reducing myocardial O2 demand** through rest, oxygen, reassurance, and medication

```
CAD → Fixed coronary narrowing
        ↓
Exercise → Increased myocardial O2 demand
        ↓
Supply cannot meet demand → Ischemia → Chest pain (angina)
        ↓
Stop activity → O2 → SL NTG q5min x3 → Calm patient → Notify provider
        ↓
Relief = Stable angina | No relief after 3 doses = EMERGENCY → possible MI
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is angina pectoris and how does it differ from a myocardial infarction?

**Stable Angina:**
- Transient myocardial ischemia — inadequate O2 supply during increased demand
- Ischemia is **temporary and REVERSIBLE** — no permanent cell death
- Predictable pattern: triggered by exertion or emotional stress
- **Relieved by rest and/or nitroglycerin within 3–5 minutes**

**Unstable Angina:**
- Occurs at rest or with minimal exertion; new onset or worsening pattern
- NOT relieved by rest or 3 doses of NTG → medical emergency

**Myocardial Infarction (MI):**
- Prolonged ischemia → **IRREVERSIBLE myocardial cell death (necrosis)**
- NOT relieved by NTG or rest
- Elevated troponin, CK-MB; ST changes on ECG
- Requires immediate intervention (PCI, thrombolytics)

---

### Q2. What are the classic signs and symptoms of angina?

**Classic Presentation:**
- Chest pressure, squeezing, heaviness, tightness ("elephant on chest")
- Substernal location; may radiate to left arm, jaw, neck, shoulder, or back
- Precipitated by exertion, emotional stress, cold weather, heavy meals
- Duration 1–5 minutes (stable); > 20 minutes suggests MI or unstable angina
- Relieved by rest and/or sublingual nitroglycerin

**Associated Symptoms:** Dyspnea, diaphoresis, nausea, pallor, anxiety, fatigue

**Atypical Presentations (especially women, diabetics, elderly — Grace is all three!):**
- Jaw pain, neck pain, epigastric discomfort, or fatigue WITHOUT classic chest pain
- Diabetic patients may have **silent ischemia** — neuropathy reduces pain perception
- Women more likely: back pain, nausea, extreme fatigue

---

### Q3. What is the correct procedure for administering sublingual nitroglycerin?

1. **Sit or lie the patient down** — NTG causes vasodilation → hypotension → fall risk
2. **Check BP** — HOLD if SBP < 90 mmHg
3. Place 0.4 mg tablet **under the tongue** — do NOT swallow
4. **Reassess pain and BP after 5 minutes**
5. **May repeat q5 min up to 3 doses** total if pain not relieved
6. **After 3 doses with no relief → notify provider IMMEDIATELY** → possible MI/unstable angina
7. **Warn patient:** Headache (common and expected), dizziness, flushing
8. **Storage:** Amber bottle; away from heat, light, moisture; replace every 6 months

**Contraindications:** SBP < 90 mmHg; recent phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitor use (sildenafil, tadalafil); severe aortic stenosis; right ventricular infarction

### Q4. What risk factors does Grace Carter have for coronary artery disease?

| Risk Factor | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Age 71** | Post-menopausal women lose estrogen's cardioprotective effect |
| **Diabetes mellitus** | Accelerates atherosclerosis; causes silent ischemia; doubles CAD risk |
| **Hypertension** | Damages arterial walls → plaque formation; increases cardiac workload |
| **Hyperlipidemia** | LDL deposits in arterial walls → atherosclerotic plaques |

Grace has four major modifiable risk factors, making her at high risk for both angina exacerbations and MI.

---

### Q5. What are the priority nursing interventions for a patient experiencing angina?

1. **Stop all activity** — have patient sit or lie down
2. **O2 via nasal cannula** — titrate to SpO2 ≥ 94%
3. **Assess BP** before each NTG dose — hold if SBP < 90
4. **SL NTG 0.4 mg** — repeat q5 min x3 if needed
5. **Calm and reassure** — anxiety increases O2 demand
6. **Obtain 12-lead ECG** per order
7. **Notify provider** — especially if pain unrelieved after 3 doses
8. **Document** — time pain started, rating, location, radiation, interventions, response

---

### Q6. How does calming a patient reduce angina?

Anxiety triggers sympathetic activation → tachycardia → increased myocardial O2 demand → worsens ischemia. Reassurance and calming the patient reduces heart rate and oxygen consumption, which is directly therapeutic in angina management.

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Immediate Actions
Grace has returned from exercise (ambulation or cardiac rehab) with chest pain:

1. "Ms. Carter, I need you to stop and sit down right now."
2. Check SpO2 and vital signs — especially BP
3. Apply O2 via nasal cannula
4. "Can you describe your chest pain? Where is it? Any radiation? Rate it 0–10."
5. Check BP before NTG — hold if SBP < 90
6. Administer SL NTG 0.4 mg
7. Reassess pain and BP in 5 minutes
8. Stay at bedside continuously

### SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails up once patient is in bed
- **W** — Wristband — two identifiers
- **I** — IV access? Cardiac monitor leads on?
- **P** — Pull cord within reach
- **E** — Emergency: code cart nearby; 12-lead ECG available

### Key Teaching Points for Grace
- "Always rest immediately when you feel this pain coming on."
- "Take your nitroglycerin under your tongue — don't swallow it."
- "You may get a headache — that's normal and means the medicine is working."
- "If you take three tablets five minutes apart and the pain doesn't go away, call 911 immediately."
- "Never take Viagra or Cialis if you are on nitroglycerin — the combination can cause dangerous drops in blood pressure."

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Which finding causes the nurse to HOLD sublingual NTG?
**SBP < 90 mmHg** — NTG causes vasodilation and can cause severe hypotension, syncope, and cardiovascular collapse if BP is already low.

### Q2. Medication of choice for initial angina relief:
**Sublingual nitroglycerin** — onset 1–3 minutes; dilates coronary arteries; reduces preload.

### Q3. After 3 doses of NTG with no relief, the nurse should:
Notify the provider immediately — 12-lead ECG, troponin, IV access, continuous monitoring. Unrelieved angina = possible unstable angina or MI.

### Q4. Why does calming an anxious patient help with angina?
Reduces sympathetic drive → lowers HR → decreases myocardial O2 demand → reduces ischemia.

### Q5. Which secondary diagnosis most increases Grace's risk of silent ischemia?
**Diabetes mellitus** — neuropathy impairs cardiac pain sensation; Grace may not perceive the full severity of ischemia.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Nitroglycerin (Nitrostat) 0.4 mg SL
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Nitrate vasodilator |
| **Mechanism** | Releases nitric oxide → vasodilation → increased coronary O2 delivery; decreased preload |
| **Onset** | 1–3 minutes SL |
| **Adverse effects** | Headache (very common), hypotension, dizziness, flushing |
| **Hold if** | SBP < 90 mmHg; recent sildenafil/tadalafil use |
| **Nursing precautions** | Sit/lie patient before giving; assess BP before each dose; wear gloves for transdermal |
| **Patient teaching** | Sit down first; expect headache; call 911 if 3 doses don't work |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Acute Pain** r/t myocardial ischemia secondary to stable angina exacerbation — AEB: chest pressure/pain post-exercise, diaphoresis, dyspnea
2. **Decreased Cardiac Output** r/t reduced coronary perfusion — AEB: chest pain, tachycardia
3. **Activity Intolerance** r/t angina precipitated by exertion — AEB: pain with exercise, dyspnea
4. **Anxiety** r/t acute cardiac event — AEB: restlessness, fear, sympathetic activation worsening ischemia
5. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t angina management and NTG use — AEB: multiple uncontrolled risk factors
