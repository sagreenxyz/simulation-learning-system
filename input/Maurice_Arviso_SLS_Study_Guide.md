## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the signs that a patient needs supplemental oxygen?

**Early signs of hypoxia (oxygen deficiency):**
- Restlessness and anxiety
- Confusion or agitation
- Tachycardia (increased heart rate)
- Tachypnea (increased respiratory rate)
- Dyspnea (shortness of breath)
- Diaphoresis (sweating)
- Use of accessory muscles (retractions, nasal flaring)
- SpO2 < 95% (or below patient's baseline)

**Late signs of hypoxia (more serious):**
- Cyanosis (bluish discoloration of lips, fingernails, skin)
- Bradycardia (late, ominous sign)
- Decreased level of consciousness → stupor → unresponsiveness
- Decreased or absent respiratory effort

> **Key point:** Cyanosis is a LATE sign — by the time cyanosis is visible, the patient is already severely hypoxic. Treat early signs aggressively.

**Objective indicator:** SpO2 < 95% on pulse oximetry requires nursing assessment and possible intervention. SpO2 < 90% is a medical emergency.

### Q2. What are the oxygen delivery methods and their flow rates?

| Device | Flow Rate | FiO2 (% O2 delivered) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nasal cannula** | 1–6 L/min | 24–44% | Most common; allows eating/talking; least restrictive |
| **Simple face mask** | 5–10 L/min | 40–60% | Must run at ≥5 L to prevent CO2 rebreathing |
| **Partial rebreather mask** | 6–10 L/min | 50–70% | Reservoir bag; keep inflated |
| **Non-rebreather mask (NRB)** | 10–15 L/min | 60–90% | Highest non-invasive O2; one-way valve; emergency use |
| **Venturi mask** | Varies by color | 24%, 28%, 31%, 35%, 40%, 60% | Most precise FiO2; preferred for COPD patients |
| **BiPAP/CPAP** | — | Variable | Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation |
| **Mechanical ventilation** | — | 21–100% | Invasive; intubated patient |

> **For Maurice:** Nasal cannula is likely the initial device — allows eating and speaking while delivering supplemental oxygen. Monitor SpO2 continuously.

### Q3. What are non-oxygen breathing assistance methods?

- **Positioning:** Semi-Fowler's (30–45°) or High-Fowler's (60–90°) maximizes lung expansion by allowing diaphragm to descend; use when dyspnea present
- **Incentive spirometry:** Encourages deep breathing, prevents atelectasis; patient inhales slowly and deeply to a target volume
- **Deep breathing and coughing exercises:** Mobilizes secretions, re-expands alveoli
- **Nebulizer treatments / MDI bronchodilators:** Open airways (if bronchospasm present)
- **Chest physiotherapy (CPT):** Percussion and vibration to loosen secretions
- **Suctioning:** If patient unable to clear secretions independently
- **Turning and repositioning:** Prevents consolidation and promotes lung drainage
- **Hydration:** Adequate fluid thins secretions and makes them easier to clear

### Q4. What are the cultural considerations when caring for a Native American patient?

**Communication style:**
- Native American patients often have a **quieter, more reserved communication style** — silence does not indicate lack of understanding or disengagement
- Direct eye contact may be considered **disrespectful or aggressive** in some Native American cultures — the nurse should not insist on eye contact
- Allow longer pauses in conversation — do not rush to fill silence
- Non-verbal communication is highly valued

**Family and decision-making:**
- Family and community involvement in healthcare decisions may be **collective rather than individual**
- The elder or tribal authority may be involved in major decisions
- Maurice asked his son Jonathan to step out before the spill — he values **privacy and independence**; this should be respected and never shamed

**Spiritual and end-of-life beliefs:**
- Many Native American traditions include connection to nature, ancestors, and the spirit world
- Sacred rituals, ceremonies, or medicine man/healer involvement may be important
- Ask: "Are there any spiritual or cultural practices that are important to you while you're here?"
- Maurice's **fear of dying in the hospital** is directly related to losing his wife and father in hospitals — this fear must be acknowledged and addressed with compassion

**Nursing implications:**
- **Never shame Maurice** about the urine spill — approach with matter-of-fact kindness
- Ask about his preferences for who is present during care
- Offer choices to preserve autonomy and dignity
- Consult cultural liaisons or Native American health resources if available
- Approach Maurice as an equal partner in his care

### Q5. What is community-acquired pneumonia (CAP)?

**Definition:** Pneumonia acquired outside of a hospital or healthcare facility, in the community setting

**Common causative organisms:**
- *Streptococcus pneumoniae* (most common bacterial cause)
- *Mycoplasma pneumoniae* (atypical pneumonia — "walking pneumonia")
- *Haemophilus influenzae*
- Viral: Influenza, RSV, COVID-19

**Risk factors for Maurice:**
- Age 60 (immune response diminishes with age)
- Father had a "cough" at age 55 and died — possible family history of pulmonary disease
- Unknown vaccination history (pneumococcal and influenza vaccines reduce CAP risk)

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Productive cough (yellow/green sputum)
- Fever and chills
- Dyspnea
- Pleuritic chest pain (sharp pain worsened by breathing)
- Decreased breath sounds, crackles on auscultation
- Fatigue, malaise
- SpO2 decrease

**Nursing priorities:**
- Monitor SpO2 and respiratory status closely
- Administer prescribed antibiotics on time
- Oxygen therapy as needed
- Encourage oral fluids (>2L/day) to thin secretions
- Position for comfort and maximum lung expansion
- Monitor for sepsis (pneumonia is a leading cause of sepsis)

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — The Urine Spill Situation
Maurice spilled urine on the bed and floor while using the urinal. His son Jonathan stepped out first.

**How to handle it:**
1. Approach **calmly and matter-of-factly** — no sighing, no expressions of frustration
2. Do NOT say: "Oh, how did that happen?" or show any negative reaction
3. Say: "Mr. Arviso, accidents happen — let's get you cleaned up and comfortable."
4. Protect his dignity — close the door, draw the curtain
5. Ask if he would like Jonathan to return or remain absent during care
6. Provide clean linens, clean gown, and fresh bed

### Bed Bath and Hygiene Care Steps
1. Explain the procedure
2. Ensure privacy — curtains drawn, door closed
3. Gather all supplies before beginning (basin, washcloths, towels, clean gown, clean linens)
4. Maintain patient warmth — cover areas not being washed
5. Wash and dry each area thoroughly
6. Clean perineal area LAST (most contaminated)
7. Change gown and bed linens — use occupied bed-making technique (patient rolls to one side while you change half, then the other)
8. Dispose of soiled linens per facility policy
9. Ensure call light is within reach; bed in lowest position

### Oxygenation Assessment
- Check current SpO2 reading
- Auscultate lung sounds — assess for crackles, diminished sounds
- Assess respiratory rate, depth, effort
- Assess for dyspnea, use of accessory muscles
- Assess color — lips, mucous membranes, nail beds
- If SpO2 < 95% → apply oxygen per order (nasal cannula); if no order and emergency → initiate per protocol and notify provider

### Therapeutic Communication with Maurice
- Acknowledge his history: "I know you've been through a lot with losing your wife and your father. It makes sense that being in the hospital is really hard."
- Validate independence: "You've been taking care of yourself your whole life — I want to support that."
- Ask about his preferences and traditions: "Are there any ways I can make your care feel more comfortable and respectful of your beliefs?"
- If Maurice is reluctant to call for help: "I want you to know it's not a burden to call us — that's exactly what we're here for."

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS

### Q1. What nursing diagnoses apply to Maurice Arviso?

1. **Impaired Gas Exchange** r/t alveolar inflammation from pneumonia — AEB decreased SpO2, crackles on auscultation, dyspnea
2. **Anxiety** r/t fear of dying in a hospital, history of losing family members in hospital
3. **Risk for Falls** r/t getting up alone (did not want to bother staff); spilled urine reaching urinal independently
4. **Self-Care Deficit (Hygiene)** r/t acute illness and urine spill — AEB soiled bed and gown
5. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t pneumonia management, oxygen therapy, and when to ask for help

---

### Q2. What are the 3 priority nursing diagnoses in order?

1. **Impaired Gas Exchange** — airway and oxygenation are always the first priority
2. **Anxiety** — fear of hospital death is affecting his willingness to accept care
3. **Risk for Falls** — demonstrated by his attempt to manage independently and getting up without calling

---

### Q3. When would Maurice need to be escalated to a higher level of care?

Signs of worsening pneumonia requiring escalation:
- SpO2 < 90% despite supplemental oxygen
- Respiratory rate > 30 breaths/min
- Worsening mental status
- Signs of sepsis (HR > 90, fever or hypothermia, confusion, hypotension)
- Failure to improve with antibiotics within 48–72 hours
