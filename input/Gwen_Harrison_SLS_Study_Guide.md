## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is elder abuse and what are the types?

**Elder Abuse:** Any intentional or negligent act by a caregiver or other person that causes harm or serious risk of harm to a vulnerable older adult.

**Types of Elder Abuse:**

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical abuse** | Non-accidental physical force causing injury or pain | Hitting, pushing, slapping, burning, restraining improperly |
| **Sexual abuse** | Non-consensual sexual contact | Assault, inappropriate touching |
| **Emotional/Psychological abuse** | Verbal threats, humiliation, intimidation | Yelling, name-calling, threats, isolation |
| **Financial exploitation** | Unauthorized use of money or property | Stealing, forging checks, changing will |
| **Neglect** | Failure to meet basic needs | Withholding food, medications, hygiene, medical care |
| **Self-neglect** | Person's own inability/refusal to meet their basic needs | Severe hoarding, refusal of needed care |
| **Abandonment** | Desertion by caregiver | Leaving patient alone without care provision |

**Gwen's situation:** Physical abuse (bruising) + Neglect (dehydration, malnutrition, poor hygiene)

### Q2. What are the signs and symptoms of elder abuse and neglect?

**Physical Signs of Abuse:**
- **Multiple bruises in different stages of healing** — suggests repeated trauma over time
- Bruising in unusual locations: inner arms, torso, upper arms, buttocks (accidental injuries typically occur on shins, forearms, knees)
- Lacerations or abrasions inconsistent with reported cause
- Burns (especially cigarette burns, contact burns with defined borders)
- Fractures inconsistent with stated cause or level of mobility
- Rope burns (wrist, ankles — inappropriate physical restraint)
- Bilateral or symmetric injuries (accidental injuries are usually unilateral)

**Signs of Neglect:**
- Malnourishment, significant weight loss
- Dehydration (dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, concentrated urine)
- Poor hygiene: unwashed, unkempt hair, soiled clothing, untreated wounds
- Pressure ulcers (from being left in one position without repositioning)
- Untreated medical conditions (UTI unresolved despite access to healthcare)
- Over/under-medication

**Behavioral Indicators:**
- **Fear or anxiety** when caregiver enters the room
- Evasive, withdrawn, or minimizing answers
- Waits for caregiver's permission to speak
- Caregiver answers all questions without allowing patient to speak
- **Discrepancy** between caregiver's explanation and patient's injuries
- Caregiver appears frustrated, indifferent, or controlling

### Q3. How should the nurse approach the assessment to establish a trusting relationship?

**Approach:**
- **Private interview** — assess Gwen ALONE; ask caregiver to step out: "It's our policy to briefly assess patients privately — I'll come get you when we're done."
- **Slow, non-threatening approach** — introduce yourself; sit at eye level
- **Non-judgmental language** — avoid any language that implies blame or disbelief
- **Open-ended questions:** "Can you tell me how you got this bruise?" not "Did someone hurt you?"
- **Validate and normalize:** "Sometimes when people are in a difficult situation at home, they're afraid to talk about it. You're safe here."
- **Do not pressure** — if she is not ready to disclose, she may not yet feel safe
- **Build on small disclosures** — "You mentioned things have been hard at home. Can you tell me more about that?"

**What NOT to do:**
- Confront the caregiver in front of Gwen
- Promise absolute confidentiality (you are a mandatory reporter)
- Conduct assessment with caregiver present (she will not disclose)
- Express shock, disgust, or disbelief at disclosures

### Q4. What is mandatory reporting of elder abuse?

**All 50 U.S. states have mandatory reporting laws for elder abuse.** In most states, healthcare workers (nurses, doctors, social workers) are **mandatory reporters** — they are LEGALLY REQUIRED to report suspected abuse or neglect.

**Mandatory Reporting Process:**
1. Report to **Adult Protective Services (APS)** — within 24–72 hours (varies by state)
2. Report to **law enforcement** if criminal abuse suspected
3. The nurse does NOT need proof — **reasonable suspicion** is sufficient to report
4. The investigation is conducted by APS, not the nurse
5. **Document all findings objectively** — use exact quotes when possible; describe injuries with precise measurements, location, color; avoid subjective interpretation ("patient appears to have been beaten")
6. **Do NOT notify the alleged abuser** that a report is being made

**Failure to report** is a legal and ethical violation — do not allow the abuser's relationship to the patient to prevent you from reporting.

### Q5. What nursing interventions ensure Gwen's safety during hospitalization?

1. **Separate patient from alleged abuser** — limit visiting time; never leave caregiver alone with Gwen without supervision
2. **Social work referral** — immediate; assess home situation, living arrangements, alternative care options
3. **Document ALL injuries** — photograph with patient consent; detailed written description
4. **Notify provider** — document medical findings consistent with abuse
5. **Contact APS** — mandatory report
6. **Interdisciplinary team** — social worker, case manager, psychiatry, ethics committee if needed
7. **Discharge planning** — Gwen should NOT be discharged to the same abusive situation without intervention
8. **Address immediate medical needs** — UTI treatment, rehydration, malnutrition

### Q6. What are the special considerations for Gwen's Parkinson's disease?

**Parkinson's Disease Overview:**
- Progressive neurodegenerative disorder — loss of dopaminergic neurons in substantia nigra
- Cardinal signs: Tremor (resting), Rigidity, Akinesia/bradykinesia, Postural instability (TRAP)

**Nursing Priorities:**
1. **Medications ON TIME** — carbidopa-levodopa must be given at exact prescribed times; delays cause sudden "off" episodes — severe rigidity, tremor, immobility, dysphagia
2. **Fall prevention** — shuffling gait, festination, postural instability = HIGH fall risk
3. **Swallowing assessment** — dysphagia common; aspiration risk; assess before oral medications
4. **Communication** — hypophonia (soft voice), masked facies; give extra time; listen patiently
5. **ADL assistance** — fine motor tasks (buttons, utensils) are difficult
6. **Constipation prevention** — autonomic dysfunction; adequate fiber and fluids

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Building Trust
1. Introduce yourself warmly; sit at eye level
2. "Mrs. Harrison, I'm your nurse today. I want to make sure you're comfortable. How are you feeling?"
3. Observe the caregiver's behavior — are they controlling? Speaking for Gwen? Watching her responses?

### Private Assessment — Asking About Abuse
"Mrs. Harrison, we have a policy to speak with patients privately for a few minutes. [Turn to caregiver] Would you mind waiting in the hallway while I do this quick assessment? I'll come get you shortly."

Once alone:
- Begin with physical assessment — note injuries objectively
- "I notice some bruises on your arms. Can you tell me how those happened?"
- "Are you safe at home? Does anyone hurt you?"
- "Is there anything you'd like to tell me while it's just the two of us?"

### Documentation Language
- CORRECT: "Patient has three circular, yellowish-green bruises (3–4 cm diameter) on bilateral upper arms. Patient states 'I fell.' Patient makes poor eye contact and lowers voice when caregiver enters room."
- INCORRECT: "Patient appears to have been beaten by her caregiver."

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION Q&A

### Q1. What is the most important nursing action when elder abuse is suspected?
Report to Adult Protective Services (APS) — nurses are mandatory reporters. Reasonable suspicion is all that is required. Document findings objectively. Ensure patient safety during hospitalization.

### Q2. How does the nurse assess for abuse without the caregiver present?
Ask caregiver to step out (using a policy-based reason — "it's our standard practice"). Create private space and use open-ended, non-judgmental questions. Build trust before asking direct questions.

### Q3. What injury pattern is most suspicious for physical abuse in the elderly?
Multiple bruises in different stages of healing, located on atypical areas (inner arms, torso, upper arms), with explanations inconsistent with injury severity or patient's functional ability.

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Risk for Injury** r/t physical abuse and neglect — AEB: multiple bruises in various stages, malnourishment, dehydration
2. **Impaired Physical Mobility** r/t Parkinson's disease — AEB: tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, postural instability
3. **Powerlessness** r/t abusive relationship and dependence on caregiver — AEB: evasive responses, fearful behavior, limited ability to self-advocate
4. **Deficient Fluid Volume** r/t neglect — AEB: dehydration, unresolved UTI
5. **Risk for Relocation Stress Syndrome** r/t potential need for placement change following abuse disclosure
