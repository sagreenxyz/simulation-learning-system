## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four core themes**:

1. **Recognizing signs of elder abuse and neglect** — the nurse must conduct a thorough, sensitive assessment to identify physical, emotional, and neglect indicators during the admission process
2. **Mandatory reporting obligations** — nurses are mandatory reporters of suspected elder abuse in most states; the nurse's role is to report suspicion, not to investigate or prove abuse
3. **Therapeutic communication to establish trust** — an abused patient may be fearful, protective of the abuser, or shame-bound; building trust requires patience, privacy, and a non-judgmental approach
4. **Parkinson's disease nursing implications** — medication timing, fall risk, aspiration risk, and functional limitations complicate care and may contribute to the patient's vulnerability

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the types of elder abuse and how does each present?

**Elder abuse** is any intentional or negligent act by a caregiver or trusted person that causes harm or serious risk of harm to a vulnerable older adult. It is significantly underreported — estimates suggest only 1 in 14 cases is reported.

| Type | Definition | Signs and Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical abuse** | Intentional infliction of physical pain or injury | Unexplained bruising (especially in unusual locations — torso, buttocks, back), fractures, burns, welts, lacerations; injuries inconsistent with explanation |
| **Emotional / Psychological abuse** | Verbal or non-verbal acts causing psychological distress | Fearfulness, withdrawal, depression, anxiety, low self-esteem, caregiver speaks for or over patient |
| **Sexual abuse** | Non-consensual sexual contact of any kind | Unexplained genital/rectal injuries, STIs, patient distress with personal care |
| **Financial abuse** | Illegal or improper use of an elder's funds or property | Unpaid bills despite adequate income, sudden account changes, caregiver controlling finances |
| **Neglect** | Failure of caregiver to provide basic needs | Poor hygiene, malnutrition, dehydration, pressure injuries, untreated medical conditions, unkempt appearance |
| **Self-neglect** | Failure of a person to meet their own basic needs | Hoarding, poor hygiene, not filling prescriptions, social isolation |
| **Abandonment** | Desertion by a caregiver | Patient left alone without care, caregiver fails to return |

**Gwen's case:** The nurse should assess for physical abuse (unexplained bruising) AND neglect (unresolved UTI, signs of poor hygiene, possible malnutrition) given her dependent status with Parkinson's disease.

### Q2. What are the nurse's mandatory reporting obligations for elder abuse?

**Mandatory reporting:**
- In most U.S. states, nurses are designated **mandatory reporters** of suspected elder abuse, neglect, and exploitation
- The nurse's obligation is to **report reasonable suspicion** — the nurse does NOT need to prove abuse occurred, conduct an investigation, or wait for a confession
- Failure to report is a legal violation in most states and may result in professional discipline or criminal charges

**Who to report to:**
- **Adult Protective Services (APS)** — primary agency for community-based elder abuse reporting
- **Long-term care ombudsman** — for residents of nursing homes or assisted living facilities
- **Law enforcement** — for suspected criminal acts (assault, theft)
- **Supervisor / charge nurse / provider** — immediately within the facility

**Documentation principles:**
- Document objective observations using the patient's **exact words** in quotation marks
- Describe injuries specifically: size, color, shape, location (use body diagrams when available)
- Document the caregiver's explanation and any inconsistencies noted
- Do NOT document your conclusion (e.g., "patient is being abused") — document findings and report
- Photography of injuries may be done per facility policy

**Important:** Confidentiality is NOT a barrier to reporting suspected abuse — mandated reporting supersedes confidentiality obligations.

### Q3. How should the nurse sensitively assess for abuse?

**Creating the right environment:**
- Conduct the assessment **in private** — ask the caregiver to leave the room (use a legitimate reason: "I need to take some vitals and perform a physical assessment — hospital policy requires this be done privately")
- Ensure the patient feels physically safe before asking sensitive questions
- Maintain a **calm, non-judgmental, unhurried** demeanor
- Sit at eye level; use gentle, open body language

**How to ask:**
- Begin with open, general questions: "Can you tell me a little about your daily life at home?"
- Transition gently: "I ask all of my patients these questions as a routine part of our assessment."
- Direct questions (use simple, clear language):
  - "Do you feel safe at home?"
  - "Has anyone ever hurt you or threatened you?"
  - "Has anyone made you feel afraid?"
  - "Has anyone taken money from you or your accounts without your permission?"
  - "Is there anyone who refuses to help you when you need something?"

**Why patients may not disclose:**
- Fear of retaliation from abuser
- Emotional bond with the abuser (often a family member)
- Fear of being placed in a nursing home if abuse is reported
- Shame, embarrassment, or self-blame
- Cognitive impairment limiting ability to report
- Belief that it is a private family matter

**Nurse's response if patient discloses:**
- "I believe you. What happened to you is not okay, and it is not your fault."
- Validate feelings; do not minimize or rush to solutions
- Explain what will happen next — involve social work

### Q4. What are the nursing implications for a patient with Parkinson's disease?

**Parkinson's disease (PD)** is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by dopamine depletion in the substantia nigra, resulting in motor dysfunction.

**Cardinal motor signs (TRAP):**
- **T**remor — resting tremor (pill-rolling), often one-sided initially
- **R**igidity — cogwheel or lead-pipe muscle rigidity
- **A**kinesia/Bradykinesia — slow or absent movement initiation
- **P**ostural instability — shuffling gait, stooped posture, difficulty with balance

**Key Nursing Priorities:**

| Priority | Rationale and Intervention |
|---|---|
| **Fall prevention** | Shuffling gait, postural instability, freezing episodes; bed alarms, non-skid footwear, assistive device, clear environment |
| **Medication timing (Sinemet/carbidopa-levodopa)** | Must be given at EXACT scheduled times — delays cause "wearing off" and worsening motor symptoms; never hold without provider order |
| **Aspiration precautions** | Dysphagia and impaired swallowing coordination are common; upright position for meals, speech therapy consult, thickened liquids if ordered |
| **Constipation** | Autonomic dysfunction + reduced mobility; high fluid intake, stool softeners, monitoring bowel function |
| **Communication** | Hypophonia (soft voice), dysarthria — allow extra time, do not speak for patient |
| **Pressure injury risk** | Reduced mobility; turn/reposition frequently; skin assessment |
| **Orthostatic hypotension** | Autonomic dysfunction and medication effects; sit before standing, monitor BP in two positions |

**Sinemet (carbidopa-levodopa) nursing notes:**
- Carbidopa prevents peripheral conversion of levodopa; reduces nausea and allows lower levodopa doses
- Give on time, every time — schedule adherence is critical
- Administer **30–60 minutes before meals** for better absorption (protein can interfere with levodopa absorption)
- Monitor: dyskinesias (involuntary movements — sign of excess), "on/off" fluctuations

### Q5. How do you assess and manage a urinary tract infection in an elderly female with Parkinson's disease?

**UTI Assessment in the Elderly:**
- Classic symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency) may be absent or minimal in older adults
- Atypical presentation: sudden change in mental status (confusion, agitation), falls, functional decline, or worsening of Parkinson's symptoms
- **Obtain:** Urinalysis (UA) with culture and sensitivity (C&S) before starting or changing antibiotics
- Assess for signs of upper UTI / pyelonephritis: flank pain, fever, nausea/vomiting, costovertebral angle tenderness
- **Unresolved UTI** (Gwen's situation): consider resistant organism, non-compliance with treatment, or reinfection — culture results are essential to guide antibiotic selection

**Why Gwen is at increased UTI risk:**
- Female anatomy (short urethra)
- Parkinson's disease — neurogenic bladder, urinary retention or incomplete emptying
- Decreased mobility and functional independence with hygiene
- Possible neglect (poor hygiene contributing to recurrent infections)

**Nursing Management:**
- IV antibiotics per provider order and culture sensitivity results
- Encourage fluid intake (oral or IV) to flush urinary tract
- Strict I&O; monitor urine characteristics (color, clarity, odor)
- Foley catheter — use only if medically necessary (increases UTI risk if used unnecessarily)
- Perineal hygiene education — front to back; avoid harsh soaps
- Monitor for systemic infection / urosepsis: fever, hypotension, tachycardia, altered mentation → notify provider immediately

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry
1. Perform hand hygiene
2. Introduce yourself — address her as "Ms. Harrison" or ask her preference
3. Verify identity (two identifiers)
4. Observe environment and patient appearance IMMEDIATELY upon entry — note hygiene, affect, any visible injuries, how caregiver interacts with patient
5. Note if caregiver appears to answer for the patient, interrupt, or display controlling behavior

### Conducting the Abuse Assessment (Private Setting)
- Ask caregiver to step out: "It's hospital policy to do part of the admission assessment privately — it usually only takes a few minutes."
- Acknowledge the patient's situation: "Ms. Harrison, I want you to know that everything you share with me is important, and my job is to make sure you get the best care possible."
- Proceed through sensitive questions calmly and without leading the patient
- If patient appears fearful or reluctant: "You are safe here. You are not in trouble."

### Physical Assessment — What to Look For
- **Skin:** Unusual bruising (multiple colors = different ages), burns, lacerations in areas not consistent with falls; assess entire body including under clothing
- **Hygiene:** Unwashed hair, soiled clothing, body odor, evidence of prolonged moisture exposure (incontinence-associated dermatitis)
- **Nutritional status:** Weight, visible muscle wasting, dry mucous membranes
- **Affect:** Fearful, flat, tearful, flinching with physical contact

### Parkinson's Disease Medication Check
- Verify Sinemet is scheduled at correct times and has not been missed — ask patient when she last received her medication
- If doses have been missed: notify provider; do not double dose without order

### Q2. What is the nurse's role in the interdisciplinary team response to suspected elder abuse?

The nurse is a **key member** of the interdisciplinary team but does not act alone:

- **Nurse:** Identifies and documents signs; conducts sensitive assessment; makes mandatory report to APS; ensures immediate patient safety; notifies charge nurse and provider; maintains therapeutic relationship with patient
- **Social Worker:** Conducts formal safety assessment; coordinates APS referral; identifies community resources; assists with discharge planning (including alternative placement if home is unsafe)
- **Provider:** Evaluates injuries; orders diagnostic tests (X-rays for fractures if warranted); documents medical findings; may file a report
- **Case Manager:** Coordinates care transitions; connects to home health, respite care, or long-term care placement
- **Patient Advocate:** Ensures patient's rights and preferences are respected throughout the process
- **APS (Adult Protective Services):** Investigates reports; determines safety; coordinates protective services

**Key principle:** The patient's safety is the priority. If the patient is in immediate danger, the team may act to ensure safety even if the patient does not request intervention.

---

### Q3. How can the nurse support a patient who is reluctant to disclose or who recants?

Reluctance to disclose or recanting is **common** and does not mean abuse did not occur. The nurse should:

- **Continue to support the patient** — do not withdraw care or become confrontational
- Acknowledge the difficulty: "I understand it can be hard to talk about this, and I respect whatever you decide to share."
- Remind the patient of her rights: "You have the right to safe care and to make decisions about your life."
- **Do not pressure or interrogate** — coercive questioning may increase trauma and fear
- Understand that the patient may be **economically dependent** on the abuser or have no alternative housing — safety planning must address practical barriers
- The nurse still has an **obligation to report** based on observed objective signs — a patient's request not to report does not eliminate the nurse's mandatory reporting duty when abuse indicators are objectively present
- Ensure social work is involved to offer options and resources without judgment
