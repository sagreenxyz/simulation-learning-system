## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What communication strategies promote effective interaction with a patient who has a tracheostomy?

Because Nancy cannot speak (total laryngectomy + permanent trach), the nurse must use alternative communication methods:

**Low-tech options:**
- **Pen and paper / whiteboard and dry-erase marker** — most practical for writing short responses
- **Communication boards** — with letters, words, pictures, and common phrases
- **Pointing and gesturing** — effective for yes/no questions and simple choices
- **Letter boards** — patient spells out words by pointing

**Higher-tech options:**
- **Text-to-speech apps** on tablet or phone
- **Speech-generating devices (SGDs)** — prescribed by speech-language pathologist
- **Passy-Muir valve (PMV)** — a one-way speaking valve that attaches to the tracheostomy tube and directs airflow through the vocal cords, allowing speech; appropriate for patients with a larynx (not for Nancy, who has had a laryngectomy)

**Communication principles:**
- **Allow adequate time** for the patient to respond — never rush
- Ask **yes/no questions** when possible
- **Watch body language and nonverbal cues** — facial expression, eye contact, gestures
- Reduce environmental noise
- Establish a consistent communication system at the beginning of care
- Include family in the communication plan
- Respect the patient's pace — frustration is common; be patient

### Q2. What is MRSA? What precautions are required, and what patient/family education is needed?

**A. What is MRSA?**
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) is a strain of Staphylococcus aureus bacteria that has developed resistance to methicillin and other common beta-lactam antibiotics, making infections difficult to treat. MRSA is a common healthcare-associated infection (HAI) and can colonize the skin, nose, and respiratory tract without causing disease (colonization), or can cause active infection.

**B. Contact Precautions Required:**
1. **Gown and gloves** must be worn upon entering the room — put on BEFORE entering
2. **Hand hygiene** — mandatory before and after contact with the patient, after removing PPE
3. **Dedicated equipment** — use single-patient-use items (stethoscope, BP cuff) or clean/disinfect between patients
4. **Private room** or cohort with another MRSA patient
5. **Limit visitors** — teach visitors hand hygiene and contact precautions
6. **Waste disposal** — all materials used in the room are disposed of in the room
7. **Signage** — contact precautions sign posted at the door

**Donning (putting on) PPE sequence:**
1. Hand hygiene
2. Gown
3. Mask (if respiratory precautions also apply)
4. Goggles/face shield (if splashing possible)
5. Gloves (over gown cuffs)

**Doffing (removing) PPE sequence (most contaminated items first):**
1. Gloves (inside out)
2. Goggles
3. Gown (pull away from body, rolling inward)
4. Mask
5. Hand hygiene after each step

**C. Patient and Family Education:**
- What MRSA is and how it spreads (contact, not airborne)
- Importance of hand hygiene — before and after touching patient, after using bathroom
- Do not share personal items (towels, razors)
- Proper wound care if applicable
- Visitors should not come if they are immunocompromised
- Treatment with antibiotics (vancomycin or linezolid are first-line for MRSA)
- When to be retested/when precautions can be lifted (2 negative cultures, 24+ hours apart)

### Q3. What are the steps for performing sterile tracheostomy care?

**Equipment needed:**
- Tracheostomy care kit (sterile)
- Sterile normal saline
- Sterile gauze
- Sterile tracheostomy ties
- Sterile gloves
- PPE for MRSA (gown, gloves)
- Suction equipment

**Procedure — Tracheostomy Care:**
1. Explain procedure to patient (and write it out if needed for communication)
2. Position patient in semi-Fowler's (30–45 degrees)
3. Don PPE (contact precautions for Nancy — gown + gloves)
4. Pre-oxygenate the patient before suctioning
5. Set up sterile field
6. **Suction the tracheostomy first** — before cleaning, to remove secretions
   - Insert catheter to just beyond end of tube (do NOT apply suction on insertion)
   - Apply suction during withdrawal while rotating catheter
   - Suction no longer than **10–15 seconds per pass**
   - Allow patient to rest between passes (SpO2 must return to baseline)
   - Limit to 2–3 passes per suctioning session
7. **Clean the inner cannula:**
   - Remove inner cannula
   - Soak in sterile normal saline and clean with sterile brush
   - Rinse and replace
8. **Clean stoma site:**
   - Use sterile gauze dampened with normal saline
   - Clean around stoma in circular motion outward from stoma
   - Dry thoroughly
9. **Change tracheostomy ties:**
   - Have second person hold tracheostomy tube in place
   - Remove old ties
   - Apply new ties — ties should allow 1–2 finger widths between tie and neck (not too tight/too loose)
10. **Apply tracheostomy dressing:**
    - Place sterile split gauze under flanges
    - Do NOT cut gauze (frayed fibers can enter trachea)
11. Document procedure and patient response

### Q4. What are the nutritional challenges for patients with a tracheostomy?

- **Dysphagia** — difficulty swallowing; tracheostomy tubes can compress the esophagus, altering swallowing
- **Risk of aspiration** — secretions or food may enter the airway, especially with an inflated tracheostomy cuff
- **Anorexia** — decreased appetite from illness, fatigue, anxiety
- **Caloric needs** — increased with infection/fever/healing; may exceed oral intake
- **Altered taste and smell** — laryngectomy and tracheostomy alter normal airflow through the nose and mouth

**Nursing interventions:**
- Consult **speech-language pathology (SLP)** for swallowing evaluation
- Consult **dietitian** for nutritional assessment and caloric goals
- Elevate HOB during and after oral intake to reduce aspiration risk
- Offer soft, moist foods if oral intake is possible
- Consider enteral feeding (NG or G-tube) if oral intake is inadequate
- Monitor weight, albumin, and prealbumin as nutritional markers

---

### Q5. What are the complications of long-term tracheostomy use?

- **Tracheal stenosis** — narrowing of the trachea from scar tissue
- **Tracheomalacia** — weakening of tracheal wall cartilage
- **Infection** — stoma site or lower respiratory tract
- **Granuloma formation** — overgrowth of tissue around stoma
- **Tracheoesophageal fistula** — abnormal connection between trachea and esophagus
- **Tube obstruction** — from thick or dried secretions
- **Accidental decannulation** — tube falls out; requires immediate replacement
- **Skin breakdown** around stoma from moisture, friction, or infection
- **Aspiration** — if cuff not properly inflated during feedings

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Contact Precautions FIRST
Before entering the room:
1. Don **gown and gloves** (contact precautions)
2. Check the isolation sign on the door
3. Prepare all needed supplies before entering (minimize trips in/out)

### Communication Setup with Nancy
- Introduce yourself at eye level
- Establish a communication system immediately: "Mrs. Gilbert, I know you communicate by writing. I have a whiteboard here for you. Nod once for yes, shake your head for no — is that okay?"
- Keep communication board visible at all times

### Assessment Priorities
- **SpO2** — continuous oxygen; what is her current saturation?
- **Respiratory assessment** — rate, work of breathing, auscultation (pneumonia: crackles, diminished sounds)
- **Tracheostomy assessment** — audible secretions? inner cannula clean? stoma site intact?
- **Temperature** — patient denied fever on admission but may have developed one
- **Nutrition/hydration** — decreased appetite; assess oral intake

### Tracheostomy Suctioning — Key Points (During Simulation)
- **Hyperoxygenate** before and after suctioning (give 100% O2 for 30–60 sec via manual resuscitation bag or ventilator)
- Insert catheter without suction; apply suction only while withdrawing
- Maximum **15 seconds** per pass — prevents hypoxia
- Allow SpO2 to return to baseline between passes
- Document: frequency, secretion characteristics (color, consistency, amount), patient response

### What to Assess About the Secretions
- **Color:** clear/white = normal; yellow/green = infection; pink/bloody = mucosal trauma or hemorrhage
- **Consistency:** thin/watery vs. thick/tenacious (thick secretions indicate need for more hydration and/or mucolytics)
- **Amount:** note whether suctioning is needed frequently (indicates active infection or inadequate airway clearance)
- **Odor:** foul odor suggests infection

## 📋 POST-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What is the appropriate suction catheter size for tracheostomy suctioning?

The suction catheter diameter should be **no more than half the inner diameter of the tracheostomy tube**. Using a catheter that is too large can cause excessive negative pressure and mucosal trauma. A common guide: multiply the tracheostomy tube inner diameter in mm by 2, then select the French size catheter equal to or less than that number.

---

### Q2. How should tracheostomy ties be assessed?

Tracheostomy ties should be:
- **Secure but not too tight** — allow **1–2 finger widths** between the tie and neck
- Changed when wet, soiled, or frayed
- Replaced by **two people** — one to hold the trach tube in place while the other changes the ties, to prevent accidental decannulation

### Q3. What nursing diagnoses apply to Nancy Gilbert?

1. **Ineffective Airway Clearance** r/t tracheostomy secretions and pneumonia — AEB wet cough, audible secretions, dyspnea
2. **Impaired Verbal Communication** r/t laryngectomy and permanent tracheostomy — AEB nonverbal; communicates by writing
3. **Risk for Infection** r/t MRSA colonization and open tracheostomy stoma
4. **Imbalanced Nutrition: Less Than Body Requirements** r/t decreased appetite, dysphagia risk, and increased metabolic demand from infection

---

### Q4. What are Passy-Muir Valve (PMV) considerations?

A PMV is a one-way valve that sits on the tracheostomy tube. On inhalation, the valve opens. On exhalation, it closes, redirecting airflow upward through the larynx and vocal cords, allowing speech.

**Important for Nancy:** Because she had a **total laryngectomy**, she no longer has vocal cords. A PMV would NOT restore speech for Nancy. However, for patients with a larynx who have a tracheostomy (e.g., post-ventilator or post-surgery with larynx intact), a PMV can dramatically improve quality of life.

---

### Q5. What are the goals of care for Nancy Gilbert?

1. Maintain a patent airway — clear secretions via tracheostomy suctioning as needed
2. Manage pneumonia — antibiotics (treat MRSA), hydration, oxygen therapy, respiratory assessments
3. Prevent MRSA transmission — strict contact precautions
4. Support communication — implement and maintain effective alternative communication strategies
5. Prevent aspiration — assess swallowing, position, and involve SLP
6. Educate husband about home smoking cessation — ongoing exposure to secondhand smoke is a major exacerbating factor for respiratory health
