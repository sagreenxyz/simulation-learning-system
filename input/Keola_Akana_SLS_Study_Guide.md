## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **three interlocking themes**:

1. **Digoxin toxicity recognition** — digoxin has a narrow therapeutic window (0.5–2.0 ng/mL); toxicity causes life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias
2. **Hypokalemia** — furosemide causes potassium loss; low potassium dramatically increases digoxin toxicity risk; these two conditions are dangerous together
3. **Heart failure management** — the student must understand the complex medication regimen and know what to hold, what to give, and when to escalate

The critical connection:

```
Heart Failure → Furosemide (to remove fluid) → Hypokalemia (potassium lost)
        ↓
Low potassium → Digoxin toxicity threshold LOWERED
        ↓
Even therapeutic digoxin levels become TOXIC when K+ is low
        ↓
Signs: N/V, confusion, visual changes (halos), bradycardia, dysrhythmias
        ↓
Nurse must: HOLD digoxin → Check K+ → Replace K+ → Notify provider
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. What are the nutritional implications, key assessment findings, and nursing interventions for a patient with hypokalemia?

**Normal potassium:** 3.5–5.0 mEq/L
**Hypokalemia:** K+ < 3.5 mEq/L

**Nutritional Implications:**
- Patient needs a **high-potassium diet**
- Foods rich in potassium: bananas, oranges, kiwi, avocados, spinach, tomatoes, baked potatoes, sweet potatoes, yogurt, beans, peas, peanut butter, bran, raisins, figs
- **Low-sodium diet** also required in heart failure — some potassium-rich foods are high in sodium; dietitian consultation recommended
- Avoid excessive caffeine and alcohol — both increase potassium excretion

**Key Assessment Findings:**
- **Cardiovascular:** Decreased blood pressure, thready/weak pulse, orthostatic hypotension, dysrhythmias, **flattened T wave on ECG** (hallmark), U waves on ECG, prolonged QT interval
- **Neuromuscular:** Muscle weakness, fatigue, leg cramps, paresthesias (tingling/numbness), areflexia
- **GI:** Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, hypoactive bowel sounds, abdominal distention, constipation, paralytic ileus (severe)
- **Respiratory:** Shallow, ineffective respirations (respiratory muscle weakness — severe hypokalemia)
- **Renal:** Polyuria, decreased urine concentration ability
- **Neurological:** Lethargy, irritability, confusion

**Nursing Interventions:**
1. Administer prescribed potassium replacement (IV KCl — NEVER IV push; must be diluted and infused slowly; can cause cardiac arrest if given rapidly)
2. Monitor cardiac rhythm (telemetry) — hypokalemia causes dysrhythmias
3. Monitor serum potassium levels
4. Encourage high-potassium foods
5. Monitor and maintain adequate urine output (IV KCl can accumulate if kidneys not working)
6. Assess for digitalis toxicity — hypokalemia POTENTIATES digoxin toxicity
7. Monitor respiratory rate and depth — severe hypokalemia can cause respiratory failure
8. Fall precautions — muscle weakness increases fall risk

### Q2. What are the signs and symptoms of digoxin toxicity and how would the nurse assess for these symptoms?

**Therapeutic digoxin level:** 0.5–2.0 ng/mL
**Toxicity:** > 2.0 ng/mL (can occur even at "therapeutic" levels with low potassium)

**Signs and Symptoms by Body System:**

**GI (often FIRST signs):**
- Anorexia (loss of appetite)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhea
- Abdominal pain

**Neurological:**
- Fatigue, weakness
- Headache
- Confusion, disorientation
- Depression

**Visual (classic — often tested):**
- **Yellow-green tint** to vision (xanthopsia) — classic digoxin toxicity sign
- **Seeing halos** around lights
- Blurred vision
- Blind spots (scotoma)

**Cardiovascular (most dangerous):**
- **Bradycardia** — heart rate < 60 bpm → HOLD digoxin
- Heart block (1st, 2nd, 3rd degree)
- AV nodal block
- Ventricular dysrhythmias (PVCs, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation)
- Irregular pulse

**Nursing Assessment:**
- Check **apical pulse for a full minute** before each dose — HOLD if HR < 60 bpm
- Monitor digoxin serum levels (draw trough: 6–8 hours after last dose)
- Monitor potassium levels — hypokalemia worsens toxicity
- Monitor cardiac rhythm on telemetry
- Assess for GI symptoms (anorexia, N/V) — often the first warning
- Ask about visual changes (halos, color changes)
- Assess level of consciousness
- **Antidote:** Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind) — for severe toxicity

### Q3. How would the nurse provide family-centered care?

- **Involve family** in all teaching — especially about recognizing signs of digoxin toxicity and hypokalemia
- Inform family about the **medication schedule** — who can help ensure the patient takes medications as prescribed at home
- Share the **plan of care** transparently — inform family of current concerns, goals, and expected course
- Arrange family support for **cardiac rehabilitation** — behavioral changes are more successful with family support
- Teach family members when to **call the provider or 911** — if patient's heart rate < 60, patient is confused, or patient sees halos/colored vision
- Encourage family to assist with **dietary changes** — high-potassium, low-sodium foods; limit alcohol
- Incorporate family into **discharge planning** — medication organizers, follow-up appointments
- **Advocate** for the patient and family — ensure they are fully informed about the patient's condition and prognosis

### Q4. Describe the pathophysiology of heart failure.

**Definition:** Heart failure (HF) is a complex clinical syndrome in which the heart cannot pump sufficient blood to meet the body's metabolic demands, OR can only do so at elevated filling pressures.

**Left-Sided Heart Failure (most common):**
- Left ventricle cannot effectively pump blood forward into the aorta
- Blood backs up into the pulmonary circulation → **pulmonary edema**
- Signs: dyspnea, orthopnea (difficulty breathing lying flat), paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, crackles in lungs, S3 heart sound, elevated BNP
- Causes: MI, cardiomyopathy, hypertension, valvular disease

**Right-Sided Heart Failure:**
- Right ventricle cannot pump blood into the pulmonary circulation
- Blood backs up into the systemic venous circulation
- Signs: peripheral edema (ankles, legs), JVD (jugular venous distension), hepatomegaly, ascites, weight gain
- Often caused by left-sided failure (most common cause) or pulmonary hypertension

**Compensatory Mechanisms (become harmful over time):**
- **Sympathetic nervous system activation** — tachycardia, vasoconstriction → increases cardiac work
- **RAAS activation** — fluid retention → increases preload and cardiac work
- **Ventricular remodeling** — hypertrophy and dilation of ventricle

### Q5. Compare and contrast key information for HF medications.

| Medication | Action | Key Nursing Consideration |
|---|---|---|
| **Digoxin** | Increases contractility (positive inotrope); slows AV node | Check apical HR (hold if <60); monitor K+ (low K+ → toxicity); therapeutic level 0.5–2.0 ng/mL |
| **Furosemide (Lasix)** | Loop diuretic — reduces fluid overload | Monitor K+ (causes hypokalemia); take in AM; monitor I&O; weight daily |
| **Spironolactone** | Potassium-sparing diuretic; aldosterone antagonist | Avoid high-K+ foods; monitor for hyperkalemia; avoid with ACE inhibitors (risk of hyperkalemia) |
| **Potassium Chloride** | Replaces potassium lost from diuretics | NEVER IV push; dilute IV KCl; give with food PO; monitor renal function |
| **Atenolol** | Beta-1 blocker; reduces HR and BP | Hold if HR < 60 or BP < 90 systolic; do not abruptly stop; may mask hypoglycemia |

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — SWIPE Safety Check
- **S** — Siderails appropriate
- **W** — Wristband: two-identifier verification
- **I** — IV access present? IV fluids running?
- **P** — Pull cord/call light within reach
- **E** — Equipment: cardiac monitor in place? Telemetry leads attached?

### Before Administering Digoxin — CRITICAL STEPS
1. Check serum **digoxin level** (therapeutic: 0.5–2.0 ng/mL)
2. Check serum **potassium** (must be normal before giving digoxin — low K+ → toxicity)
3. Assess **apical pulse for 1 full minute**
4. **HOLD digoxin** if:
   - HR < 60 bpm
   - HR > 100 bpm (may indicate toxicity)
   - Irregular rhythm (new)
   - Potassium < 3.5 mEq/L
   - Digoxin level > 2.0 ng/mL
   - Patient reports N/V, visual changes, anorexia
5. Notify provider if held

### Cardiac Assessment for HF Patient
- **Heart sounds:** Listen for S3 (extra sound — indicates fluid overload), S4, murmurs
- **Respiratory:** Crackles/rales (pulmonary edema), SpO2, respiratory rate
- **Peripheral:** Bilateral ankle/leg edema — grade 1+–4+; assess JVD
- **Weight:** Compare to previous day (1 kg = 1 liter of fluid)
- **I&O:** Urine output ≥30 mL/hr indicates adequate renal perfusion
- **Telemetry:** Monitor for dysrhythmias (PVCs, bradycardia, heart block)

### Q2. Describe assessment cues and anticipated nursing actions for a client experiencing digoxin toxicity.

**Assessment cues:**
- GI: Anorexia, N/V, diarrhea
- Neuro: Confusion, fatigue
- Visual: Seeing halos, yellow-green tint to vision
- Cardiac: Bradycardia (HR < 60), new irregular rhythm, heart block on ECG

**Nursing actions:**
1. HOLD the digoxin
2. Assess apical pulse for 1 full minute
3. Check serum digoxin level and serum potassium
4. Notify provider with SBAR
5. Place on cardiac monitor
6. Administer antidote if ordered: Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind)
7. Monitor continuously until toxicity resolves
8. Document all findings and interventions

---

### Q3. What is the Braden Scale and how does it apply?
Braden Scale assesses **pressure ulcer risk** — six subscales scored 1–4. Score ≤18 indicates at-risk. For Keola, advanced age, cardiac disease, and potential edema all impact skin integrity.

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Digoxin (Lanoxin)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Cardiac glycoside |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits Na-K-ATPase pump → increases intracellular calcium → increased contractility; vagal stimulation → slows SA and AV nodes |
| **Indication** | Heart failure (increases contractility); atrial fibrillation (rate control) |
| **Therapeutic level** | 0.5–2.0 ng/mL |
| **Adverse effects** | Bradycardia, heart block, ventricular dysrhythmias, N/V/anorexia, visual changes (halos, yellow-green), confusion |
| **Antidote** | Digoxin immune Fab (Digibind/DigiFab) |
| **Nursing precautions** | Apical pulse x1 min (hold <60); check K+ (low K+ → toxicity); monitor digoxin level; assess for toxicity signs before each dose |
| **Patient teaching** | Take pulse daily; report HR < 60, N/V, vision changes; do not skip doses; avoid antacids within 2 hours |

---

### Furosemide (Lasix)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Loop diuretic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits Na-K-2Cl transporter in loop of Henle → promotes sodium, chloride, and water excretion |
| **Indication** | Fluid overload in heart failure; hypertension; edema |
| **Adverse effects** | Hypokalemia ⚠️, hyponatremia, hypovolemia, ototoxicity (high doses), photosensitivity |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor electrolytes (especially K+); weigh daily; I&O; assess edema; give in morning (diuresis occurs within 1 hour) |
| **Patient teaching** | Take in morning to avoid nighttime urination; weigh daily; report muscle cramps, dizziness; eat high-K+ foods |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Decreased Cardiac Output** r/t altered contractility and dysrhythmia potential secondary to heart failure and digoxin toxicity risk
   - AEB: HR irregularities, edema, fatigue, history of heart failure

2. **Excess Fluid Volume** r/t impaired cardiac pumping and sodium/water retention
   - AEB: bilateral edema, weight gain, crackles, elevated BNP

3. **Activity Intolerance** r/t decreased cardiac output and fatigue
   - AEB: dyspnea on exertion, fatigue, inability to perform ADLs

4. **Deficient Knowledge** r/t complex medication regimen and signs of toxicity/electrolyte imbalance
   - AEB: new admission for medication adjustment; need for cardiac rehabilitation teaching
