## 🔑 The Big Picture — What This Case Is Really About

This scenario has **four interlocking themes**:

1. **Diabetic wound complications** — diabetes impairs wound healing, immune response, and sensation; foot/pressure ulcers can become deep infections requiring surgical debridement
2. **Postoperative wound assessment** — the student must systematically assess the debrided wound and understand the stages and types of debridement
3. **Heart failure + diabetes interaction** — both diseases affect fluid balance, medication management, and wound healing; they interact in complex ways
4. **Hypoglycemia risk during hospitalization** — insulin given for stress hyperglycemia can cause dangerous hypoglycemia, especially in NPO or poorly eating patients

The interconnected pathologies:

```
Type 2 Diabetes → Peripheral neuropathy (cannot feel wound developing)
                → Peripheral vascular disease (poor blood flow to heal wound)
                → Hyperglycemia (impairs immune function and healing)
        ↓
Wound develops on foot → Becomes infected → Stage II–III pressure/diabetic ulcer
        ↓
Wound debridement required (surgical/sharp) to remove necrotic tissue
        ↓
Postoperative: Assess wound + Pain management + Glucose management
        ↓
Heart Failure complicates: Fluid overload risk, medication interactions
        ↓
Insulin given for hyperglycemia → Risk of HYPOGLYCEMIA
```

## 📝 PRE-SIMULATION QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

### Q1. Describe the staging of pressure ulcers and what the wound looks like at each stage.

| Stage | Description | Appearance |
|---|---|---|
| **Deep Tissue Injury (DTI)** | Intact skin with persistent non-blanchable deep red, maroon, or purple discoloration OR blood-filled blister | Purple/maroon discoloration; may feel boggy or firm; may be painful |
| **Stage 1** | Non-blanchable erythema of intact skin | Red, intact skin; does NOT turn white when pressed; may be warm or cool |
| **Stage 2** | Partial-thickness skin loss involving epidermis and/or dermis; shallow open ulcer | Pink/red wound bed; ruptured serum-filled blisters; no slough |
| **Stage 3** | Full-thickness skin loss; fat visible; slough and/or eschar may be present | Crater-like wound; fat visible; slough/eschar may obscure depth; no fascia/muscle exposed |
| **Stage 4** | Full-thickness loss with exposed muscle, bone, tendon, or ligament | Fascia, muscle, tendon, or bone visible; slough/eschar often present; tunneling common |
| **Unstageable** | Full-thickness loss obscured by slough or eschar; depth unknown | Wound base covered by slough (yellow) or eschar (brown/black); cannot determine stage until debrided |

### Q2. Why do pressure ulcers form? Where are they most commonly found?

**Cause:** Sustained pressure reduces blood flow to the skin and underlying tissues. When blood supply is inadequate, cells become hypoxic and eventually die (necrosis).

**Contributing factors:**
- **Friction** — skin rubbing against surface during repositioning
- **Shear** — deeper tissues shift while skin remains stationary (sliding down in bed)
- **Moisture** — urine, sweat, or wound drainage macerate skin
- **Malnutrition** — inadequate protein and calories impair tissue viability and healing
- **Immobility** — inability to self-reposition
- **Diabetes** — impairs circulation, sensation, and immune response (as in Mathias)
- **Heart failure** — peripheral edema and poor perfusion

**Most common locations (bony prominences — most pressure):**
- **Sacrum/coccyx** (most common in bed-bound patients)
- **Heels**
- **Lateral malleoli (ankles)**
- **Greater trochanter (hips)**
- **Ischial tuberosities** (sitting)
- **Elbows**
- **Back of head (occiput)**
- **Ears** (if on nasal cannula or O2 mask)

### Q3. Describe the four different types of debridement.

**1. Surgical/Sharp Debridement:**
- Hyperkeratotic, infected, and nonviable tissue is removed using a scalpel, scissors, or curette
- Most rapid method
- Used for large amounts of necrotic tissue; infected wounds
- Requires sterile field and appropriate anesthesia; typically performed in OR or wound care clinic
- **Mathias's scenario** — he underwent surgical wound debridement

**2. Mechanical Debridement:**
- Uses physical force to remove necrotic tissue
- Methods: wet-to-dry dressings (most traditional), wound irrigation, whirlpool therapy, ultrasound
- Non-selective — can remove healthy tissue along with necrotic tissue
- Wet-to-dry dressings: moist gauze placed in wound → dries → removed with necrotic tissue adherent

**3. Autolytic Debridement:**
- Uses the body's own enzymes and moisture to liquefy and dissolve necrotic tissue
- Selective — only necrotic tissue is liquefied; healthy tissue is spared
- Achieved with moisture-retentive dressings: hydrocolloids, hydrogels, transparent films
- Slow (days to weeks); NOT appropriate for infected wounds
- Most comfortable for patient

**4. Enzymatic Debridement:**
- Chemical enzymes (collagenase/Santyl — most common) are applied topically to dissolve necrotic tissue
- Selective debridement
- Applied once daily; requires damp wound environment
- Useful when surgical debridement is not possible

### Q4. What are the possible nursing interventions for a patient with a pressure ulcer?

**Pressure Reduction/Repositioning:**
- Turn and reposition every **2 hours** (or more frequently) — MOST IMPORTANT intervention
- Use a **30-degree lateral tilt** rather than full side-lying position (reduces trochanter pressure)
- Elevate head of bed **no more than 30 degrees** (reduces shear over sacrum)
- Use pressure-redistributing mattress (foam, air, gel)
- Protect heels — elevate off mattress with pillows; use heel protectors

**Wound Care:**
- Cleanse wound with normal saline or prescribed wound cleanser at each dressing change
- Apply appropriate dressing based on wound condition (see dressing types below)
- Document: location, stage, size (L × W × D in cm), wound bed color, type/amount of drainage, periwound skin condition
- Culture if signs of infection present
- Consult wound care/ostomy/continence (WOC) nurse

**Dressing Selection by Wound Type:**
| Wound Condition | Appropriate Dressing |
|---|---|
| Dry necrotic wound | Hydrogel or hydrocolloid (promote moisture) |
| Wet/draining wound | Foam dressing; alginate |
| Infected wound | Silver-containing dressing; antimicrobial |
| Clean granulating wound | Non-adherent dressing; foam |

**Nutrition:**
- High-protein diet (1.25–1.5 g/kg/day) for wound healing
- Vitamins C and A, zinc supplementation
- Adequate hydration
- Dietitian consultation

**Pain Management:**
- Pre-medicate before wound care changes
- Assess pain before and after wound care
- Document response to analgesics

### Q5. What is the role of RN, LPN, and CNA in wound care?

**RN (Registered Nurse):**
- Stage and measure wound
- Assess wound for infection, healing progression, complications
- Order wound care supplies (within standing orders/protocol)
- Create and update the plan of care
- Coordinate with wound care team (WOCN)
- Educate patient and family

**LPN (Licensed Practical Nurse):**
- Perform wound dressing changes (per scope of practice and facility policy)
- Assess and document wound characteristics
- Report changes to RN

**CNA/Nursing Assistive Personnel (NAP):**
- Alert nurse to any skin changes or wound appearance changes
- Assist with repositioning
- Assist with hygiene
- Report patient complaints of pain near wound
- Cannot perform wound assessment or dressing changes

### Q6. What is the pathophysiology of heart failure as it applies to Mathias?

*(See Keola Akana study guide for full HF pathophysiology)*

**Specific to Mathias:**
- Heart failure creates edema in the extremities — including the feet — which impairs wound healing by reducing oxygen delivery to wound tissue
- Poor cardiac output → reduced perfusion to peripheral tissues → slowed wound healing
- Loop diuretics (furosemide) reduce edema but can cause hypokalemia — monitor potassium
- Fluid management is critical — balance between preventing fluid overload (HF) and adequate wound healing perfusion

## 🏥 DURING THE SIMULATION — What to Do & Say

### On Entry — Immediate Priorities
1. **SWIPE safety check** — ensure bed low; call light accessible; IV patent
2. **Assess level of consciousness** — diabetes + surgery + pain medications = confusion risk
3. **Check blood glucose** — HIGH PRIORITY for postoperative diabetic patient
4. **Assess postoperative wound** — surgical debridement site

### Wound Assessment (REEDA + More)
- **R** — Redness: periwound erythema? Spreading?
- **E** — Edema: swelling at wound site?
- **E** — Ecchymosis: bruising?
- **D** — Drainage: type (serous, serosanguineous, sanguineous, purulent), amount, odor
- **A** — Approximation: wound edges together or gaping?
- **Depth:** How deep is the wound? Can you see fat, fascia, muscle, bone?
- **Size:** L × W × D in cm
- **Necrotic tissue:** Any remaining slough (yellow) or eschar (black)?
- **Granulation tissue:** Pink/red bubbly tissue = healing sign
- **Tunneling/undermining:** Probe gently if ordered

### Hypoglycemia Monitoring
- Check blood glucose q4–6h per order (more frequently if insulin recently given)
- Before giving regular insulin — check glucose; verify sliding scale
- Ensure patient has or will eat adequate meal before giving bolus insulin
- Know signs: shaking, diaphoresis, confusion, tachycardia
- D50 at bedside or readily available

### Managing the HF + Diabetes Dual Diagnosis
- Weigh daily — sudden increase suggests fluid retention (HF worsening)
- Monitor I&O strictly
- Assess legs/feet for edema
- Monitor potassium (furosemide → hypokalemia)
- Monitor blood glucose q4–6h (stress hyperglycemia + insulin use)
- Ensure nutritional support promotes wound healing

### Q5. How does Type 2 diabetes affect wound healing?
- Hyperglycemia impairs neutrophil function → reduced immune response to infection
- Peripheral vascular disease (PVD) → reduced blood flow → reduced O2 and nutrients to wound
- Peripheral neuropathy → cannot feel wound forming or worsening
- Impaired collagen synthesis → slower wound closure
- Increased risk of infection (high glucose is a great bacterial growth medium)

## 💊 MEDICATION REVIEW

### Clindamycin — IV — HIGH ALERT
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Lincosamide antibiotic |
| **Mechanism** | Inhibits bacterial protein synthesis by binding to 50S ribosomal subunit |
| **Spectrum** | Gram-positive organisms, anaerobes; MRSA (in some versions replaced by vancomycin) |
| **Route** | IV direct |
| **Dosage range** | 300–600 mg q6–8h (up to 2.7 g/day) |
| **Rate** | IV: 1 mL/hr (diluted per protocol) |
| **Adverse effects** | **C. difficile colitis** (major risk — bloody diarrhea, abdominal cramping), nausea, diarrhea, arrhythmias (rapid infusion), hypotension |
| **Nursing precautions** | Monitor bowel function; report diarrhea immediately; infuse at correct rate; HIGH ALERT — requires double check |
| **Patient teaching** | Report any diarrhea (especially bloody/black); complete full course; do not take antidiarrheals without checking with provider |

---

### Regular Insulin SQ — HIGH ALERT
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Short-acting insulin |
| **Mechanism** | Binds insulin receptors → promotes cellular glucose uptake → lowers blood glucose |
| **Indication** | Stress hyperglycemia management in hospitalized T2DM patient |
| **Onset/Peak/Duration** | Onset: 30 min / Peak: 2–4 hours / Duration: 5–8 hours |
| **Nursing precautions** | VERIFY correct insulin TYPE (look-alike/sound-alike risk); double-check with second RN; ensure patient will eat after administering; monitor blood glucose before and 1–2 hours after |
| **Hypoglycemia response** | D50 IV if unable to swallow; juice/glucose tabs if alert |

---

### Furosemide (Lasix)
| Category | Details |
|---|---|
| **Classification** | Loop diuretic |
| **Indication** | Heart failure — reduces fluid overload |
| **Monitoring** | Potassium (hypokalemia risk); daily weight; I&O; edema assessment |
| **Nursing precautions** | Give in morning; monitor electrolytes; assess BP before giving |

## 🏥 PRIORITY NURSING DIAGNOSES

1. **Impaired Skin Integrity** r/t diabetic wound requiring surgical debridement
   - AEB: open wound post-debridement; T2DM impairing healing; malnutrition risk

2. **Acute Pain** r/t surgical wound debridement and wound care
   - AEB: patient report of pain; surgical wound present

3. **Risk for Unstable Blood Glucose** r/t T2DM, insulin administration, and postoperative stress
   - AEB: T2DM; receiving regular insulin sliding scale; postoperative NPO to advancing diet

4. **Excess Fluid Volume** r/t heart failure and insufficient diuresis
   - AEB: history of HF; on furosemide; monitor for edema and weight gain

5. **Risk for Infection** r/t surgical wound and T2DM-related immune compromise
   - AEB: open wound; hyperglycemia impairs immune response; receiving antibiotics
